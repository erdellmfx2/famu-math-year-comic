# Week Thirty-Four: The Week Readiness Started Looking Real

The question that ended the previous week found its answer sooner than either Malik or Nia expected.

Who was watching?

By the beginning of Week Thirty-Four, the answer was not mysterious anymore.

Professors were watching. Classmates were watching. The students who drifted into study circles unsure of themselves were watching. The department was watching in that quiet academic way that did not always announce itself out loud, but noticed patterns with remarkable accuracy. At FAMU, effort could be seen. Consistency could be seen. The way a student carried themselves through an ordinary week—especially a crowded spring week—could reveal just as much as a test score.

That realization might have made earlier versions of Malik and Nia tense for the wrong reasons. Malik might have become overly controlled, trying to perform readiness instead of simply living it. Nia might have felt the old temptation to overextend herself, mistaking visibility for responsibility. But this week began differently.

Instead of panic, there was focus.

Instead of trying to look impressive, they both leaned more deeply into the habits that had already started changing them.

Friday, March 20, arrived with the layered hum of a real FAMU spring day. Baseball was on the road at Arkansas Pine Bluff. Softball traveled to Alabama State. The campus carried that familiar blend of motion and pride—students walking briskly across the grounds with backpacks and iced drinks, conversations about assignments mixing easily with updates about weekend games, tables outside student spaces catching pockets of laughter and debate. FAMU never felt like a place where academic life and student life had to compete for oxygen. They braided together. That was part of what made the rhythm of the university feel so full.

Malik spent part of the day updating his internship and career checklist, but unlike earlier in the semester, the document no longer felt like a nervous archive of future hopes. It looked more like a map that was beginning to connect. He had notes about deadlines, skills he wanted to sharpen, faculty he hoped to speak with, and possible summer directions. What struck him most was how practical it all felt now.

The future was no longer some giant dramatic doorway.

It was being built out of repeated Fridays like this one.

Later, in class, Dr. Delgado reminded students to keep their setups clean before rushing into solutions. “A messy beginning creates fake difficulty,” she said, writing a carefully organized first line on the board. “Sometimes the problem is not as confusing as your page makes it look.”

Malik almost smiled to himself. A few months earlier he would have received that as ordinary procedural advice. Now he heard the deeper lesson inside it. A clean setup was not just visual order. It was intellectual honesty. It meant not pretending to understand the structure before you actually did.

After class he stayed back for a moment, reviewing a half-worked example.

Dr. Delgado glanced at his notebook. “Better,” she said.

He looked up. “Better than before, or better than good?”

That made her laugh softly. “Better than rushed. Which is often the more important improvement.”

That sentence stayed with him for the rest of the day.

Across campus, Nia attended a student organization planning meeting that could easily have turned into one of those overloaded sessions where everyone had enthusiasm and nobody had boundaries. But Nia came in calmer than she once would have. She listened closely, took notes, and only volunteered where her energy could actually matter. She proposed one specific contribution rather than five vague ones, and because it was specific, people took it seriously.

One of the upperclassmen nodded and said, “That would really help. You always make things clearer when a plan gets too crowded.”

It was a small comment, but it landed.

Someone was watching.

Noticing.

And not for superficial reasons.

What they were noticing was judgment. Follow-through. The ability to bring order and clarity into busy spaces.

That evening Malik and Nia crossed paths near the mathematics building while the last gold light of the day stretched across the walkway.

“You look thoughtful,” Nia said.

“I’m trying to decide if that’s good news or pressure,” Malik replied.

“What is?”

“The fact that people are starting to notice things.”

Nia tilted her head. “I think it depends on whether you think you have to become someone new overnight.”

“And if I don’t?”

“Then it’s just confirmation,” she said. “Of what you’ve already been building.”

That steadied him more than she knew.

Saturday, March 21, brought another busy sports day—baseball again at Arkansas Pine Bluff and softball at Alabama State for a doubleheader—and still both of them protected the core of the day for academic growth.

Malik gave himself a two-hour deep-work block at the library. The room had its usual spring-semester atmosphere: laptops open, chargers stretched awkwardly toward outlets, whispered frustration, determined silence, the occasional relieved exhale when a solution finally clicked. There was something comforting about working among people who were all carrying serious goals in different forms.

He moved carefully through a difficult set of calculus work, pausing whenever he felt the urge to skip straight from intuition to execution. More and more, he could hear his professors’ voices as part of his own internal discipline.

State assumptions.
Clean setup.
Don’t let speed impersonate understanding.

At one point he caught himself trying to force a familiar pattern onto a problem that did not quite fit. The old version of him might have kept pushing anyway, hoping to wrestle the problem into submission. This time he stopped, crossed out the false start, and began again from the concept.

The reset cost him a few minutes.

It probably saved him twenty.

Elsewhere, Nia led a whiteboard study circle that felt increasingly like a real academic community rather than a loose gathering of anxious students. She opened the session by saying, “Today, I want us to picture the idea before we calculate it. If we can’t see what the math is doing, the symbols will start bossing us around.”

That line made the group laugh, but it also unlocked something.

They worked more slowly at first, sketching shapes, relationships, and conceptual connections before moving into algebra. A student who usually stayed quiet raised a hand and said, “I think I finally understand why this step happens instead of just memorizing that it happens.”

Nia smiled immediately. “That’s the whole point.”

In moments like that, the culture of the FAMU Mathematics Department shone clearly. Rigor was never treated as coldness. High standards did not require humiliation. Students were pushed to think well, but they were also given room to grow into that standard through mentorship, repetition, and community.

Sunday, March 22, brought homework due, a weekly planning reset, and one more baseball game at Arkansas Pine Bluff. It was the kind of day that could disappear into maintenance work if handled carelessly. Instead, it became one of the most quietly important days of the chapter.

Malik did timed calculus drills and then reviewed the mistakes without flinching from them. He had become disciplined about naming the source of each miss.

Unclear initial condition.
Visualized too late.
Moved before checking what was actually being asked.

The notes no longer felt like evidence against him. They were evidence for him—proof that he was learning how to diagnose himself accurately.

Nia spent part of the day rewriting class notes into a large concept map, drawing connections between procedures, interpretations, and recurring ideas. Dr. Bennett’s phrase—interpret numbers as evidence—stayed with her as she worked. She realized it applied to her own life too. The week itself was producing evidence. Her steadier schedule, sharper choices, stronger study sessions, and better boundaries were not small side notes. They were data. They showed that maturity was not theoretical anymore.

When they met later for a short planning check-in, the tone between them was easy.

“I think I’m finally respecting the reset day,” Malik said.

Nia nodded. “That’s because you stopped treating it like a day for catching up on guilt.”

He laughed. “That is painfully accurate.”

“It’s supposed to help the week feel possible,” she said. “Not punish you for the last one.”

Monday, March 23, moved the story forward with renewed academic pressure. A calculus problem set was assigned. Softball traveled to Auburn. Malik attended office hours with targeted questions, and Nia helped a peer with a difficult concept.

This was the meaningful resolution promised by the previous week’s anticipation: the sense that bigger things were coming did not explode into a dramatic announcement. It resolved at the start of this week through recognition that they were already operating inside bigger responsibility. Their ordinary choices now had influence.

During office hours, Malik asked a question that was narrower and sharper than the ones he used to bring.

Dr. Price looked over his page and said, “You’re asking better questions.”

Malik blinked. “Because I know more?”

“Partly,” Dr. Price said. “But mostly because you’re thinking before you ask. That matters.”

It was such a simple comment that it almost passed by unnoticed. But it stayed with him because it named the exact change he had been trying to build.

Meanwhile, Nia sat with a classmate who had become tangled in a concept and, worse, had started narrating the struggle as a personal failure.

Nia stopped that quickly.

“No,” she said gently. “You are not behind as a person. You are just unclear on one part of the chain.”

Then she broke the idea apart with patience, asking the student to explain each step back in their own words. By the end, the student sat up straighter.

“Okay,” they said, almost surprised. “That actually makes sense now.”

Nia felt a quiet satisfaction that had less to do with being helpful and more to do with what kind of mathematician she was becoming. Someone who could understand deeply enough to make others less afraid.

Tuesday, March 24, had no major sports event attached to it, but it still carried momentum. Malik updated his internship checklist again, this time adding action items rather than just possibilities. Nia attended another planning meeting and noticed she was increasingly being treated as someone dependable, not just enthusiastic.

That distinction mattered.

Dependable was heavier than impressive.

Dependable meant someone could build with you.

Wednesday, March 25, tightened the week with a quiz or concept-check window and baseball at home against Troy University. The home-game energy lifted campus noticeably. Students moved with extra buzz, and the atmosphere around FAMU felt proud and bright. But inside the math spaces, the pressure was real.

Malik returned to the library for another deep-work block, and Nia led another whiteboard study circle before the concept check. There was tension in both of them, but it was cleaner tension now—not chaotic, not self-defeating. It was the tension of people who cared and had prepared.

Before the quiz, Nia told the group, “Don’t let the first hard-looking problem decide your emotional state. Read. Breathe. Find structure.”

Malik, hearing that from nearby, thought: that’s exactly what she has learned to do herself.

The concept check came and went in the concentrated blur familiar to every student deep into spring semester. By the time it ended, both of them looked tired, but not shaken.

Outside, with the sounds of the baseball crowd beginning to rise from a distance, they exchanged the kind of relieved smile that only means something after honest effort.

“How’d it feel?” Nia asked.

“Fair,” Malik said. “Not easy. But fair.”

She nodded. “Same.”

Then he added, “I noticed something.”

“What?”

“I didn’t spend the whole time trying to prove I belonged.”

Nia’s expression softened. “That’s big.”

He looked out across campus. “I just worked the problems.”

Thursday, March 26, ended the week with no flashy event, which made it the perfect day for reflection. Malik practiced calculus drills with a timer once more. Nia rewrote her notes into an even cleaner concept map. Dr. Okafor’s familiar wisdom—visualize the concept first—echoed through both of their routines in different ways.

By late afternoon they found themselves walking slowly past the mathematics building again, the place that had quietly held so many of their turning points.

“This week felt different,” Nia said.

Malik nodded. “Yeah. Less like chasing readiness.”

“More like using it?”

He smiled. “Exactly.”

For a while they walked in silence, watching students move through the ordinary beauty of campus life—friends greeting each other across the yard, someone hurrying toward a meeting, someone else standing outside laughing with a professor after a serious conversation had clearly turned lighter. The life of the university felt big around them, but not impersonal. That was one of FAMU’s gifts. Students could grow here without disappearing.

At last Nia said, “You know what I think the real shift is?”

“What?”

“We’re not just responding to the semester anymore. We’re shaping it.”

That was true, and both of them knew it.

But the week did not end in complete rest.

It ended with a new and sharpened anticipation.

Because once readiness starts looking real—once professors notice, once peers trust you, once your habits begin producing visible steadiness—another possibility rises right behind it.

Responsibility expands.

Not someday. Soon.

As they headed in separate directions, each carrying notes, unfinished work, and the strange satisfaction of a demanding week handled well, the feeling that followed them was not fear.

It was something brighter and heavier at the same time.

Expectation.

They had spent weeks building discipline, clarity, and trust.

Now it felt increasingly likely that the FAMU Mathematics Department, and the larger world opening around it, would ask them to do something more with all three.

And for the first time, that possibility did not feel distant.

It felt close enough to hear approaching.
