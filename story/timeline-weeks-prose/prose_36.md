# Week Thirty-Six: The Week the Door Opened Wider

April did not begin by asking Malik and Nia whether they were ready for more.

It simply arrived with more already in motion.

The unresolved feeling from the end of the previous week—the sense that trust was turning into leadership and that leadership would soon demand something larger—found its answer early on Friday, April 3. The answer was not a single dramatic crisis. It was a week shaped by overlapping pressures: athletic energy across campus, repeated exam-window tension, office hours, study circles, concept checks, and the quiet realization that other students were no longer only watching Malik and Nia grow.

They were beginning to depend on them.

That Friday morning FAMU felt bright, busy, and alive in the particular way it often did when academics and athletics were both pulling at the edges of the day. Baseball was at Prairie View A&M. Softball was hosting Alabama A&M. Students moved across campus in clusters, carrying backpacks, water bottles, game talk, deadlines, and the slightly sharpened expressions that appeared whenever a demanding stretch of the semester came into view.

Malik had claimed a table early and set a timer on his phone before opening his calculus review packet.

A few months ago, the timer would have felt like an accusation.

Now it felt like training.

He worked through one derivative problem, then another, checking not only speed but whether he was staying clean in the setup. Whenever his pencil moved too fast, he heard the accumulated voices of the semester correcting him: Dr. Brooks telling him to show his reasoning clearly, Dr. Delgado insisting on a clean setup before solving, Dr. Price asking him to state his assumptions before he started treating an answer like destiny.

Across from him, Nia spread out her notes and began transforming pages of lecture material into a concept map. She used arrows, circles, color, and careful spacing, building relationships instead of just lists. To anyone passing by, it might have looked decorative. It was not. It was one of the ways she had learned to keep herself from drowning in information by forcing ideas to reveal their structure.

One first-year student slowed near their table.

“Y’all already studying for next week?” he asked.

Malik glanced up. “Not next week,” he said. “This week.”

The student blinked. “That bad?”

Nia smiled. “Not bad. Just real.”

The student laughed, but then lingered. “Can I sit for a minute? I’m trying to figure out where to even start.”

And just like that, the week’s answer began taking shape.

The invitation from the previous week had become something more practical. Malik slid his review packet slightly to the side. Nia turned her notebook so the student could see the map. What followed was not a full tutoring session, not yet, but a small beginning: a conversation about how to organize study time before the pressure became panic.

“Don’t start with everything,” Malik said. “Start with what keeps showing up.”

“And connect topics before you memorize steps,” Nia added. “If the ideas are isolated in your head, the test will feel harder than it is.”

By the time the student left, he looked lighter.

“So,” Nia said once he was gone, “I think your comment from yesterday came true fast.”

Malik capped his pen. “About holding the door open?”

She nodded.

He looked around the student union, the courtyard beyond, the movement of campus. “Yeah,” he said quietly. “I think the door just got wider.”

Saturday, April 4, kept baseball at Prairie View A&M on people’s minds, but academically it gave Malik and Nia a different kind of momentum. Malik went to office hours with targeted questions, his page arranged with uncommon discipline: not a scattered list of complaints, but specific points where his reasoning broke down under time pressure. Dr. Delgado reviewed the page in silence first, then nodded.

“This,” she said, tapping the margin, “is the work of someone who is learning how to diagnose instead of just react.”

Malik exhaled a small laugh. “I’m trying.”

“I can see that.” She pointed to one problem. “Your algebra is not the main issue here. Your setup is too eager. You are solving before the problem has fully introduced itself.”

That sentence landed with the force of something true far beyond mathematics.

Dr. Delgado continued, “Clean setup before solving. Not because neatness is pretty. Because clarity protects you from avoidable mistakes.”

Malik rewrote the first lines of the problem more slowly.

“There,” she said. “Now continue.”

This time the rest unfolded with less friction.

Meanwhile Nia spent part of the afternoon helping a classmate who had grown quiet in recent weeks. At first he insisted he was “mostly fine,” which in college often meant exactly the opposite. Nia did not push too hard. She simply asked him to explain the concept in his own words. Halfway through the explanation, he stopped and rubbed his forehead.

“I know pieces of it,” he admitted. “But it doesn’t stay connected.”

Nia nodded with immediate recognition. “That’s actually useful information.”

He looked surprised. “Useful?”

“Yes. Because now we know the issue isn’t that you’re incapable. It’s that the ideas are floating instead of attaching.”

She drew the concept visually, building the structure slowly enough for him to see it emerge. When the understanding clicked, his posture changed.

“Oh,” he said, almost laughing. “That actually makes sense now.”

“That’s because it always could,” Nia replied. “You just needed a better route into it.”

Moments like that energized her, but this semester had taught her to watch the difference between meaningful service and overextension. So after helping him, she closed her notebook instead of opening it for three more hours of unscheduled work. Growth, for Nia, now included stopping before generosity turned into exhaustion.

Sunday, April 5, brought homework due and the usual weekly planning reset. The campus pace softened slightly, but not enough for anyone serious to mistake it for rest. Malik updated his internship and career checklist in the morning, making notes not only about deadlines and applications but about skills he wanted to discuss more confidently: quantitative reasoning, consistency under pressure, collaborative problem-solving, and communication. He was beginning to understand that the strongest version of his résumé would not be a list of claims but a record of habits already forming.

Nia attended a student organization planning meeting that could easily have expanded into a chaotic swirl of good intentions. Instead, she helped narrow it.

“What absolutely needs to happen this week?” she asked, marker in hand.

The room went quiet.

“What would be nice but not necessary?”

An upperclassman grinned. “You really do know how to save a meeting.”

Nia laughed, but she still drew two columns on the board: **Must Move** and **Can Wait**.

It was a small act, yet it echoed the same lesson she was learning academically. Clear structure was not restrictive. It was what allowed energy to become useful.

Later that evening Malik and Nia met outside with their planners, notes, and the sky slowly turning gold over campus.

“This week feels crowded,” Malik said.

“It is crowded,” Nia answered. “But I don’t think crowded has to mean chaotic.”

He looked at her, half-smiling. “That sounds like something you should put on a poster for the department.”

“Only if you put ‘clean setup before solving’ under it.”

They laughed, but beneath the joke was a real sense of readiness. The coming exam windows were no longer abstract. They were approaching fast.

Monday, April 6, sharpened the week. A new calculus problem set was assigned. Malik protected a two-hour deep-work block in the library, stacking his materials carefully and refusing to let his attention scatter. Across the table from him sat two students from an earlier study circle, and when one of them got stuck, he hesitated before asking.

“Can I interrupt for one question?”

Malik looked up. Months earlier he might have guarded his focus too tightly, afraid that helping someone else would cost him momentum. Now he understood something more mature: sometimes explaining a concept clarified your own thinking.

“Yeah,” he said. “Show me where it goes sideways.”

The student pointed to the line where the setup had drifted.

Malik smiled despite himself. “Okay. First thing? You started solving before the problem fully introduced itself.”

The phrase was so exactly Dr. Delgado’s that both students laughed.

“Wow,” the other one said. “You sound like a professor.”

Malik shook his head. “Not even close. But the advice is good.”

Elsewhere, Nia led a whiteboard study circle that afternoon, and what began as a handful of students soon became a small crowd. She wrote the week’s major ideas at the top of the board and drew lines between them before anyone reached for shortcuts.

“Visualize the concept first,” she said, echoing Dr. Okafor. “Then let the symbols tell the same story.”

A quiet student in the back raised a hand and asked a question so tentative it nearly dissolved in the room. Nia took it seriously immediately. She repeated it clearly, answered it without hurry, and then asked if anyone else had wondered the same thing.

Three more hands went up.

That moment changed the tone of the room. Confusion stopped feeling embarrassing and started feeling communal. The study circle became exactly what the FAMU Mathematics Department at its best encouraged: rigorous, welcoming, confident without being cruel, and deeply invested in helping undergraduates grow together.

Tuesday, April 7, marked the first midterm-style exam window of the week.

Campus felt different from the start of the morning. Even the students joking on the walkways seemed to do it with a little extra edge. Malik ran timed calculus drills one last time before class, but instead of fighting the clock, he treated each problem like a conversation between method and judgment. Nia rewrote her notes into a fresh concept map, not because she had forgotten the material but because redrawing the structure helped steady her mind.

When they met briefly before the exam, neither tried to pretend complete calm.

“You good?” Malik asked.

“I’m focused,” Nia said. “Which is not the same thing as relaxed.”

“That’s honest.”

“You?”

“Nervous,” he admitted. “But not scattered.”

She nodded approvingly. “That’s growth.”

The exam itself demanded exactly what the semester had been trying to teach them: reasoning under pressure, clean setup, conceptual clarity, discipline. There were moments when Malik felt his pulse rise, but instead of rushing, he wrote one line at a time and forced himself to state what he knew before jumping to what he hoped. Nia encountered one problem that at first seemed familiar and then subtly shifted shape. For a second she felt the old urge to overwork every possibility. Instead she paused, sketched the idea, and let the structure reveal the path.

When they came out, the relief was real but incomplete.

“That was fair,” Malik said.

“Fair and sneaky,” Nia replied.

He laughed. “Yes. Exactly that.”

But there was pride there too. Not pride in perfection—something more durable than that. Pride in who they had become under academic stress.

Wednesday, April 8, raised the stakes again with both a quiz or concept-check window and another midterm-style pressure point, while baseball headed to Troy University. Malik returned to office hours for targeted questions about what still felt unstable after Tuesday. Nia helped a peer with a difficult concept, and by now the pattern had become unmistakable: students sought them out not only because they were strong, but because they were trustworthy.

Dr. Brooks listened to Malik explain a problem and then said, “You’re stronger when you let your reasoning be visible. Don’t hide the path trying to look fast.”

That advice met him exactly where he needed it.

At nearly the same hour, Nia sat with two students outside the math building, walking them through an idea one careful layer at a time. One of them finally said, “I thought asking this meant I was behind.”

Nia shook her head. “No. Asking before the confusion hardens is one of the smartest things you can do.”

By Thursday, April 9, the final exam-window tension of the week had settled over campus like weather. Malik updated his career checklist again, but this time he added a note at the top: **Keep building the habits that make future opportunities real.** Nia attended another student organization meeting, more measured now, saying yes where it mattered and no where it protected her academic priorities.

That evening they met after the last major pressure point of the week, both carrying the tiredness that only honest effort creates.

For a while they sat without talking, watching students cross campus in the blue-gold light of early evening.

Then Malik said, “I think I finally understand something.”

Nia turned toward him. “What?”

“That being part of this department isn’t just about surviving the hard classes.” He looked back toward the building. “It’s about learning how to help create the kind of environment where other people can survive them too.”

Nia’s expression softened. “Yeah,” she said. “I think that’s what this week was really about.”

He glanced at her. “And I think it’s only getting bigger from here.”

That was the week’s final truth.

The anticipation from Week Thirty-Five had been resolved: yes, trust had turned into responsibility. Yes, the door had opened wider. Yes, Malik and Nia were becoming the kind of students others could lean on.

But the resolution did not close the story.

It opened the next one.

Because as they sat there, exhausted but steadier than they had been at the start of the year, both of them could feel another layer of spring approaching. More deadlines. Bigger decisions. More visible opportunities. More chances to lead, to choose wisely, to protect their own growth while making room for someone else’s.

The week had proven they could carry more.

Now April seemed ready to test exactly how much more that would be.

Around them, FAMU still moved with its familiar mix of excellence, warmth, pressure, and possibility. Students headed toward residence halls, meetings, games, dining spots, and late-night study plans. Somewhere inside the mathematics building, whiteboards still held traces of proofs, diagrams, assumptions, and half-erased breakthroughs.

Malik stood first and slung his bag over one shoulder.

“Same time next week?” he asked.

Nia smiled, tired but bright. “For studying or saving the department one conversation at a time?”

He grinned. “Apparently both.”

They started walking.

Behind them was a week that had asked for focus, steadiness, and care—and had received all three.

Ahead of them was a stretch of spring that no longer felt like something happening to them.

It felt like something they were growing ready to shape.

And that made the next week feel not just busy, but electric.
