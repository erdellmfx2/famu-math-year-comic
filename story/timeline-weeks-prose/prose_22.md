# Week Twenty-Two: The Threshold Between Rest and Return

The excitement they had named on Christmas Day did not disappear once the holiday lights dimmed.

If anything, it became easier to hear.

By the morning after Christmas, the week had settled into a quieter rhythm, but underneath that calm was the same unmistakable feeling Malik and Nia had ended the last chapter with: spring no longer looked like a shadow waiting to test them. It looked like a door. A demanding one, maybe. A door that would require discipline, patience, and sharper thinking than ever before. But still a door—something they could walk toward on purpose.

That realization gave the start of the week its shape.

It was not a week of dramatic campus emergencies or looming exam deadlines. FAMU was still in winter-break mode. Residence halls were quieter. Classroom buildings stood mostly still. The loudest routines of the semester had given way to family visits, slower mornings, and the kind of open calendar space students usually claim they want and then do not always know how to use. Yet for Malik and Nia, the stillness did not feel empty. It felt useful. It gave them room to interpret what the fall had taught them and decide, more deliberately than before, what kind of students they wanted to be when the campus energy surged back.

Friday, December 26, began with ordinary comfort.

Malik spent the morning with family, sitting in a living room where conversations drifted easily between holiday stories, neighborhood updates, and the usual teasing reserved for someone home just long enough to be both celebrated and assigned small chores. He did not mind. There was something grounding about being in a space where no one cared about his notation habits or whether he had finally perfected a cleaner approach to solving multi-step problems. Here, he was simply Malik—helping move chairs, carrying dishes, answering questions about school, smiling every time someone said they were proud of him.

Later, when the house grew quieter, he opened a notebook for only a short while. He kept his review light, exactly as he had promised himself. A few pages of old material. A few reminders to keep his reasoning visible. No panic, no attempt to win winter break by turning it into a secret study marathon.

What surprised him most was how natural that restraint felt.

A few months earlier, he would have interpreted light review as evidence that he was falling behind. Now he recognized it as evidence that he trusted his own process more than he used to. Dr. Okafor’s words drifted back to him as he sketched a quick diagram beside a problem idea: **visualize the concept first**. It had started as mathematical advice. Now it seemed to apply to the whole semester ahead. Before he rushed into spring, he wanted to see its shape.

Across town, Nia was doing almost the opposite kind of work and somehow arriving at the same destination.

She had given herself the day for what she called a creative reset. That meant reading without guilt, letting her mind wander, and refusing to confuse constant productivity with meaningful growth. A novel lay open beside her. So did a notebook where, every few pages, she drifted into reflections about the term ahead.

She was not writing formal goals yet. She was listening for patterns.

What parts of last semester had made her feel most alive?

Where had she been most stretched?

What did she want to carry forward, and what needed to stay behind in December?

By the afternoon, she sent Malik a voice note.

“I think I figured something out,” she said, her tone bright with that familiar mix of curiosity and conviction. “I spent so much of the fall trying to prove I could handle everything. Not every class. Everything. The clubs, the helping, the showing up, the being enthusiastic all the time. And now I’m realizing spring might go better if I spend less energy performing capacity and more energy protecting it.”

Malik listened twice before replying.

“That sounds right,” he sent back. “Also sounds like something you should write down somewhere permanent before January tries to trick you.”

Her answer came quickly.

“Already journaled. I’m becoming terrifyingly self-aware.”

He laughed out loud in the quiet room, and the sound drew a question from an aunt in the kitchen.

“What’s funny?” she called.

“School people,” Malik answered.

But even as he said it, he knew that phrase no longer captured what Nia had become in his life. She was not just another student he studied with. She was part of the system of reflection and accountability that had helped him grow. FAMU had given them courses, professors, and opportunities, yes—but it had also given them each other, and that mattered more every week.

Saturday deepened the reflective mood.

Malik turned toward career thinking, a task that sounded simple until he sat down to do it honestly. Resume polishing was never just formatting. It was interpretation. It asked him to decide what counted as evidence of becoming. He opened his documents, revised descriptions, adjusted wording, and slowly began to see a difference between the way he might have represented himself in August and the way he could represent himself now.

In August, ambition had done most of the talking.

By late December, there was more substance under it.

He had learned to recover from mistakes without dramatizing them. He had learned that precision meant more than getting correct answers; it also meant being clear about assumptions, steps, and limitations. He had learned that professional growth in a field like actuarial science would require not just mathematical strength, but consistency, communication, and the humility to keep refining weak points.

As he typed, he remembered Dr. Bennett’s advice: **interpret numbers as evidence**.

The phrase had lived in statistics and career conversations, but today it returned with broader force. Grades were evidence. Study habits were evidence. The fact that he now had a reliable error log, cleaner notes, and a more honest understanding of his own patterns—that was evidence too. Not of finished success, but of direction.

Nia spent the same day in journal reflection, though her pages moved with more emotion than Malik’s resume drafts ever would. She wrote about how much she had changed without noticing it in real time. She wrote about the early weeks of the semester, when every hard problem had felt like a referendum on her identity. She wrote about how the FAMU Mathematics Department had slowly taught her another way to think—one where challenge was normal, process mattered, and asking sharper questions was a sign of growth, not weakness.

At one point she paused, looked out the window, and thought about the culture surrounding her.

There was something powerful about learning mathematics in a place where community and excellence were not treated as opposites. At FAMU, she had seen students support one another without lowering standards. She had seen professors mentor with seriousness and care. She had seen campus life, school pride, and academic ambition coexist in ways that made success feel not lonely, but shared.

That thought stayed with her long enough that she wrote a line in large letters across the page:

**I do not want to succeed alone.**

Sunday, December 28, brought a burst of visible Rattler energy again with the men’s basketball game at Georgia Tech.

Even during break, FAMU’s momentum refused to vanish entirely. Group chats lit up. Social posts circulated. Students checked scores, traded predictions, and carried school pride across state lines through their phones and conversations. For Malik and Nia, the game became part of the day’s background music—evidence that campus identity was still alive even while classes paused.

Malik used the day for rest and reset routines. He cleaned his space, reorganized a drawer he had been avoiding for weeks, and made himself do the kind of small physical maintenance that always improved his thinking once he finally stopped resisting it. There was satisfaction in creating order without urgency.

Nia, meanwhile, allowed herself a light preview of next topics. Not deep studying—just enough contact to make the coming term feel familiar rather than abrupt. She skimmed notes, circled a few ideas, and stopped before the work could harden into pressure.

That evening they talked by phone while both half-followed reactions to the basketball game.

“So,” Nia said, “have you noticed that we’re doing something deeply suspicious?”

Malik frowned. “That sentence usually means you’re about to accuse me of being organized.”

“No, this is worse. We are preparing for next semester in a healthy way.”

He smiled. “Disturbing.”

“I know. Where is the panic? Where is the chaos? Where is the last-minute identity crisis?”

“Retired, hopefully.”

She was quiet for a second before answering more seriously. “I think the difference is that the anticipation from Christmas actually settled into something real. I’m still excited. But now I’m not excited in a vague way. I know what I’m excited for.”

“What is it?” Malik asked.

“Getting better on purpose.”

The answer stayed with him after the call ended.

Monday and Tuesday unfolded in a gentle alternation of family time, reading, reflection, and preparation. Malik returned to light review without overextending it. Nia moved between creative rest and more journaling, each entry a little more practical than the last. When she revisited Dr. Brooks’s reminder to **show your reasoning clearly**, she realized it applied to her planning too. So she stopped writing only in moods and started outlining actual intentions.

Not twenty goals.

Not an impossible ideal version of herself.

Just a few meaningful commitments: protect study time, say no earlier, ask deeper questions, leave room for joy.

Malik, for his part, found himself thinking often about Dr. Delgado’s emphasis on clean setup before solving. He began applying it to his environment, his calendar, even the way he thought about future opportunities. If spring was going to demand more, then he wanted the setup to honor that. Better systems now meant less noise later.

By New Year’s Eve, the reflective energy of the week had gathered into something almost ceremonial.

The day carried its own natural sense of threshold. Even people who claimed not to care about resolutions still felt, in some corner of themselves, the tug of review and possibility. Malik leaned into that mood through rest and reset routines, making the day quiet enough to hear his own thinking. Nia used part of the evening for a final light preview of future material, then closed her notebook on purpose before midnight.

They exchanged messages as the year turned.

**Happy New Year,** Malik wrote. **We made it through semester one.**

Nia replied: **And we are absolutely not the same people who started it.**

A few minutes passed, and then she added another message.

**Do you think next semester is when it starts feeling real? Like not just surviving college, but actually building a path?**

Malik looked at the question for a long moment.

He thought about office hours, about corrected mistakes, about the slow transformation of fear into discipline. He thought about the pride he felt whenever he saw FAMU students striving visibly, seriously, joyfully. He thought about the professors who had not only taught content, but modeled intellectual maturity. He thought about how often the Mathematics Department seemed to quietly tell students, through culture as much as instruction: you belong in rigorous spaces, and you can grow into even more.

Finally he typed back:

**I think it already started. I just think spring is when we’ll notice it more.**

New Year’s Day arrived with that answer still hanging between them.

January 1 was officially a holiday, but emotionally it felt like a beginning in progress. Malik spent time with family again and did only the lightest academic touchpoint. Nia read, reflected, and let herself enjoy the day instead of loading it with symbolic pressure. Yet both of them carried a sharpened sense that the week had done exactly what it needed to do.

The unresolved excitement from the previous chapter had not vanished; it had matured.

At the start of the week, spring had been a promising unknown.

By the end of it, spring had a clearer meaning.

It would be the season where better habits were tested. The season where confidence had to become practice. The season where belonging at FAMU—and specifically within the culture of the FAMU Mathematics Department—would move beyond aspiration and begin to feel lived in. Research, mentorship, peer support, stronger academic identity, deeper campus involvement: none of those possibilities felt imaginary anymore.

They felt near.

That nearness created the final tension of the week.

Not fear.

Not confusion.

A sharpened readiness that still had nowhere to go just yet.

Winter break was still holding them in a pause, and pauses can be strangely intense when people know movement is coming. Malik could feel it in the way he kept glancing at plans without obsessing over them. Nia could feel it in the way her journal entries kept drifting from reflection into strategy. They were both still resting. They were both still with family, books, routines, and the gentler side of campus life. But they were no longer merely recovering from the fall.

They were preparing to return stronger.

And somewhere just beyond the calm of the holiday week, the next chapter of the year was beginning to gather speed.

When FAMU called them back in full—back to classrooms, questions, whiteboards, deadlines, and the rising expectations of spring—they would answer differently than they had in August.

Not because the road would be easy.

But because they had finally learned how to meet hard things with steadiness, with community, and with a sense of purpose that was starting to feel like home.

That was the gift of Week Twenty-Two.

It turned anticipation into intention.

And by the time the New Year opened in front of them, both Malik and Nia could feel it:

the real momentum of year one was only just beginning.
