# Week 2 Prose Narrative — August 8–14, 2025
## College Prep Week: Building Foundations Before the Starting Line

---

### Overview

Week 2 captures the quiet, purposeful rhythm of preparation. Classes haven't started yet, but Malik and Nia are already in motion — not with the urgency of deadlines, but with the deliberate energy of students who know that August is when the foundation gets laid. This week introduces recurring patterns: the importance of structure versus curiosity, the growth of their complementary partnership, and the steady presence of FAMU's faculty whose guidance shapes not just how they study, but who they become.

---

### Day-by-Day Story

---

#### Friday, August 8 — The Organization Phase

The morning sun spills through the blinds of Malik's dorm room as he sits at his desk with a mechanical pencil and a fresh stack of composition notebooks. He's been awake since 6:30 AM, unable to shake the feeling that everything depends on how well he sets up now. On his laptop, he opens the FAMU course registration portal and begins creating a folder system — one per class, with subfolders for syllabi, assignments, notes, and study guides.

**"Control what you can control,"** he mutters, labeling his final folder: MAC2311 — Calculus I.

His phone buzzes. It's a group chat from the actuarial science student association — they're planning a meet-and-greet for the second week of classes. Malik saves the date immediately. **Every commitment needs a slot.**

Meanwhile, Nia sits cross-legged on her bed across campus, a spiral notebook open in her lap. She's drafting her personal learning goals for the semester — not just grades, but what she wants to understand deeply:

- *"Can I explain the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus without looking at notes?"*
- *"I want to feel comfortable asking questions in class."*
- *"I want to find math people who also love philosophy."*

Her roommate laughs. **"You're writing a manifesto for a class that hasn't started yet."**

Nia grins. **"That's exactly the point."**

Later that afternoon, both students independently walk past the SET (Science, Education, and Technology) building — the home of FAMU's mathematics department. Neither enters, but both pause. Malik glances at the office hours posted on a bulletin board. Nia peers through the glass at a hallway lined with faculty photos and student research posters. **It's the first time either of them sees the words "Undergraduate Research in Mathematics" in print.**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Okafor's principle echoes in their minds: *"Visualize the concept first."* Nia writes it in the margin of her notebook. Malik types it into a fresh Google Doc titled "Course Maxims."

**Comic Scene Hint:** A split-panel showing Malik's hyper-organized folder structure (laptop, colored tabs, neat handwriting) versus Nia's creative chaos (sticky notes in different colors, a mind map branching outward, and a coffee mug with "I think, therefore I'm confused" on it).

#### Saturday, August 9 — The Numbers of Everyday Life

**"Welcome to adulthood,"** Malik says to himself, staring at a spreadsheet he's built from scratch.

The spreadsheet is beautiful — color-coded, formula-driven, with projected balances for the entire semester. Tuition (covered by scholarship). Meal plan (budgeted). Textbooks (estimated). Entertainment ($50/month — he's being generous). Emergency fund (mandatory). He even has a tab for "Actuarial Exam Fees" that won't apply until next year, but **Malik believes in preparation.**

He texts his mom a screenshot: *"Look what I made."*

She replies with three heart emojis and a single sentence: *"That's my boy."*

Across the hall, a roommate asks if Malik can help him with his own budget. **For Malik, this is the first tiny confirmation that his skills have value beyond the classroom.**

Nia's morning looks different. She's sitting outside on a bench near the quad, sunlight filtering through oak trees, organizing her notes. But she's not organizing by subject — she's organizing by color:

- **Blue** for definitions
- **Green** for theorems
- **Pink** for examples
- **Orange** for questions she wants to ask

It looks chaotic to anyone passing by, but to Nia, **it's a map of how her brain works.** The colors help her remember. The layout helps her connect ideas that textbooks keep separate.

**"Your notes look like a rainbow exploded,"** says a passing upperclassman, smiling.

**"Exactly,"** Nia replies. **"And I know exactly where everything is."**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Bennett's voice from orientation week: *"Interpret numbers as evidence."* Malik applies it to his budget — what data tells him about his spending patterns. Nia applies it to her notes — does the evidence support her understanding of the material?

**Comic Scene Hint:** Two parallel vignettes — Malik in a quiet room with perfect columns and formulas, Nia outside under the trees with a notebook that looks more like art than notes.

#### Sunday, August 10 — The Organizations Fair and the Meal Plan Dilemma

The dining hall at lunchtime is a small war zone of indecision. Malik stands in line, phone in hand, calculator app open. He's comparing:

- **Plan A:** 14 meals/week + $200 Rattler Bucks = $2,100/semester
- **Plan B:** 19 meals/week + $100 Rattler Bucks = $2,550/semester
- **Plan C:** Unlimited + $50 Rattler Bucks = $2,850/semester

He runs the numbers three times. **The actuarial mind doesn't guess — it computes.** He settles on Plan A plus an extra $50 for emergency snacks. Optimized.

Nia, meanwhile, is at the **Student Organizations Fair** on the quad — a sea of tables, banners, flyers, and upperclassmen waving sign-up sheets.

- **FAMU Math Club:** *"We do puzzles and pizza every other Friday."* — Join.
- **National Society of Black Engineers (NSBE):** *"Internships, mentorship, conferences."* — Join.
- **FAMU Chapter of the Association for Women in Mathematics:** *"Research talks and grad school prep."* — Join.
- **Golden Girls Dance Team:** *"Auditions in September."* — Nia smiles, thinks about it, writes down the date.
- **SGA Freshman Council:** *"Application due Friday."* — She takes a form.

By the time she's done, she's signed up for five organizations and has three application deadlines to track.

**"Maybe I overdid it,"** she whispers to herself, walking back to her dorm with a stack of flyers.

**She did overdo it. But she doesn't know that yet.**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Price's maxim — *"State your assumptions before modeling"* — becomes useful here. Malik's budget is only as good as his assumptions about textbook costs. Nia's schedule is only as good as her assumptions about how much time each commitment requires. **Neither has accounted for the assumption that fails first.**

**Comic Scene Hint:** The organizations fair in full color — tables stretching across the quad, Nia's arms full of flyers, a banner reading "FAMU: The Pride of the Nation" in the background. A small detail: Dr. Brooks walks past carrying coffee, nodding approvingly at the crowd of new students.

#### Monday, August 11 — Mapping the Future

Malik spends the morning in the library, not studying — **planning.** He's researching the actuarial science pathway at FAMU:

- Course sequence (confirmed)
- SOA (Society of Actuaries) exam schedule (Exam P in January is ambitious but possible)
- Internship application windows (spring recruiting starts February)
- Recommended extracurriculars (FAMU Financial Math Society, case competitions)

He prints out a **four-year plan** and tapes it above his desk. **Day one of the long game.**

He texts his academic advisor: *"When can I take the first actuarial exam? I want to take Exam P before sophomore year."*

The reply comes within an hour: *"Let's talk in Week Two. I can recommend study materials and help you register. Good initiative, Malik."*

Malik leans back in his chair. **For the first time since arriving on campus, he feels like he belongs in a long-term story.**

Nia's Monday is less about the career and more about the person. She sits in her room with a blank piece of paper and draws a circle in the center labeled "Me." From it, she draws spokes:

- **Academics**
- **Health**
- **Friendships**
- **Family**
- **Rest**
- **Exploration**

**"I need a balance plan,"** she tells herself. **"Not a schedule — a plan."**

She decides:
- **Study blocks:** 9 AM–12 PM, 2 PM–5 PM (max)
- **Exercise:** 3x/week minimum
- **Friendship time:** At least one unplanned interaction daily
- **Rest:** No work after 10 PM. Non-negotiable.

**This is Nia's superpower — self-awareness before the pressure hits.**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Brooks' advice crystallizes: *"Show your reasoning clearly."* Malik applies it to his four-year plan — every decision documented, every assumption annotated. Nia applies it to her balance plan — the reasoning behind each boundary, written in the margins of her notebook.

**Comic Scene Hint:** Split screen — left side: Malik's wall with the four-year plan and actuarial exam schedule pinned above his desk. Right side: Nia's circle-spoke diagram, posted on her wall next to a photo of her family.

#### Tuesday, August 12 — Repeat, Refine, Remember

The week's rhythm begins to emerge. Malik is back at his folder system, this time adding digital backups to Google Drive and a shared folder for his study group. **He's learned from yesterday: preparation is iteration, not a single pass.**

He also checks his budget spreadsheet again, realizing he underestimated textbook costs by $80. He adjusts. **The model improves.**

Nia reviews her learning goals from Friday and adds three more:
- *"Attend at least one office hour in the first two weeks."*
- *"Find one upperclassman mentor in math."*
- *"Learn one professor's teaching style before the first exam."*

**These aren't just goals — they're pre-commitments to growth.**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Delgado's voice cuts through: *"Clean setup before solving."* Malik applies it to his semester prep: clean folder structure, clean budget, clean schedule. Nia applies it to her mindset: clear intentions, clear boundaries, clear questions.

**Comic Scene Hint:** A single panel showing both students in their respective rooms, both making small adjustments — Malik with a calculator and receipt, Nia with a pen and notebook — unaware of each other but building the same discipline in parallel.

#### Wednesday, August 13 — Visualization Returns

The weather is thick and humid as Malik walks across campus to the student union. He sits in the corner with a textbook he bought used — *Calculus: Early Transcendentals*, 9th edition — and reads the first section.

**He doesn't try to solve anything.** He visualizes.

Dr. Okafor's words return: *"See the shape of the problem before you touch the numbers."*

Malik closes his eyes. **He pictures a curve. He pictures the tangent line touching it at exactly one point. He pictures the limit approaching, the derivative emerging from the geometry.**

When he opens his eyes, he writes nothing. **But he understands more than if he'd done twenty practice problems.**

Nia's Wednesday is about organizing her notes from the last few days. She has pages of color-coded material now — definitions, maxims from professors, personal goals, questions. She reorganizes them into a single binder:

- **Tab 1:** Course Notes (empty for now — classes haven't started)
- **Tab 2:** Professor Maxims (Dr. Okafor, Dr. Bennett, Dr. Brooks, Dr. Delgado, Dr. Price)
- **Tab 3:** Semester Plan (calendar, balance wheel, learning goals)
- **Tab 4:** Questions (things she wants to ask when class begins)

**She's building a knowledge system before she has knowledge to put in it.** It's preparation as architecture.

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Okafor again — *"Visualize the concept first"* — continues to be the week's thematic thread. Malik and Nia are both learning that **understanding begins with imagination, not calculation.**

**Comic Scene Hint:** Close-up on Malik, eyes closed, hand tracing a curve in the air. In the background, a faint visual echo of the derivative concept forming. Cut to Nia's binder, tabs labeled in her handwriting.

#### Thursday, August 14 — The Campus as Classroom

The week closes with both students stepping beyond their routines.

Malik takes a walk through the SET building, reading the bulletin boards with new attention:
- **NSF REU opportunities in mathematics** — poster from a summer program at Michigan State
- **FAMU Graduate Research Symposium** — call for undergraduate presenters
- **Tutoring positions available** — math department, paid, flexible hours

**He takes a photo of each one.** The future is taking shape.

Nia visits the campus organizations fair for the second time — not to sign up for more, but to make real connections. She talks to the president of the Math Club for fifteen minutes, learning about weekly problem sessions and guest speakers. She signs up for the email list. **She's not just collecting memberships anymore — she's building relationships.**

**Professor Touchpoint of the Day:** Dr. Bennett returns: *"Interpret numbers as evidence."* Malik interprets his photo collection as evidence that opportunities exist. Nia interprets her conversations as evidence that community is accessible.

**Comic Scene Hint:** A wide panel showing the SET building hallway — bulletin boards in the background, Malik taking a photo on his phone, Nia talking animatedly with the Math Club president. The FAMU seal visible above the door.

### Weekly Themes and Threads

**1. The Foundation of Preparation**
Week 2 establishes that both characters are building systems before the pressure begins. Malik builds spreadsheets, folder structures, and four-year plans. Nia builds learning goals, balance wheels, and question logs. **Neither approach is right or wrong — they're complementary.**

**2. Faculty Wisdom as Narrative Thread**
Each day features a professor's maxim woven into a character action. This week's rotation (Okafor → Bennett → Price → Brooks → Delgado → Okafor → Bennett) creates a rhythm where faculty guidance is constant even before classes begin. The maxims aren't just quotes — they're **tools the characters learn to use.**

**3. Parallel Growth, Separate Spaces**
Malik and Nia don't interact this week. They exist in parallel — similar dedication, different methods, separated by campus geography. The narrative trusts that readers can hold two stories simultaneously, building anticipation for when their paths finally cross.

**4. FAMU as Ecosystem**
The university is a character in its own right this week. The SET building, the organizations fair, the dining hall, the library, the quad — each location reinforces that **FAMU is a rich ecosystem of opportunity, not just a backdrop for math problems.** The bulletin boards, faculty offices, and student organizations all signal that this is a place where students are expected to grow.

**5. Undergraduate Research as Future Thread**
The REU poster, the research symposium call, and the math club's guest speaker series all plant seeds for future storylines. Neither Malik nor Nia knows it yet, but **their first research experience is being foreshadowed** by the campus environment around them. This is early — deliberately early — but the seeds are visible to careful readers.

**6. The Contrast Between Structure and Exploration**
Week 2's central tension is structural: Malik seeks control through organization, Nia seeks understanding through connection. Both are valid. The story doesn't rank them. Instead, it asks: **What happens when two very different preparation styles meet a shared challenge?** That question will define the first phase of the academic year.
