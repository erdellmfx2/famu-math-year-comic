# Week Twelve: The Week the Campus Learned to Hold Two Kinds of Energy

The first answer came quietly.

After all the tension that had built through the previous week—the checking and not checking, the half-finished conversations, the wary glances at portals and grade columns and unread notifications—Malik finally opened the result that had been haunting the edge of his mind.

He did it on Friday morning with less drama than he would have imagined.

Not in the middle of a crowd.
Not with Nia looking over his shoulder.
Not with some cinematic soundtrack playing in the background of his life.

He stood beneath the shade of a live oak near the mathematics building, phone in one hand, notebook in the other, and took a breath that felt more deliberate than natural.

When the page loaded, he blinked once.
Then again.

Not perfect.
But better than his fear had predicted.

A solid recovery. Proof that the systems he had started building were not imaginary. Proof that office hours, error logs, timed drills, and slower thinking were doing something real. Proof that the distance between panic and progress was sometimes shorter than it felt.

He stared at the screen for another second, then let out a breath that came out halfway as a laugh.

“Okay,” he murmured to nobody. “Okay. I can work with that.”

That sentence mattered.

A month ago, Malik might have judged the number against pride alone—whether it matched the version of himself he wanted everyone else to see. But this time he saw it differently. Not as a verdict. Not as a trophy. As evidence.

And at FAMU, that distinction was becoming part of how he lived.

By the time he met Nia later that morning, she could read the answer in his face before he said a word.

“You checked,” she said, narrowing her eyes with theatrical seriousness.

“I checked.”

“And?”

He tried to play it calm, but the corner of his mouth gave him away. “Better than the disaster story I wrote in my head.”

Nia stopped walking and pointed at him like he had just solved something bigger than a calculus problem. “See? That is growth. Not the grade part, even though I’m glad. The part where you admit your imagination was trying to sabotage you.”

He laughed. “That sounded exactly like something Dr. Bennett would say.”

“Good,” Nia said. “That means I’m spending time around the right people.”

They kept moving down the path, morning light slipping across the brick walkways, students passing in little clusters with backpacks, coffee cups, and the particular October expression that mixed exhaustion with determination. FAMU was alive in that way it often was during the middle of the semester—everybody carrying something, everybody becoming something.

The relief at the start of the day did not erase the week ahead. It simply changed the way Malik stepped into it.

Instead of moving like someone trying to outrun failure, he moved like someone finally willing to build.

That afternoon he headed to a study hall block with a timer, two sharpened pencils, and the same page of derivative drills he had nearly thrown across the room a week earlier. Only this time, he heard Dr. Okafor’s voice in his mind before he started.

*Visualize the concept first.*

So he did.

Before touching the first line, he sketched the curve. He marked what was increasing, what was flattening, what changed and why. He slowed down enough to understand the motion before asking his hands to perform it. For someone who had spent most of the semester trusting neatness more than intuition, the shift felt subtle and revolutionary at the same time.

When he caught himself rushing, he stopped.
When he made a mistake, he traced it back.
When he got one right, he asked why it worked.

Across the table, Nia was rewriting her class notes into a sprawling concept map so colorful and carefully arranged that it looked halfway between a study tool and a mural. Arrows linked ideas across topics. Definitions sat beside examples. Tiny questions in the margin marked places she wanted to return to later.

Malik looked over after a while and shook his head. “You make studying look artistic.”

Nia kept writing. “You make panic look organized. Everybody has gifts.”

He laughed hard enough that two students at the next table glanced over and smiled.

That was another thing FAMU gave them in small, steady ways: a culture where hard work did not always have to be lonely. The mathematics department was rigorous, yes. Exacting, yes. But it was also full of moments where laughter and correction and ambition could sit at the same table.

On Saturday, the campus shifted into game-day rhythm.

The football game against Alcorn State brought a wave of sound that seemed to start in the residence halls and ripple outward from there—music, footsteps, bright conversation, shirts in school colors, families arriving, student groups gathering. Even students who had promised themselves a quiet weekend felt the pull of it.

That was the challenge of a week like this: not choosing between academics and joy, but learning how to hold both without dropping either.

Malik met Nia near a campus lawn where students were already setting up for the day, and for once he did not look like someone internally negotiating a spreadsheet of guilt.

“You did your work early,” Nia said, instantly suspicious.

“I protected the morning,” he replied.

She gave him a look of solemn approval. “Look at you. Becoming one of the elders.”

“Please. I am nineteen.”

“In math-semester years, that’s at least thirty-seven.”

They spent part of the afternoon in the game-day current—friends calling out to them, alumni smiling at the clusters of students, the whole campus feeling briefly like a single conversation. But even there, the academic thread of the week did not disappear. It deepened.

At one point, while the band’s sound rolled through the air and the stands pulsed with energy, Nia found herself helping a classmate who had cornered her with a question that had been bothering him since Thursday.

“Not now if this is weird,” he said, already half apologizing, “but when Dr. Bennett says interpret numbers as evidence, I know what the words mean individually. I just don’t know what I’m supposed to do with them.”

Nia smiled, because that was such a real math-student problem—understanding every word and still not yet owning the idea.

“Okay,” she said, turning slightly away from the crowd so they could hear each other. “Think of it this way. Numbers aren’t decorations. They’re clues. They’re telling you what happened, or what might be happening, or what can’t be true. Evidence means they should support a claim, not just exist on the page.”

He frowned in concentration.

She kept going. “If your result says something impossible, don’t just move on because the algebra looked clean. Ask what the number is trying to tell you. That’s where understanding starts.”

His face changed almost immediately—not into mastery, but into recognition.

“Oh,” he said. “So the number is part of the argument.”

“Exactly.”

That one word landed with enough force that he grinned, thanked her twice, and jogged off before he forgot it.

Malik had watched the exchange from a few feet away. Not intrusively. Admiringly.

There was something powerful about seeing Nia in those moments. She did not just know mathematics; she opened it for other people. She treated understanding like a door worth holding. And in the FAMU Mathematics Department, where mentorship lived not only in offices but in hallways, bleachers, whiteboards, and library corners, that ability mattered.

By Sunday, the week narrowed again.

Homework was due. Calendars needed resetting. The noise of the game had faded into memory, and the quieter discipline of planning returned.

Malik sat at a table in the student center with his internship and career checklist open beside his course planner. Weeks earlier, he might have treated long-term career preparation as something reserved for a calmer season, a later version of himself, a mythical future student who would one day have everything under control.

Now he was learning that progress came from smaller acts than that.

Update the resume bullet.
Mark the application date.
Write down the professor whose recommendation might matter later.
Notice the opportunity before it passes.

Nia arrived carrying notes from a student organization planning meeting and dropped into the chair across from him with the deep exhale of someone who had been useful in six different directions all day.

“How bad?” Malik asked.

She tilted her head. “Bad in the sense that everybody has ideas. Good in the sense that some of them are actually excellent.”

He nodded. “That sounds right.”

She opened her notebook and showed him a page full of event planning notes, deadlines, names, and possible collaboration ideas. “I’m trying to be more intentional,” she said. “Not just saying yes to everything because it sounds good in the moment.”

Malik leaned back and gave her a thoughtful look. “You know what’s wild?”

“What?”

“You’re getting better at structure, and I’m getting better at flexibility.”

Nia smiled slowly. “So basically we’re both stealing from each other.”

“Academically,” Malik said.

“Of course,” she replied, deadpan.

Monday brought a new calculus problem set and, with it, the familiar hum of pressure gathering again.

The library deep-work block that afternoon felt different from earlier weeks. Not easier, exactly. But more honest. Malik worked for two uninterrupted hours, timer on, phone away, mistakes visible. Nia led a whiteboard study circle later that evening where she gently but firmly kept the group from collapsing into vague confidence.

“Don’t just say that makes sense,” she told one student. “Show me where it makes sense.”

Malik, standing off to the side, almost laughed at how much she sounded like Dr. Brooks.

At some point during the session, one of the quieter students finally stepped up to the board. He hesitated, marker hovering, and admitted he was not sure whether his setup was right.

“Good,” Nia said immediately.

He blinked. “Good?”

“Good. That means we can actually improve something real.”

The room loosened after that.

That was the department at its best: precision without cruelty, standards without humiliation, correction without shame.

On Tuesday, the men’s basketball game against Edward Waters brought another burst of campus energy, smaller than football but sharp and exciting in its own way. Malik and Nia did not stay long, but they stayed long enough to feel it—the noise, the school spirit, the sense that student life at FAMU was not a distraction from becoming, but part of it.

Even so, both of them carried unfinished work in the back of their minds.

The quiz window was approaching.

By Wednesday, the air around that fact had changed. Students spoke a little faster. Chairs in office hours filled more quickly. Questions became more specific.

Malik arrived with targeted notes instead of vague anxiety.
Nia helped another peer untangle a difficult concept without taking over the work.
Dr. Okafor reminded them again to visualize before calculating, to understand before performing.

And this time, the advice did not float above their lives as abstract wisdom. It met them in the exact places they needed it.

Late that evening, after the day had finally begun to settle, Malik and Nia walked across campus under a sky gone soft and dark at the edges. The buildings were quieter now. The paths were thinner with people. Their steps slowed naturally as they neared the residence hall turn.

“You feel it?” Nia asked.

“The quiz?”

“The whole thing.”

Malik was quiet for a second. “Yeah.”

Because he did feel it.

Not just the normal tension of another academic checkpoint. Something else too. A sense that they were entering a stretch of the semester where habits would start revealing what they were made of. Where small decisions would stop feeling small. Where the version of themselves they had been building in fragments might finally be tested in public.

Nia looked ahead, then back at him. “I think this is the part where everything gets a little more real.”

He nodded. “I think you’re right.”

She smiled, but it was not a fully relaxed smile. It had anticipation in it. Challenge. Readiness with a trace of nerves.

“Good,” she said. “I’d rather meet the real version than the imaginary scary one.”

Malik laughed softly. “That might be the smartest thing anybody’s said all week.”

They stopped where the path divided.

For a brief second neither of them moved.

In the distance, somewhere beyond the quiet of the walkway, campus life kept breathing—someone laughing from a dorm window, a car easing past, the low hum of a place full of futures still being assembled.

Malik adjusted the strap on his backpack. “Tomorrow’s going to be something.”

Nia’s eyes brightened in that way they did when she was nervous and determined at the same time. “Yeah,” she said. “And I think we’re finally the kind of students who can answer it.”

That was not certainty.
It was better than certainty.
It was earned belief.

And as they turned toward their separate buildings, carrying the relief of one resolved tension and the rising edge of another, the week closed on the kind of excitement that does not come from comfort.

It comes from knowing the next challenge is near—and knowing, for the first time, that you just might be ready when it arrives.
