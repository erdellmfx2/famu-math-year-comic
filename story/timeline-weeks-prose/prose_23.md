# Week Twenty-Three: The First Return

The momentum that had been building quietly through winter break finally found somewhere to land.

By the time January 2 gave way to the first full weekend of the new year, Malik and Nia were no longer standing at the threshold of spring and wondering what it might ask of them. Week Twenty-Two had already done the emotional work of turning vague anticipation into intention. What Week Twenty-Three brought was the first real test of whether that intention could survive contact with a living semester.

And almost immediately, FAMU gave them the answer it always gave students at the start of a term:

welcome back—now move.

The week began in the last soft light of winter break, but even that softness had changed. It was no longer the drifting, unstructured quiet of late December. It had a leaner edge now, as if everyone could feel campus preparing to inhale. The return of classes sat just ahead of them, visible enough to shape their choices before it had fully arrived.

On Friday, January 2, Malik spent part of the day polishing his resume again, but this time the task felt less like self-presentation and more like alignment. He sat at a table with his laptop open, a notebook beside him, and a list of internship ideas that looked both exciting and intimidating in the same way serious futures often do. He adjusted bullet points, revised phrasing, and kept hearing Dr. Price’s voice in the back of his head: **state assumptions before modeling**.

The advice had come from a mathematical frame, but Malik had started applying it to nearly everything.

What assumptions was he making about himself?

That he had to look finished before he could pursue opportunity?

That only polished students deserved to aim high?

That uncertainty on a resume meant weakness rather than room to grow?

He sat back and rubbed his forehead, then crossed out a line he had written too stiffly and replaced it with something clearer, more honest, more grounded in what he had actually done. The shift was small on the page, but meaningful in his chest. He was starting to understand that professionalism did not mean pretending to be complete. It meant representing real growth responsibly.

Later that afternoon, he sent Nia a screenshot of one section.

**Too formal or decent?**

Her reply came a minute later.

**Decent. Very Malik. Efficient. Slightly terrifying. Add one line that sounds like an actual person wrote it.**

He smiled despite himself.

Across town, Nia spent the day with her journal open on the bed beside her, pausing every few minutes to write and then stare into space. Her reflections were no longer just emotional snapshots. They were turning into a map. She wrote about the fall semester with more tenderness than frustration now. Mistakes that had once felt defining had become instructional. She could name exactly where she had overextended herself, exactly where she had hidden fatigue behind enthusiasm, exactly where the support systems at FAMU had caught her before small problems became large ones.

She thought about professors who challenged students without making them feel small. She thought about the FAMU Mathematics Department culture that treated rigor as a communal expectation rather than a gatekeeping performance. She thought about study groups, whiteboards, hallways, office hours, student organization conversations, and the subtle but constant message that students were not just there to pass classes. They were there to become thinkers, mentors, researchers, leaders.

That thought carried its own kind of pressure.

But this year, pressure was beginning to feel less like a threat and more like a calling.

She wrote one line in her journal and circled it twice:

**If spring is harder, let me be steadier—not louder.**

On Saturday, January 3, the campus atmosphere remained technically on break, but school pride pushed through anyway. The men’s basketball team was playing at Bethune-Cookman, and the game gave the day a pulse that neither Malik nor Nia could ignore. Messages moved through group chats. Posts went up. Students checked in with each other the way they always did when FAMU spirit had a clear object to rally around.

Malik used the day for rest and reset routines. He cleaned, stretched, reorganized his backpack, and laid out materials for the coming week with an intentional calm that would have been impossible for him during the first semester. Back then, preparation often had panic hidden inside it. Now it felt more disciplined. More chosen.

When Nia called him in the evening, the sound of game reactions flickered in the background from both ends.

“You ready?” she asked.

“For class?”

“For all of it,” she said. “Class. People. Syllabi. The first week when everybody acts like this semester is going to be the semester they finally become mythical beings.”

Malik laughed. “I am prepared to become a moderately improved mortal.”

“That might actually be the healthiest goal on campus.”

She had spent the day previewing a few next topics lightly, just enough to keep the coming work from feeling like a cold shock. Dr. Brooks’s old reminder—**show your reasoning clearly**—kept looping in her mind. The more she thought about it, the more she realized that spring would demand clarity not just on paper, but in how she lived. If she wanted to protect her energy, invest in meaningful work, and stay open to student life opportunities without scattering herself, then she needed reasoning she could actually show. To herself. To friends. To the people who would ask her to do one more thing because she was capable.

Sunday, January 4, carried one last deep breath before the semester truly resumed.

Malik spent time with family and did a little light review, careful not to turn it into a four-hour performance of anxiety. He worked through a few problems slowly, paying close attention to setup. Dr. Delgado’s voice returned with clean precision: **clean setup before solving**. He noticed how much his patience had changed. In the fall, he often wanted to rush to the middle of a problem just to prove he still knew how to work. Now he was more willing to begin well.

Nia gave herself a creative reset and reading time, but even that quiet felt charged. She made tea, read for a while, then drifted into thoughts about the semester ahead. She could feel the unresolved energy from the prior week finally narrowing into something concrete. There would be no dramatic cinematic countdown. No giant speech. Just Monday. The first day back. The ordinary and powerful fact of beginning again.

When Monday, January 5, arrived, FAMU shifted in an instant.

Spring semester classes began, and the entire campus seemed to regain its full voice at once.

Walkways filled. Doors opened and closed in steady rhythm. Conversations restarted in bursts of reunion and complaint and laughter. Backpacks, coffee cups, hurried greetings, updated hairstyles, fresh notebooks, old nerves—everything that made the beginning of a semester feel like its own season returned at once.

For Malik and Nia, the first day did not feel like a repeat of August.

It felt sharper.

They met outside before heading toward class, both carrying that special first-day alertness students pretend not to have.

“You look focused,” Nia said.

“You look like you already joined three committees since breakfast.”

“I rejected two opportunities before noon,” she said with mock pride.

He stopped and looked at her. “That’s growth.”

“That’s survival.”

The joke sat on top of something real.

There was a calculus problem set assigned almost immediately, as if the semester wanted to make clear that its expectations were active from the first hour. Malik responded the way he had promised himself he would: timer out, practice drills, no melodrama. He treated the assignment as a signal, not an attack. Meanwhile, Nia rewrote fresh notes into a concept map, following Dr. Okafor’s guidance to **visualize the concept first**. Where Malik found steadiness in timed repetition, Nia found it in structure and connection. The difference in their methods no longer felt like a problem to solve. It felt like evidence of a partnership that had matured.

The FAMU Mathematics Department seemed to reward exactly that kind of growth. In classrooms and hallways alike, the tone was serious but not cold. Students were expected to think, to keep up, to ask better questions, to develop habits worthy of the discipline. At the same time, there was still an unmistakable sense of belonging threaded through the atmosphere. This was not a place where mathematical promise had to become lonely to become excellent. Students challenged one another, compared notes, shared space, and moved through the week with the knowledge that rigor and community could coexist.

That culture mattered even more on Tuesday, January 6, when the week’s pressure began to show its true face.

A midterm-style exam window appeared early enough to jolt everyone.

Even students who had promised themselves a strong start could feel the emotional whiplash. The semester had barely begun, and already the pace was asking for performance. Hallway conversations changed. Voices lowered. Plans became more precise.

Malik did something he might not have done in the fall: he went to office hours quickly, before confusion could gather pride around it.

Dr. Bennett was there, measured as always, and Malik arrived with targeted questions already marked.

“I don’t think I’m lost,” Malik said, sitting down. “But I do think there are places where I’m moving faster than I’m actually understanding.”

Dr. Bennett nodded, almost as if that sentence itself was progress. “Good. That means you noticed it early.”

They worked through the questions one by one. Each time Malik tried to rush to a result, Dr. Bennett redirected him toward interpretation.

“Don’t just calculate,” the professor said. “Treat the numbers as evidence. What are they telling you? What changes if your assumptions change?”

By the time Malik left, he was not magically free of difficulty. But he was steadier. More precise. Better aligned with the actual work.

That same day, Nia found herself helping a peer with a difficult concept after class. It started casually, just two people lingering with open notebooks, but soon a few more students joined. Nia stood at the board and talked through the idea with the kind of warmth and clarity that made hard material feel approachable without making it feel easy.

“Wait,” one student said, pointing. “So the reason this step works is not just because it’s the next algebra move. It’s because the structure already told us what kind of move was legal?”

Nia lit up. “Exactly. Yes. That’s the whole thing.”

In moments like that, the undergraduate culture around mathematics at FAMU became visible in a way no brochure could fully capture. Students were not only absorbing content. They were learning how to think together. How to explain. How to ask. How to grow into scholarly habits in public.

Wednesday, January 7, intensified the rhythm again with a quiz or concept-check window layered on top of the ongoing exam pressure. It would have been easy for the day to collapse into pure tension, but by now Malik and Nia had systems.

Malik updated his internship and career checklist in a gap between responsibilities, grounding himself in the long view so that one stressful week would not shrink his whole perspective. Nia joined a student organization planning meeting, careful this time to observe her energy honestly rather than volunteering out of reflex. She still loved student life. She still wanted to contribute. But now contribution meant discernment too.

Afterward they met briefly, both tired in that sharp, bright midweek way.

“How bad is your brain?” Nia asked.

“Functional,” Malik said. “Yours?”

“Conceptually rich. Operationally fragile.”

He laughed. “That should be on a T-shirt for the department.”

Then his expression shifted. “You okay, though?”

She nodded. “Yeah. Actually yeah. I’m stretched, but not spinning. That’s new.”

He understood exactly why that mattered.

Thursday, January 8, brought one more midterm-style exam day and with it the week’s final proving ground. By now the semester had fully reintroduced itself. There would be no gentle easing in. No symbolic grace period. Spring wanted maturity early.

Malik answered with a two-hour deep-work block in the library, the kind of disciplined session that turned noise into sequence. He worked silently, cleanly, showing every step, resisting the urge to perform speed when what he needed was clarity.

Not far away, Nia led a whiteboard study circle, guiding students through concepts with a confidence that was no longer built on saying yes to everything. This yes had shape. Purpose. Boundaries. She was not trying to be everywhere. She was choosing where she could be most useful.

The room held that particular academic electricity that comic scenes love because real campuses create it so often: dry-erase markers squeaking, someone snapping their fingers when an idea finally lands, a half-erased graph lingering in the corner, backpacks on the floor, students leaning forward because they care more than they want to admit. If there was a single image that captured why this story belonged at FAMU, it might have been that room. Not because the work was easy, but because the atmosphere made striving feel shared.

When the session ended, Nia leaned against the board for a second and exhaled.

Malik appeared in the doorway a few minutes later.

“You look like the week tried you,” he said.

“It did,” she answered. “But I think I tried it back.”

He grinned. “That’s a strong line.”

“It’s a true line.”

They walked out into the evening with the campus around them still alive in the steady way only a real academic week can produce. The first return of spring had come fast. It had come with assignments, pressure, office hours, concept checks, leadership moments, and the quiet demand that both of them live up to the growth they had claimed over break.

And somehow, despite the fatigue settling into their shoulders, neither of them felt discouraged.

They felt confirmed.

Week Twenty-Three resolved the suspense that Week Twenty-Two had carried forward. The question had been whether their winter-break clarity would hold once FAMU’s pace returned.

It did.

Not perfectly. Not effortlessly. But truly.

They had not outgrown difficulty. They had grown into better ways of meeting it.

That resolution, though, did not end the tension of the story. It transformed it.

Because now a new question rose in its place.

If they could begin the semester this strongly—if Malik could seek help early, if Nia could lead without overextending, if both of them could move through the FAMU Mathematics Department with more steadiness, more purpose, more visible belonging—then what else might this spring ask them to become?

The answer waited just beyond the week’s final pages.

The first return had gone well.

Which only made the rising challenges ahead feel more real, more promising, and more dangerous in the best possible way.

Spring was no longer approaching.

It was here.

And now that Malik and Nia had met it standing, the year seemed ready to open wider than either of them had imagined.
