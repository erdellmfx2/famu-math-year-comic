# Week 43 Prose — May 22 to May 28, 2026

By the time Friday settled over Florida A&M University, the campus had entered that distinct early-summer rhythm that felt quieter than the regular academic year but never empty. The sidewalks still carried motion. Doors still opened and closed with purpose. Study rooms still filled with students trying to make good on promises they had made to themselves in harder months. Summer had not erased ambition. If anything, it had stripped away the noise and left the intention behind.

Malik felt that as soon as he crossed the open walkway toward the mathematics building with his notebook tucked under one arm and three carefully folded pages of questions in his backpack. During the fall, he would have told himself that needing office hours meant he had not prepared enough. Now he knew better. At FAMU, he had learned that preparation was not the absence of confusion. Preparation was knowing how to bring your confusion somewhere useful.

Inside, the building felt cool and still, the air carrying a faint blend of dry-erase marker, old paper, and polished tile. A few students sat outside classrooms reviewing notes or scrolling through assignments, each wearing that same concentrated expression of someone trying to make summer count. Malik spotted Dr. Price at the end of the hall speaking with another student, one hand lightly raised as if balancing an invisible equation in the air.

When it was his turn, Malik spread his notes across the desk and exhaled. “I think I understand the steps,” he said, “but when I try to set up the model myself, I start second-guessing everything.”

Dr. Price gave him the calm, knowing look Malik had come to recognize. “Then start before the algebra,” she said. “Tell me what you are assuming. If the assumptions are clear, the model has somewhere honest to begin.”

That sentence landed harder than Malik expected. He looked back at his page. Assumptions. Conditions. What the problem was really asking him to believe before it asked him to solve. He began again, slower this time, saying each premise out loud. Dr. Price nodded only when needed, letting him hear himself reason his way into clarity.

By the end of the session, the math itself no longer felt like a wall. It felt like a structure he could enter. He left office hours with more than corrected work. He left with a steadier posture, as though one more invisible piece of college had finally clicked into place.

Outside, under a bright afternoon sky, he texted Nia.

**Malik:** Office hours was actually useful-useful today.

**Nia:** “Actually useful-useful” is high praise from you.

**Malik:** Dr. Price said state assumptions before modeling.

**Nia:** That’s not just math advice. That’s life advice.

Malik smiled despite himself. That was Nia’s gift. She could take a professor’s line and make it sound like a chapter title.

Later that day, they met in a shaded campus courtyard where summer light filtered through the trees and stretched soft patterns across the pavement. Nia had just finished helping a classmate work through a difficult concept from an earlier assignment. She looked energized rather than drained, which Malik had learned to notice. Earlier in the year she would give too much of herself away all at once. Now she had begun practicing a different kind of generosity—focused, bounded, sustainable.

“How’d it go?” she asked.

Malik sat beside her and opened his notebook. “Better than I expected. Worse than I wanted.”

Nia laughed. “That sounds like growth.”

He showed her where Dr. Price had circled the places he had rushed into procedure without naming the setup. Nia leaned in, studying the page seriously.

“The wild part,” she said, “is that this is exactly what happens when people get stuck in group work too. Everyone wants to jump into solving before anyone agrees on what the problem is.”

“Now you’re turning office hours into life advice too.”

“I learned from the best.”

They sat there a while longer, talking through classes, summer pacing, and the strange relief of a week that was not trying to break them. It was a steady week. No dramatic collision of deadlines. No single moment demanding heroics. But both of them had begun to understand that not every meaningful week announced itself with crisis. Some weeks were important because they gave you space to prove that your new habits were real.

Saturday carried that quieter lesson forward. Malik spent part of the morning updating his internship and career checklist at a library table near the windows, where sunlight poured across stacks of papers and made even ordinary planning feel important. His list had grown more thoughtful over time. It no longer said vague things like “get experience” or “improve résumé.” Now it held specific actions: contact faculty mentor about research opportunities, revise personal statement, identify actuarial internship deadlines, attend career prep sessions, ask stronger questions about data work and graduate pathways.

Across campus, Nia joined a student organization planning meeting that might have seemed small to an outsider but mattered deeply to her. The room buzzed with the particular energy of students trying to build something good together—community events, outreach ideas, ways to make incoming students feel less alone than they had felt when they first arrived. Nia listened, contributed, and—most importantly—did not overpromise. She offered clear help where she could truly follow through. Growth had made her more generous, not less. She was learning that leadership was not saying yes to everything. Leadership was helping the right things happen well.

When she later told Malik about the meeting, she repeated something Dr. Brooks had said in class before: “Show your reasoning clearly.” She laughed at how often professor advice seemed to follow them outside the classroom.

“But it fits,” she said. “Even in planning. If people can see how you got to an idea, they trust the work more.”

Sunday brought the weekly planning reset along with a homework deadline, and FAMU’s library became its own kind of sanctuary. Students moved with low-voiced determination between floors, whiteboards, tables, and printers. Malik settled into a deep-work block with the discipline of someone who had learned how much peace there was in starting on time. Nia, meanwhile, led a whiteboard study circle that drew a handful of students into a real conversation instead of a performative one. She asked people to explain what they thought first before anyone rushed to clean answers. When someone stumbled, she did not rescue them too quickly. She gave them room to think in public.

That had become part of the culture growing around them, one FAMU had nurtured all year through professors, peers, office hours, study halls, and a department that treated growth as something visible and collective. The mathematics department did not make excellence feel private. It made excellence feel shared. Students were expected to work hard, yes, but they were also taught to bring others with them.

On Memorial Day, the campus atmosphere shifted into something more reflective. The holiday softened the tempo without erasing it. Family calls lasted longer. The walkways seemed to invite slower steps. Malik spent part of the day drilling calculus problems with a timer, testing both speed and calm, while Nia rewrote a thick cluster of notes into a clean concept map that turned scattered ideas into something elegant and navigable.

They met that evening near a campus green where the light had begun to turn gold.

“Dr. Okafor said to visualize the concept first,” Nia said, tapping her notebook. “So I did. And honestly? The page looks less scary now.”

Malik glanced at her concept map. “You make math look like architecture.”

“And you make it look like logistics.”

“Both are useful.”

“Exactly.”

The exchange was simple, but it carried the whole history of their year inside it. They had spent months learning that their differences were not problems to eliminate. They were strengths to coordinate.

Tuesday and Wednesday continued the steady pattern. Malik attended another office-hours visit, this time more confident and more targeted. He no longer arrived hoping a professor would somehow fix a vague unease. He arrived with exact questions. Nia helped a peer untangle a concept that had been causing quiet panic, and she did it with patience instead of urgency, showing the student not only the answer but the path. On Wednesday, she joined another planning meeting while Malik returned to his internship checklist, refining it with a seriousness that made the future feel less abstract.

That same day, a concept-check window opened, and even though it was not the most dramatic academic event of the term, it introduced a current of tension that both of them felt. Summer classes had a way of making small assessments feel bigger because there was less time to recover, less room to drift. The week had been kind, but kindness in college could turn quickly if you mistook it for permission to relax completely.

Malik knew that. Nia knew that too.

Thursday evening, they returned to the library for one last study session before the week closed. Outside, the campus was beautiful in that calm late-day way that made possibility feel almost visible. Students crossed the quad in groups. Someone laughed too loudly outside the doors and then hushed themselves. A faculty member paused to greet two students by name. In another corner of the building, a flyer about undergraduate research remained pinned to a board, a small reminder that the life of the mind here was not limited to assignments. It extended outward—to mentorship, inquiry, discovery, and the belief that students at FAMU could contribute something meaningful to the world.

Nia noticed Malik staring at the flyer.

“You thinking about it?” she asked.

“Maybe more seriously than before.”

“You should.”

He looked down at his checklist. “I’m trying not to get ahead of myself.”

Nia leaned back in her chair. “That’s fine. Just don’t stay behind yourself either.”

Malik shook his head, smiling. “You really do talk like chapter titles.”

“And you’re going to put that on a T-shirt one day.”

They worked until the library lights seemed brighter than the sky beyond the windows. The material came more cleanly now, not because it had become easy, but because they had become better. Better at asking. Better at pacing. Better at seeing warning signs before they became problems. Better at using the ecosystem around them—the department, the professors, the study spaces, the culture of mentorship, the peer networks that had turned strangers into collaborators.

When they finally packed up, the week seemed ready to fold itself away. But just before they left, Nia paused at the research flyer again.

“There’s a faculty information session next week,” she said. “Undergraduate opportunities. Math modeling, data, maybe even summer projects.”

Malik adjusted the strap on his backpack. “Next week?”

“Mm-hmm. I heard two seniors talking about it after the org meeting. Sounds real.”

He looked at her. She looked back with that familiar spark—the one that usually meant a new challenge was about to become a new turning point.

For a second neither of them spoke. The quiet between them was not empty. It was charged.

A few months earlier, the idea of adding one more ambition to their already demanding lives might have felt reckless. Now it felt like possibility. Not because the path ahead was guaranteed, but because they had earned the right to imagine themselves on it.

Malik glanced once more at his revised checklist, now suddenly incomplete in a new and exciting way.

“So,” he said, “I guess I need another category.”

Nia grinned. “Good. I was hoping this week would end with homework for your future self.”

They stepped out of the library into the warm Tallahassee evening, carrying notebooks, plans, and the subtle electricity of something beginning before it had even been fully named. Behind them, the mathematics building and the library stood like steady witnesses to the work they had already done. Ahead of them, another week waited—with more questions, more responsibility, and maybe, if they were bold enough to walk toward it, the first real glimpse of the future they had been building all year.

And for the first time, that future did not feel far away.
