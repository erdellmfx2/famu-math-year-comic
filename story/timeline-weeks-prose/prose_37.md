# Week Thirty-Seven: The Weight They Could Carry

The promise at the end of the previous week did not stay abstract for long.

By the time Thursday, April 10 arrived, the question was no longer whether Malik and Nia were becoming the kind of students other people leaned on. That question had already been answered. The new question was harder, sharper, and far more revealing:

Could they carry that trust without losing themselves?

Spring at FAMU had a way of making every day feel full before the day had even properly begun. Athletics gave the campus one layer of energy, academics gave it another, and somewhere between those forces students learned whether they would be pushed around by the semester or learn to move with purpose inside it. That week, baseball was on the road at Jackson State, softball was battling Alabama State, and the mathematics department kept moving with the quiet certainty of people who understood that April did not reward drifting.

Thursday morning opened warm and bright, with the kind of sunlight that made the red brick and green spaces around campus seem almost too vivid to be ordinary. Students crossed paths carrying laptops, folders, drinks, deadlines, and fragments of conversations about quizzes, travel schedules, group chats, and weekend plans. The whole campus felt awake.

Malik reached the library early for a two-hour deep-work block. He had learned by then that deep work did not happen by accident. It had to be claimed. Protected. Built on purpose.

He spread out his notebook, his problem set, and the sheet where he had begun keeping what he privately called his recovery rules: slow the first line down, state assumptions, clean setup before solving, do not confuse panic with urgency.

He had barely gotten through the first problem when someone approached his table.

“Hey,” the student said carefully, “are you busy-busy or just studying busy?”

Malik looked up and almost smiled.

There was a difference.

“Depends,” he said. “What happened?”

The student held up a page. “I think I’m mixing up the idea and the procedure again.”

Malik gestured toward the empty chair beside him. “Sit down for five minutes.”

The student sat immediately, relief written all over his face.

Malik scanned the page. The work was not careless. That was the first thing he noticed. It was anxious. The lines moved too quickly, like someone trying to outrun confusion.

He tapped the setup. “You’re not starting in the wrong place because you can’t do this,” he said. “You’re starting in the wrong place because you’re trying to get to the answer before the problem tells you what it is.”

The student stared at him. “That is exactly what it feels like.”

Malik nodded. “Yeah. I know.”

For the next few minutes he walked him through the structure instead of the shortcut. He showed him how to slow the opening, identify what was known, and force the page to become evidence rather than a guessing contest. It was the same discipline FAMU professors had been pressing into him all year, and now it passed through him with surprising naturalness.

When the student left, he said, “Thanks, man. You explain it like it’s fixable.”

Malik sat with that sentence for a while after the student was gone.

Like it’s fixable.

There was something powerful in that. Not just mathematically, but humanly. That was part of what the FAMU Mathematics Department did when it was operating at its best: it treated difficulty as something to work through, not proof that someone did not belong.

Later that afternoon Nia led a whiteboard study circle, and the room filled faster than usual. She noticed it right away. More first-years than normal. A few students from classes adjacent to hers. Even one student who usually stayed near the door as if ready to leave at the first sign of embarrassment.

Nia uncapped the marker and wrote the week’s big idea across the board in large, clear letters.

“Before we solve anything,” she said, “we’re going to make the concept visible.”

A student near the front laughed lightly. “You always say that.”

“Because it keeps being true,” Nia replied.

A few people smiled, and the room relaxed.

She began drawing the relationships between the ideas rather than listing steps. Curves, arrows, labels, connections. The board turned into a map of thought. As she talked, she kept asking questions instead of delivering a performance.

“What changes here?”

“What stays the same?”

“What would this look like if you had to explain it to someone who missed class?”

The student by the door finally raised his hand.

“I get lost,” he admitted, “when it stops looking like the homework and starts looking unfamiliar.”

Nia nodded immediately. “Good. That’s an important problem.”

He blinked. “Good?”

“Yes,” she said. “Because now we’re naming the actual issue. Not ‘I’m bad at math.’ The issue is transfer. That’s workable.”

Something changed in the room after that. Several students straightened. Someone else admitted they had the same problem. Another said timed quizzes made the whole page feel unfamiliar even when the concept wasn’t. What might once have become a quiet spiral of individual shame turned into a shared academic conversation.

That was leadership too, Nia realized. Not just having answers, but creating a space where confusion could be named without humiliation.

Friday, April 11, carried sports energy across campus again as baseball remained at Jackson State and softball stayed in action against Alabama State. The atmosphere around FAMU held that familiar spring contradiction: festive at the edges, demanding at the center.

Malik spent part of the day doing timed calculus drills. The timer still elevated his pulse, but no longer in the same destructive way. He had learned how to keep speed from becoming recklessness. Each time he finished a problem, he checked not only whether the answer was right but whether the process would survive pressure.

Nia, meanwhile, rewrote her notes into a concept map so detailed it looked almost architectural. She had discovered that rewriting notes this way did more than help her remember. It helped her recognize where her understanding still had weak joints.

That afternoon the two of them met outside after class, settling onto a bench under a patch of spring shade while students streamed past in FAMU colors, game shirts, and department hoodies.

“You look like you’re arguing with your notes,” Malik said, nodding at her notebook.

“I was,” Nia answered. “They were being vague.”

He laughed. “Did you win?”

“I imposed order.”

“That sounds more like you.”

She glanced at him. “And you?”

He held up his timer. “I’m trying not to let this thing turn me into a worse version of myself.”

Nia smiled. “That might be one of the most honest academic statements anybody has ever made.”

Then her expression softened. “But you’re doing better. I can tell.”

Malik looked out across campus before replying. “I think I finally believe pressure doesn’t have to erase what I know.”

“That,” Nia said, “is a serious shift.”

It was. And they both knew it.

Saturday, April 12, brought homework due, a weekly planning reset, and baseball still at Jackson State. It was the kind of day that could quietly determine the rest of the week if handled well.

Malik went to office hours with targeted questions. He was no longer showing up just to ask what went wrong. He was showing up with evidence. Dr. Price looked over his page and listened as he explained one modeling issue that had bothered him.

When he finished, Dr. Price said, “State assumptions before modeling. Always. If your assumptions are shaky, a polished answer only hides the weakness.”

Malik nodded. “I’ve started hearing that in my head before I even start.”

“Good,” Dr. Price said. “That means you’re developing judgment, not just memory.”

That comment stayed with him long after office hours ended.

Across campus, Nia spent part of the afternoon helping a peer with a difficult concept. The student was frustrated enough to joke about changing majors, but the joke had too much force under it to be harmless. Nia noticed.

“Okay,” she said, pulling out a fresh sheet of paper. “Forget the whole chapter for a second. Tell me the smallest piece you do trust.”

The student frowned, thought, and answered.

“Good,” Nia said. “Now we build from there.”

They worked slowly, layer by layer, until the student’s face changed from defeated to focused.

When they finished, he said, “I thought I was behind everybody.”

Nia shook her head. “A lot of people look calm and are confused. Don’t compare your insides to other people’s outside performance.”

He laughed softly. “You should write that down somewhere.”

“Maybe after finals.”

But helping him also tested her. She could feel how easily one conversation could become three, how naturally she could spend an entire day pouring herself into everybody else’s problems. That was one of the central struggles of her year: not whether she cared, but whether she could care sustainably. So after that session ended, she stopped. She updated her planner, reset her own priorities, and let that count as enough.

Sunday, April 13, brought a new calculus problem set and the familiar Sunday rhythm of preparing before the week could take control. Malik updated his internship and career checklist at a table outside, writing down deadlines, possible summer pathways, and the competencies he was genuinely beginning to embody rather than merely admire from a distance.

Nearby, Nia joined a student organization planning meeting. This time she entered it differently than she might have months earlier. She was enthusiastic, yes, but bounded. Engaged, but intentional.

At one point discussion drifted in six directions at once, and she gently stepped in.

“What has to happen this week?” she asked. “Not eventually. This week.”

The room quieted. Pens moved. Priorities sharpened.

An upperclassman looked at her with open appreciation. “You always bring us back to the center.”

Nia smiled, but the comment affected her more than she showed. All year, she had feared that structure might make her seem less generous, less warm, less expansive. Instead, she was discovering that good structure protected what mattered.

Monday, April 14, arrived with another surge of campus motion. Baseball traveled to the University of North Florida. Softball was at Jacksonville University. Inside the mathematics building and across library floors, however, a different competition was underway: students against distraction, confusion, and late-semester fatigue.

Malik protected another two-hour deep-work block. Nia led another whiteboard study circle. By now their names carried a kind of informal academic credibility among classmates. Not because they were flawless, but because they were consistent.

During the study circle, one of the younger students said, “I feel like everybody else understands faster than I do.”

Before Nia could answer, another student shook her head. “That’s not true. They just start earlier now.”

Nia paused, almost smiling.

That answer told her as much about the culture forming around them as any professor’s praise could have. Students were beginning to teach each other healthier ways of thinking.

“That’s exactly right,” Nia said. “A lot of success looks like being honest early.”

Tuesday, April 15, brought the quiz and concept-check window. The pressure was real, but unlike earlier in the year, it did not feel shapeless. Malik practiced calculus drills with a timer and entered the assessment with steadier breathing than he would have believed possible in the fall. Nia rewrote her notes into one last clean concept map and walked in carrying not just information but perspective.

Afterward they found each other outside the building almost immediately.

“Well?” Nia asked.

Malik let out a breath. “I didn’t rush the first problem.”

She grinned. “Look at you becoming emotionally mature in mathematics.”

He laughed. “I hate how accurate that is.”

“And the rest?”

“Not easy,” he said. “But I stayed with it.”

Nia nodded. “Same. There was one question that tried to scare me into overthinking.”

“Tried?”

“Tried,” she repeated.

There it was again: not perfection, but poise.

Wednesday, April 16, ended the week without spectacle but not without meaning. Malik attended office hours again for targeted questions, and Nia helped another peer with a difficult concept. Dr. Bennett, in a brief conversation that afternoon, reminded Malik to interpret numbers as evidence, not verdicts. That sentence struck deeper than it first appeared. Numbers could inform. They could warn. They could guide. But if handled badly, they could also become emotional judgments people pinned to themselves.

Late that evening Malik and Nia met near the mathematics building as the day gave way to the kind of soft dark that made campus feel reflective rather than crowded. The noise had thinned. The pace had loosened. Windows still glowed in a few rooms where students were finishing work.

For a while they just walked.

“I’ve been thinking,” Malik said at last, “about what that student told me on Thursday.”

“The one who said you explained it like it was fixable?”

He nodded. “Yeah.”

Nia looked ahead. “That was a big sentence.”

“It was.” He adjusted the strap on his bag. “And I think that’s what this week was about. Not just carrying pressure. Carrying it in a way that helps other people believe they can carry theirs too.”

Nia was quiet for a moment.

Then she said, “I think that’s part of what FAMU does when we’re at our best. We don’t just prove we can do hard things. We make hard things feel possible for the next person.”

Malik glanced at her and smiled. “That sounded like a department motto.”

“It should be.”

They kept walking past buildings that had held so much of their year: confusion, breakthroughs, office hours, laughter, low-grade panic, recovered confidence, and the slow construction of identity. Around them FAMU still felt full of motion even in the relative quiet—of mentors investing in students, of undergraduates learning how scholarship and community could reinforce each other, of a mathematics culture that was rigorous without asking anyone to become cold.

By the end of the week, the tension from Week Thirty-Six had found its answer. Yes, Malik and Nia could carry more. Yes, they could help others without immediately losing their own footing. Yes, their growth was becoming visible in the lives around them.

But April was not finished.

As they reached the edge of the walkway and slowed, both of them could feel another kind of pressure gathering just beyond the week they had survived: bigger decisions, end-of-semester consequences, and the approaching moment when habits would have to prove they were strong enough to hold under final strain.

Nia looked toward the math building one last time. “You know what I’m realizing?” she said.

“What?”

“This part of the semester doesn’t just test what you know.” She folded her arms lightly against the evening breeze. “It tests what kind of person your routines have been building.”

Malik let that sit between them.

Then he nodded. “Yeah,” he said. “And I think the next couple of weeks are going to show us the answer.”

That was where the week ended—not in fear, but in sharpened anticipation.

They had learned to carry weight.

Now spring was preparing to reveal just how far that strength could take them.
