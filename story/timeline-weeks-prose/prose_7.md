# Week Seven: The Verdict Week and the First Glimpse Beyond Survival

Friday, September 12th, arrived with a strange kind of sunlight over FAMU’s campus—bright and generous, but edged with the nervousness of unfinished business. The midterm-style exam window had closed the day before, yet nobody in the mathematics halls moved like the pressure was over. If anything, the tension had only changed shape. Last week had demanded performance. This week demanded interpretation. The papers were gone from their hands now. The work had been submitted, the proofs written, the assumptions stated, the logic defended. What remained was the waiting.

And waiting, Malik Baptiste was discovering, could be harder than solving.

He crossed the campus that morning with his backpack cinched tight and his tablet tucked beneath one arm, his stride purposeful even though his mind refused to settle. The red-brick paths still held the softness of early morning moisture. Students flowed around him in clusters—some laughing, some half-awake, some carrying the kind of studied calm that only made other people more anxious. Orange and green banners stirred lightly in the breeze. Somewhere near the student union, music drifted through the air, and for a moment the campus felt too alive, too vibrant, to accommodate the private unease he was carrying.

Inside Jackson Davis Hall, the mathematics department had its own atmosphere: quieter, sharper, full of doors half-open to offices where professors lived among chalkboards, journal articles, and the steady seriousness of ideas. Malik paused outside the office-hours sign-in sheet and glanced down at the questions he had prepared.

Not panic questions.
Not vague questions.
Targeted questions.

That alone felt like progress.

When he stepped into Dr. Okafor’s office, he saw at once why older students described certain faculty with a kind of reverence. The room was crowded without feeling cluttered—books stacked in orderly towers, a framed conference poster on one wall, a small potted plant thriving improbably near a window, and a whiteboard dense with curves, arrows, and annotations that looked less like notes and more like a captured conversation between ideas.

Dr. Okafor looked up and smiled. “Mr. Baptiste. You came prepared.”

Malik sat down, careful not to let that small approval show how much it mattered. “I’m trying to be.”

“Good. What are we working through today?”

Malik slid over a sheet of paper. “Not answers, exactly. More like… weak spots. Places where I can tell I understand the procedure but I’m not always seeing the concept fast enough.”

Dr. Okafor studied him for a brief moment, and there was something encouraging in that pause—as if the professor was not just evaluating the page, but recognizing growth in the person holding it.

“That,” he said, tapping the top of the sheet, “is already a better question than most first-years ask in September.”

He stood, moved to the whiteboard, and began sketching. “You are still trying to force every problem to introduce itself in symbolic language first. Sometimes mathematics speaks visually before it speaks formally. Visualize the concept first. Let the structure announce itself. Then write.”

Malik nodded, though his instinct was still to grip tightly to structure as if looseness might cost him precision. But he listened. He asked follow-up questions. He let the conversation move beyond right and wrong answers into something more useful: how to think when the path was not immediately visible.

By the time he left, his notes were fuller, cleaner, and more alive with meaning. He was no longer simply recording what a professor said. He was building a method.

Outside, near the shaded walkway between classes, Nia Reynolds was doing something that looked less formal but mattered just as much.

A first-year student named Keisha stood beside her with a notebook pressed to her chest and a frustrated crease between her eyebrows. Nia had noticed her earlier in class—noticed the too-long silence, the false nodding, the way some students tried to disappear rather than risk being exposed as confused.

“Okay,” Nia said, leaning against a bench and taking Keisha’s notebook gently. “Let’s forget the intimidating version for a second. Tell me what you think the idea is supposed to be doing.”

Keisha exhaled. “That’s the problem. I keep trying to memorize the steps, and then as soon as it looks different, I freeze.”

Nia’s expression softened with recognition. “Yeah. That means your brain doesn’t trust the concept yet. So we’re not going to start with steps.”

She turned to a blank page and drew a simple shape. Then another. Then arrows between them. Her hands moved quickly, but her voice stayed calm.

“Look,” she said. “If the professor writes it one way, that’s not the whole idea. That’s one doorway into it. The real question is what the math is trying to describe. Once you see that, the notation stops feeling like a trap.”

Keisha stared at the page. “You make it sound less scary.”

Nia laughed. “It’s still scary. That’s part of the charm.”

Keisha laughed too, and the tension broke.

This was becoming one of Nia’s quiet gifts at FAMU—not just understanding difficult material, but helping other people feel like they were allowed to understand it too. In another environment, such moments might have remained isolated acts of kindness. Here, inside the culture of a department that believed in growth, mentoring, and visible excellence, they became part of something bigger. Students watched each other rise. Faculty expected effort and rewarded seriousness. Community and rigor were not opposites. They were partners.

By the end of Friday, Malik and Nia met on the lawn with matching exhaustion and different kinds of satisfaction.

“How’d office hours go?” Nia asked.

Malik adjusted the strap of his backpack. “Useful. Actually useful. I think I’ve been trying to solve some problems before I really understand what kind of problem they are.”

Nia grinned. “Welcome to the human experience.”

“And you?”

“I helped Keisha stop being afraid of the notation.” Nia lifted one shoulder. “Which might also be me helping myself stop being afraid of my own messy notes.”

Malik smirked. “Your notes are not notes. They’re weather systems.”

“They are concept maps.”

“They are storms with arrows.”

She pointed at him. “And yet they work.”

He could not argue with that.

Saturday transformed the campus entirely.

By noon, the energy around FAMU had become impossible to ignore. Albany State was in town, and the football game had poured excitement into every visible corner of student life. The walkways gleamed with school colors. Music rolled across the grounds in waves. Friends called to one another from across the quad. Vendors, alumni, and students moved through the day with the familiar confidence of a campus that knew how to celebrate itself.

For Malik, game day had once felt like a distraction to manage. This year, or at least this week, he was beginning to understand something subtler: balance did not mean rejecting joy. It meant learning how to carry purpose through joy without losing either.

He sat at an outdoor table near the student center before the larger crowds fully took over, tablet open, refining his internship and career checklist. Resume bullets. Research interests. Long-term actuarial benchmarks. It was not glamorous work, but it steadied him. Every line he clarified reminded him that this first semester was not an isolated test of intelligence. It was a foundation.

Dr. Bennett’s voice echoed in his head as he revised a line describing his academic interests: *Interpret numbers as evidence.*

Evidence of skill.
Evidence of discipline.
Evidence of promise.

He looked up when Nia arrived carrying an iced drink and enough game-day enthusiasm for both of them.

“You are the only person on this campus who can make a football Saturday look like a board meeting,” she said.

“It’s not a board meeting,” Malik replied. “It’s a strategic adjustment window.”

“That is somehow worse.”

But she sat anyway.

Nia had just come from a student organization planning meeting, one of several spaces where her energy made immediate sense. She was the kind of person who could care deeply about mathematics and still move naturally through the social ecosystem of campus life. She was learning, however, that leadership could not mean saying yes to every meeting, every request, every possibility. Even in her excitement, there was more selectivity now. More intention.

“How was the meeting?” Malik asked.

“Good,” she said. “Productive. Not chaotic. Which honestly feels like a miracle.” She paused. “They want to do more with freshman involvement this year. More study support, more visible encouragement, more reasons for people not to feel isolated in hard classes.”

“That sounds like you.”

She looked at him. “What’s that supposed to mean?”

“It means,” Malik said evenly, “you make difficult things feel possible. That seems useful.”

The compliment landed with enough sincerity that she did not joke it away.

By late afternoon, the game-day atmosphere had fully swelled. They let themselves be part of it—walking through the crowd, catching pieces of conversation, hearing the pulse of the band, feeling the bright collective pride that made FAMU feel larger than any one classroom or any one week’s stress. Yet even as they smiled and moved with the current of campus life, an undercurrent remained between them.

Grades were still coming.
And beyond grades, that research flyer still waited.

Sunday restored a quieter rhythm.

Homework was due. Laundry hummed in dorm basements. The campus, while not silent, felt more honest. Less performance, more preparation.

In the library, Malik disappeared into a two-hour deep-work block with the seriousness of someone honoring a pact with himself. He chose a table far from the entrance, arranged his materials with geometric neatness, wrote the date at the top of the page, and began. No multitasking. No checking notifications. No rushing because the first part felt easy.

Deep work, he was learning, was not just a study habit. It was a form of self-respect.

Across the building, Nia led a whiteboard study circle that was growing into a real community. Not a random cluster of stressed students. A functioning academic circle. She opened the session by writing one sentence in large letters:

**Explain before solve.**

“Anybody can follow steps they just saw ten seconds ago,” she told the group. “What we need is transfer. If the problem changes shape, your brain can’t panic. So say what’s happening first.”

David was there again. Keisha too. This time they spoke sooner. Asked more. Laughed even. The room carried that rare kind of momentum where nobody wanted to look foolish because everyone was too busy trying to improve.

When Malik joined the group later, he noticed something immediately.

“They’re sharper,” he said quietly to Nia while the others packed up.

She smiled, tired but proud. “So are we.”

Monday brought another calculus problem set, and with it the familiar threat of overload. But this time the strain did not feel directionless. Malik used a timer for his drills, not to race, but to study how pressure changed his thinking. Nia rewrote a full lecture’s worth of notes into a concept map so clean it almost looked like art. Dr. Brooks reminded the class to show their reasoning clearly, and for once the instruction no longer felt like criticism in advance. It felt like a standard they were starting to meet.

Tuesday layered more refinement onto the week. Malik returned to office hours, this time with narrower questions and greater confidence. Nia helped another peer over a conceptual hump and realized she was slowly becoming the person younger students sought out on instinct. Wednesday’s quiz and concept check window tightened everybody’s nerves again, but not in the same way as before. The fear was still there. So was the adrenaline. Yet now those feelings had structure around them.

By Thursday, the emotional shape of the week finally sharpened into something unmistakable.

Some midterm results had started to circulate.
Not all.
Just enough.

A classmate stepped out into the hallway with a grin too bright to hide. Another looked dazed. Another groaned and promised a total life reset by next Monday. The department was alive with speculation, whispered comparisons, fragile confidence, and private dread.

Malik and Nia sat together after their final study session of the week in a corridor washed gold by the late-afternoon sun. The building had begun to empty, though not completely. Somewhere down the hall, a professor’s voice rose and fell behind a half-closed office door. A vending machine buzzed softly. The moment felt suspended between outcomes.

“Have you checked?” Nia asked.

Malik looked at his phone, then turned it face down on the bench between them. “No.”

Nia blinked. “You? Not checking immediately?”

“I’m trying something new.”

“That sounds dangerous.”

He let out a small laugh. “I want to be steady before I see whatever is there.”

Nia leaned back against the wall. “Fair.” Then, after a beat: “I checked one of mine. Not all of them.”

“And?”

She made a face that was half relief, half disbelief. “Good enough to breathe. Not good enough to get arrogant.”

“That might be the most useful grading category I’ve ever heard.”

They sat with that for a moment.

Then Nia nudged the folded flyer sticking out of Malik’s notebook. “So. Are we pretending not to think about that too?”

Malik pulled out the FAMU Undergraduate Research Symposium flyer and unfolded it carefully. Even now it seemed to carry a different kind of voltage than the graded work around them. Exams measured what they had learned so far. Research hinted at what they might become.

“I’m not pretending,” he said. “I’m calculating.”

Nia smiled. “Of course you are.”

He looked down at the flyer, then back up at her. “Last week was survival. This week feels like a verdict. But I don’t think the real question is whether we did well enough to feel safe.”

Nia’s expression shifted, growing more focused.

“What’s the real question?” she asked.

Malik answered slowly, as if hearing the truth of it while he spoke. “Whether we’re ready to do something bigger than just keeping up.”

The hallway seemed to quiet around them.

Nia took the flyer from his hand and smoothed it across her knee. “Then maybe next week we stop talking about research like it belongs to older students.”

Malik looked at her, and for the first time the possibility did not feel like a fantasy or an overreach. It felt difficult. Serious. Earned enough to attempt.

Not guaranteed.
But real.

Through the open windows at the end of the corridor, the last light of day spread across campus. Students moved between buildings with backpacks, laughter, fatigue, ambition, and all the ordinary invisible burdens of becoming themselves. FAMU seemed to hold all of it at once—the expectation, the support, the pressure, the pride. The mathematics department was not merely testing them. It was shaping them.

Nia folded the flyer once more and handed it back.

“Okay,” she said, her voice bright with challenge again. “Next week, we need more than good instincts. We need a real idea.”

Malik nodded. “And probably faculty willing to tell us why our first idea isn’t good enough.”

“Even better.”

He stood, and she stood with him.

Neither of them checked their phones right away.

The unresolved tension of the week remained exactly where it belonged—alive, productive, and pointed forward. Grades were coming. Confidence was still provisional. The future had not opened for them yet, not fully. But it had cracked wide enough to let in a dangerous, exhilarating thought:

They might be ready for more than survival.

And if that thought proved true, the beginning of next week would demand courage of an entirely different kind.
