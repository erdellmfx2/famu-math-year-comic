# Week Thirty: Momentum, Boundaries, and the Next Level of Growth

Week Twenty-Nine had ended with a question that neither Malik nor Nia could ignore.

They had spent the spring proving, little by little, that they belonged in difficult rooms, difficult classes, and difficult conversations. They had learned how to recover from shaky moments, how to ask better questions, how to steady themselves when pressure rose. More than that, they had become useful to other people. Their classmates trusted them. Their professors noticed them. Their presence in study spaces and office hours had started to mean something beyond their own personal success.

But that new visibility came with a fresh tension.

What happens when people start depending on you?

How do you keep growing without letting every opportunity, every request, and every open door pull you in too many directions at once?

By the time Thursday, February 20 arrived, that question had already begun to shape the week.

FAMU was alive with the layered energy that made campus feel like more than a collection of classes. Baseball had a neutral-site matchup against Alabama State. Softball was in motion too, facing Florida International and Incarnate Word. Conversations across campus bounced easily between assignments, attendance plans, game updates, weekend hopes, and the ordinary pressure of trying to do many important things well at the same time.

Malik felt that pressure early.

He sat at a table with his planner, laptop, and a page he had labeled in careful block letters: **Internship / Career Checklist**. A few months earlier, something like that might have felt premature, like one more thing to worry about before he had even fully settled into college mathematics. Now it felt necessary. Dr. Bennett had been pushing students to think of numbers as evidence, and Malik was beginning to understand that the same mindset applied to time, habits, and goals. If he wanted a future in actuarial science, he could not leave everything to vague ambition.

He tapped his pen against the page and reviewed what he had written: resume updates to plan for, skills to strengthen, faculty connections to maintain, timelines to learn, opportunities to watch. He did not need to complete everything now. He needed to stop acting as if the future would organize itself for him.

“Thinking five steps ahead again?”

He looked up. Nia stood beside the table with a grin and a folder tucked under one arm.

“Trying to,” Malik said.

Nia sat down across from him. “That tone means you’re making it more stressful than it has to be.”

“I’m being realistic.”

“You always say that when you’re building a spreadsheet in your head.”

He gave a reluctant smile. “And you always say that right before doing twelve things in one day.”

She laughed, because both of them knew there was truth in it.

Nia was heading to a student organization planning meeting later that afternoon, and she was genuinely excited about it. FAMU gave students room to be more than students in the narrowest sense. There were always chances to help organize something, build something, represent something, support something. That culture of involvement was one of the campus’s strengths. Students were invited to practice leadership before anyone officially handed them a title.

But Week Twenty-Nine’s final conversation still lingered between them.

Yes to leadership.
Yes to growth.
But not yes to everything.

Before they went their separate ways, Malik glanced at her folder. “What’s the meeting for?”

“Programming ideas,” Nia said. “Academic support, campus connection, maybe something that makes math students feel less isolated.”

“That sounds like you.”

“It does, doesn’t it?” she said lightly, though her expression softened after. “I just have to make sure sounding like me doesn’t turn into doing too much at once.”

That sentence stayed with both of them for the rest of the day.

Later, during class, Dr. Bennett offered a reminder that landed deeper than it first seemed.

“Interpret numbers as evidence,” he said. “Not as a personal verdict. Evidence helps you decide what to do next.”

Malik wrote it down immediately.
Nia underlined it twice.

It applied to grades, of course. To quiz scores. To practice results. To anything measurable. But both of them sensed that it also applied to the shape of their week. Fatigue was evidence. Overbooking was evidence. Feeling rushed before even beginning was evidence. Good students did not become great by ignoring information that made them uncomfortable.

Friday, February 21 brought another surge of campus motion.

Men’s basketball was set to face Alabama State. Baseball was on the road at Bethune-Cookman. Softball had neutral-site games against Eastern Illinois and Incarnate Word. The energy around campus was loud in some places, easygoing in others, but underneath it all students were still carrying coursework forward one decision at a time.

Malik protected two hours for a library deep-work block, and this time he guarded it more deliberately than before. He turned his phone face down. He opened only the materials he actually needed. He wrote the start and end times on the top of his notebook page as if making an agreement with himself.

The work in front of him was not impossible. That was not the issue. The issue was mental clutter. Half-finished thoughts about quizzes, future applications, class expectations, and other people’s requests could make a manageable task feel scattered.

So he narrowed his focus.

One problem.
Then the next.
Clean setup.
Clear assumptions.
No rushing just to feel fast.

He could almost hear Dr. Price’s voice in his head: **state assumptions before modeling**.

That afternoon, Nia stood at a whiteboard leading a study circle that had grown quietly over the last several weeks. What had once been a loose gathering of students reviewing whatever they happened to find confusing had become something more intentional. There was structure now. There was rhythm. There was a sense that people came not only to get answers, but to become stronger thinkers.

“All right,” Nia said, drawing a box around the problem at the top of the board. “Before anybody solves anything, tell me what this question is asking us to believe.”

A student leaned back. “Believe?”

“Yes,” Nia said. “Every math problem is built on a set of relationships. If you don’t know what the problem wants you to see, your algebra starts wandering.”

That drew a few smiles, but it also drew attention.

She moved them through the problem in layers: first the idea, then the structure, then the strategy, then the calculation. It was one of the reasons students responded so well to her. She did not simply know things. She knew how to escort people toward understanding without humiliating them for not already being there.

By the end of the session, a first-year student who had barely spoken in earlier meetings was standing at the board defending a correct setup with visible confidence.

“That,” Nia said, pointing at the work, “is stronger than getting lucky on the answer. You know why this works.”

Moments like that said something important about FAMU’s undergraduate mathematics culture. Growth was not treated like a private competition only the strongest could survive. There was room for rigor and room for support at the same time. Students could challenge one another upward. They could become better together.

Saturday, February 22 carried a homework deadline, a weekly planning reset, and more athletic motion with baseball at Southern and softball facing Eastern Illinois. The day had a practical tone, but practical days often revealed the deepest habits.

Malik used part of the afternoon to practice calculus drills with a timer. He still disliked how a ticking clock could make even familiar material feel less familiar. But he no longer treated that discomfort as proof of weakness. It was training data. Evidence.

When he hit a snag midway through one problem, he forced himself not to leap into frustrated speed. Instead, he slowed down just enough to identify where the reasoning had blurred.

Across campus, Nia spread her notes across a table and began rebuilding them into a concept map. Arrows, definitions, patterns, connections, exceptions. She was not just rewriting for neatness. She was converting information into relationships she could carry.

A friend passing by stopped and stared. “You remake your notes like that every time?”

“Not every time,” Nia said.

The friend raised an eyebrow.

Nia smiled. “Only when I actually want the material to stay in my brain.”

The friend laughed and pulled up a chair.

Without planning to, Nia spent the next twenty minutes explaining how she used concept maps to see where ideas connected and where they didn’t. It turned into an impromptu mini-workshop, the kind of thing that happened naturally around her now. She did not need a formal room reservation or a flyer to start helping people learn.

Sunday, February 23 brought a new calculus problem set and a sharper academic turn to the week.

Malik attended office hours with targeted questions, and the difference between who he had been in the fall and who he was now showed up almost immediately. He no longer approached office hours like a nervous confession. He came prepared. He knew what step had broken down, what assumption needed clarification, what kind of confirmation he was actually seeking.

Dr. Delgado reviewed his page, then nodded. “Your setup is cleaner.”

Malik felt a quiet satisfaction at that. Praise from a mathematics professor carried weight precisely because it was usually earned, specific, and unsentimental.

“But,” Dr. Delgado continued, tapping one line, “you still rush the transition here. Clean setup before solving, yes—but also a clean transition before committing.”

Malik nodded. “So I’m seeing the frame better than I’m carrying it through.”

“Exactly.”

That was what Malik increasingly appreciated about the department’s mentorship culture. Professors did not flatter students into false confidence. They gave them something better: precise feedback that made improvement possible.

Later that same day, Nia helped a peer work through a difficult concept that had become tangled with discouragement.

“I swear,” the student said, staring at the page, “every time I think I get it, the next problem changes shape.”

Nia leaned in. “That doesn’t mean you’re failing. It means you’re at the point where the course is asking for transfer, not memorization.”

The student looked up slowly. “So what do I do?”

“First, stop treating confusion like a character flaw,” Nia said. “Second, let’s figure out what stays the same even when the problem looks different.”

They worked line by line until the student’s shoulders finally dropped.

Monday, February 24 brought baseball at Mercer University and another subtle shift in the week’s emotional current.

Malik returned to his internship and career checklist, this time with more clarity and less anxiety. He added a few concrete next steps and crossed off one item he had already handled. The list still represented a future he had not yet reached, but it no longer felt like a distant judgment hanging over him. It felt like a map.

Nearby, Nia headed into another student organization planning meeting. This one was more crowded, more energetic, and more chaotic than the earlier one. People had ideas. Good ideas, even. But the room kept drifting.

Finally, Nia lifted her hand.

“What are we actually trying to make possible for students?” she asked.

The question changed the room.

Not because it was dramatic, but because it was clarifying.

An upperclassman nodded. “That’s the question.”

And from there the conversation sharpened. Academic support. Community. Visibility for students who loved math but did not always see themselves reflected in campus life. Events that felt welcoming instead of intimidating. Programming that helped first-year students imagine themselves not just passing courses, but growing into scholars, researchers, and future mentors.

By the time the meeting ended, Nia was energized—but also more aware of the danger Week Twenty-Nine had predicted. The more capable she became, the more often people would invite her into meaningful work.

Meaningful work was still work.

Tuesday, February 25 brought the quiz or concept-check window, baseball again at Mercer, and softball at Florida State. The tension that had been building all week finally concentrated into one academic moment.

Malik spent two more hours in the library before the quiz window, not cramming, but settling. He reviewed patterns. He checked assumptions. He wrote one reminder at the top of his page: **Do not confuse urgency with clarity.**

In another room, Nia led a final whiteboard study circle before heading into her own work. The atmosphere was focused, quieter than before. People were feeling the pressure now.

“Listen,” she said to the group before they broke, “the goal is not to feel zero nerves. The goal is to keep thinking while nerves exist.”

That line followed several students out of the room like something they would remember later.

When the concept check came, neither Malik nor Nia felt magically calm. But both of them felt equipped. They had done too much steady work this week to arrive empty.

And when it ended, the relief was real—but not complete.

Because there was still one more day, one more stretch of the week, one more chance for the larger question to reveal itself.

Wednesday, February 26 closed the week with men’s basketball at Southern, softball facing Wagner, and a kind of emotional clarity that only arrives after sustained effort.

Malik returned to timed calculus drills, but this time the exercise felt almost symbolic. The week had tested more than his mathematical speed. It had tested his ability to choose what mattered, protect his time, and treat evidence honestly.

Nia rewrote her notes into another concept map and saw, almost with surprise, how much stronger her understanding had become because she kept taking the long route to real learning.

Late that afternoon, they met outside the Mathematics Department again, as if the building itself had become a quiet checkpoint in their semester.

“Well?” Nia asked.

Malik adjusted the strap on his bag and let out a breath. “I think I’m finally learning that discipline isn’t just doing more. Sometimes it’s doing less on purpose.”

Nia smiled immediately. “That’s annoyingly wise.”

“I learned from watching you almost volunteer for three extra responsibilities in one day.”

She groaned. “See? That’s exactly the problem.”

They laughed, but only for a moment. The conversation turned more serious after.

Nia looked across campus, where students moved between late-day obligations, athletic pride, and the ordinary business of becoming themselves.

“I love that there’s so much to be part of here,” she said quietly. “I really do. FAMU makes you feel like your growth can touch more than one room.”

Malik nodded.

“But I’m starting to see,” she continued, “that if I say yes every time something matters, eventually I won’t have enough of myself left to do any of it well.”

Malik was quiet for a second. “That might be the next level of the semester.”

“Not harder math?”

“Oh, definitely harder math,” he said. “But also harder choices.”

That landed between them with the force of truth.

Week Thirty had answered the tension from Week Twenty-Nine, at least in part. Yes, they could be trusted. Yes, they could handle visibility. Yes, they could contribute to the academic and communal life around them in ways that were real and meaningful.

But the resolution only opened the door to a new and sharper challenge.

How do you become more without being pulled apart?

As they stood there, with the Mathematics Department behind them and the wider pulse of FAMU life all around them, both of them could feel that the next stage of growth would demand more than talent, more than effort, and more than good intentions.

It would demand discernment.

And that realization gave the end of the week its own particular kind of excitement.

Not the excitement of something easy.
Not the excitement of quick victory.
But the excitement of sensing that they were approaching a deeper version of themselves.

The quiz week had passed. The work had held. Their confidence was steadier. Their roles on campus were growing clearer.

Now the next question was waiting.

Not whether FAMU could make room for their gifts.
But whether they were ready to shape those gifts wisely enough to carry everything that might come next.

And as Week Thirty closed, that question did not feel frightening.
It felt alive.
It felt important.
It felt like the beginning of another breakthrough.
