# Week Seventeen: The Energy Before the Break

The week began with a feeling that was hard to name and impossible to miss.

By the time Friday morning settled over FAMU, the campus had entered that strange late-November rhythm where students were carrying two calendars at once. One calendar belonged to the university: office hours, study hall emphasis, homework deadlines, concept checks, problem sets, planning meetings, whiteboard circles, and all the disciplined habits that kept a semester from slipping away. The other calendar belonged to the season itself: rivalry-week talk, travel plans, family phone calls, Thanksgiving anticipation, and the emotional pull of home.

For Malik, the overlap felt less chaotic than it would have a month earlier.

That was growth.

He crossed campus toward the library with a notebook tucked beneath his arm and a quieter mind than he used to have on Fridays. Not empty—never empty—but quieter. The conversation he and Nia had shared at the end of the previous week still sat with him. The department was watching. Opportunities were getting closer. The routines they had fought to build were beginning to mean something. That realization had not made life easier, exactly. It had made the work feel more connected to the future.

Inside the library, the atmosphere carried the familiar FAMU blend of seriousness and warmth. Students leaned over laptops in clusters, greeted each other in hushed voices, traded quick jokes, and then turned back to equations, essays, slides, and spreadsheets. There was effort everywhere, but it did not feel lonely. That was one of the department’s strengths and one of the university’s strengths too: rigor here did not have to mean isolation.

Malik settled into a two-hour deep-work block and opened the week with the kind of discipline that had become less performative and more honest. He worked slowly through a set of practice ideas, then stopped himself before rushing into his old bad habit of trying to feel finished too early.

He heard Dr. Okafor’s voice in his head: visualize the concept first.

So he did.

Instead of charging straight into symbols, he paused to sketch the idea in plain language. What was the problem actually asking? What shape did the concept take before the notation got dense? What relationships needed to be true before any calculation mattered?

The answer came cleaner than he expected.

A week earlier, he might have seen that as proof that he was finally “smart enough.” Now he recognized something more useful: he was building better habits. The difference mattered. One idea made confidence fragile. The other made confidence repeatable.

Later that afternoon, across the hall in a classroom that still smelled faintly of dry-erase ink, Nia stood at a whiteboard with three students gathered around her.

It had started as a study circle. It had turned, almost inevitably, into a real session.

“No shortcuts at the start,” she said, uncapping a marker. “We can get elegant later. Right now we need the idea to stand on its own feet.”

One of the students groaned theatrically. “You always say that right before we realize we skipped something important.”

“Because you usually did,” Nia said, smiling.

The room laughed, and the tension broke.

That was another gift she had grown into. She could make difficulty feel breathable. Not easy, not fake, but possible. It was why people trusted her. She did not flatten the challenge. She made it more manageable to face.

She drew out a concept map instead of a solution path first, connecting definitions, assumptions, and the reason each step would matter later. A student who had been quiet for most of the semester finally raised her hand halfway and then, embarrassed by the gesture, lowered it.

Nia pointed gently. “No, keep that question. That one probably helps more people than you think.”

The student hesitated. “I’m just not sure when I’m supposed to treat the numbers as a pattern and when I’m supposed to treat them like evidence.”

That caught the room.

Nia nodded at once. “That’s a real question. And honestly, it sounds like something Dr. Bennett would ask on purpose.”

She rewrote the setup with that distinction in mind, and the whole group leaned in. Within minutes, the confusion that had seemed personal turned communal, then solvable.

That was the quiet beauty of the FAMU Mathematics Department at its best. Students were not only being taught content. They were being formed into a culture of thinkers—people who could ask better questions, support each other without embarrassment, and treat understanding as something worth sharing.

Saturday brought the Bethune-Cookman game, and the whole campus moved differently because of it.

Even before noon, the energy was visible. Students wore orange and green with extra pride. Residence hall doors opened and closed faster. Group chats filled with predictions, watch-party plans, and the kind of confident declarations people only make during rivalry week. Music drifted from farther across campus. The day felt larger than itself.

And yet the academic pressure did not vanish.

That tension gave the day its real texture.

Malik spent part of the morning with a timer running through calculus drills, not because it was glamorous, but because he had finally accepted that pressure had to be practiced, not merely survived. Every time he wanted to speed up sloppily, he forced himself to reset.

Clean enough to trust, he reminded himself.

When he finally met Nia later, she had rewritten part of her own notes into a sprawling concept map that looked halfway between mathematics and architecture.

“You made this for one topic?” he asked.

“One difficult topic,” she corrected. “And I’m not apologizing for being thorough.”

He looked closer. “Actually, this is good. You can see where the ideas branch.”

Nia lifted an eyebrow. “You say that like you’re surprised I know what I’m doing.”

“I say that like it’s slightly intimidating.”

“That’s healthier.”

The joke came easily now. So did the respect beneath it.

Around them, game-day conversation kept rising and falling. Students balanced snacks, school pride, side conversations about classes, and quick check-ins about what was due next. It was exactly the kind of moment outsiders often missed when they imagined student life too narrowly. At FAMU, campus joy and academic ambition did not cancel each other out. They sharpened each other. Spirit gave people energy. Discipline gave that energy direction.

By Sunday, the week bent back toward structure.

Homework came due. Planners reopened. The holiday week ahead loomed with all its usual temptation: ease up too early, drift, assume there would be more time later. Malik knew the trap well enough now to resist it. He went to office hours with targeted questions and arrived not with vague panic but with marked pages, exact sticking points, and a willingness to show where his thinking bent in the wrong direction.

Dr. Price studied one of his setups and nodded slowly.

“Better,” she said.

Malik almost laughed. “That sounded like a compliment with conditions.”

“It is a compliment with standards,” she replied. “State assumptions before modeling. When you do that, your errors have fewer places to hide.”

He wrote the sentence down immediately.

Nearby, Nia spent part of the afternoon helping a peer untangle a concept that had become more emotionally loaded than mathematically difficult. She recognized the look at once: the frustration that comes when confusion starts threatening identity.

“You are not behind because you need to revisit the foundation,” she told him. “You’re behind if you refuse to revisit it.”

He stared at her for a second. “That feels aggressive and encouraging at the same time.”

“It’s mentorship,” she said.

Monday brought a new calculus problem set and with it the ordinary heroism of student life—the kind that rarely looked dramatic from the outside. Malik updated his internship and career checklist before class, adding practical notes instead of abstract goals. He wrote down professors to follow up with, skills to strengthen, and questions he wanted to ask before the semester ended. For the first time, career preparation did not feel like some distant adult version of himself would handle it. It felt like an extension of the person he was becoming now.

Nia, meanwhile, attended a student organization planning meeting and did something subtle but important: she contributed without overvolunteering. Earlier in the semester, she might have said yes to every open task, mistaking exhaustion for commitment. Now she asked where her energy would matter most.

That was maturity too.

By Tuesday, another deep-work block gave Malik proof that consistency was beginning to outmuscle fear. By Wednesday, the quiz window arrived, and both he and Nia felt the pressure—but not in the old catastrophic way. They had routines now. Warm-up thinking. Cleaner setup. Better pacing. Questions they knew how to ask. The shift was not that anxiety had disappeared. The shift was that it no longer ruled the room.

After the concept check, they walked out together beneath a sky turning the soft gold of late afternoon.

“Well?” Nia asked.

Malik exhaled. “Not perfect. But honest. I knew what I was doing, even when I had to slow down.”

“That’s not a small thing,” she said.

He glanced at her. “How about you?”

“I think,” she said carefully, “I’m finally learning the difference between being capable and being sustainable.”

He nodded. He understood that better than she probably knew.

Thursday arrived with Thanksgiving, and the pace of campus changed again.

Some students had gone home. Some stayed. Some were in between, carrying family through phone calls, memory, tradition, or chosen community. The quieter campus made certain things easier to hear: footsteps across walkways, laughter outside residence halls, a professor’s kind check-in, the soft emotional recalibration that comes when a hard stretch of the semester meets a day meant for gratitude.

Malik spent part of the day reflecting on how much had changed since August. He was still himself—still structured, still serious, still prone to wanting control—but the fear underneath those traits no longer defined every choice. He had learned how to ask better questions, how to use office hours, how to let feedback become data, how to stay present when a problem resisted him.

Nia spent part of the day thinking about community. About how often growth had come through people. A professor’s sentence at the right moment. A peer asking a brave question. A study circle that turned into a support system. A department culture that took undergraduates seriously. The FAMU Mathematics Department had not simply challenged her; it had given her room to become larger than her own early habits.

That evening, they met for a brief walk and let the quiet campus hold the conversation.

“It feels like something’s about to shift again,” Malik said.

Nia looked at him. “Academically, or in the bigger sense?”

He considered it. “Both.”

She nodded slowly. “Yeah. Me too.”

There was no grand speech after that. It did not need one.

Because the tension at the end of the week was not panic. It was anticipation.

Finals season was beginning to gather in the distance. The routines they had built were about to face their biggest test. The opportunities hinted at by faculty attention, stronger performance, and emerging leadership were no longer abstract enough to ignore. Something was coming—an inflection point, a proving ground, maybe even the first real payoff for all the invisible discipline of the semester.

They had not arrived there by accident.

And as Thanksgiving night settled over campus, that truth gave the week its final charge:

for the first time, the future felt close enough to be exciting, and serious enough to demand everything they had learned.
