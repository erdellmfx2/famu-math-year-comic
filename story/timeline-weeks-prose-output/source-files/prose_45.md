# Week 45 Prose — June 5 to June 11, 2026

The rumor turned out to be real.

That was the first important thing the new week gave Malik and Nia, and it gave it to them early enough that neither of them had time to pretend they were not invested. By Friday morning, word had moved through the familiar summer channels of the Florida A&M University mathematics community—half hallway conversation, half purposeful whisper, fully charged by the time it reached the students who had spent the last several weeks proving to themselves that quiet discipline could build a different kind of future.

There would, in fact, be an undergraduate research information session.

Not a flashy ceremony. Not a dramatic competition. Just a faculty-led gathering about summer opportunities, project pathways, mentoring relationships, and what it meant for students to move from mastering coursework into asking questions that did not yet have neat answers.

For some students, it was merely useful information. For Malik and Nia, it felt like a door they had been walking toward without realizing how close they had come.

Malik started Friday with his two-hour deep-work block in the library, but his concentration had a different edge to it now. The library still held the same summer steadiness it always did—cool air, soft foot traffic, the scratch of pens, the low rustle of pages turning—but the room felt almost more alive because of what was sitting just outside his immediate work. His notebook lay open to a page of calculus problems, and at the top, in careful lettering, he had written:

**Solve what is here. Prepare for what is next.**

It was the sort of sentence he would once have mocked for sounding inspirational, but this year had taught him to respect clarity wherever he found it.

He worked methodically, setting each problem up before pushing toward a result. He wrote known quantities, assumptions, constraints, and the shape of the strategy before he let himself chase the computation. Dr. Bennett’s voice drifted through his memory from earlier conversations about statistics and applied thinking: interpret numbers as evidence. Not decoration. Not just output. Evidence.

That phrase mattered more now. Research, in Malik’s mind, had once felt like something abstract and distant—something for students who lived several steps above ordinary coursework. But if numbers were evidence, if assumptions mattered, if setup mattered, if clear reasoning mattered, then maybe research was not some separate universe after all. Maybe it was an extension of the habits he had been learning all year, just aimed at bigger questions.

When Nia found him later that afternoon, he was still looking at the same page, not because he was stuck but because he was thinking past it.

“You’ve got the face,” she said as she set her bag down across from him.

Malik looked up. “What face?”

“The one where you’re solving one problem and simultaneously trying to redesign your whole life.”

He laughed despite himself. “That’s an exaggeration.”

“It never is.”

Nia slid into the chair and spread out her own materials. She had come from leading a whiteboard study circle, and she still carried that alert, energized presence that always followed her when she had been in teaching mode. There was dry-erase marker on the side of her hand.

“How’d the session go?” Malik asked.

“Good. A little messy at first, then good. People were jumping straight to answers again.”

“And?”

“And I made them slow down.” She gave him a look. “Like somebody I know would appreciate.”

He nodded approvingly. “Structure. Beautiful.”

“Don’t get carried away. I still let them talk through the idea before boxing them in.”

“That’s why it works.”

She tilted her head. “You heard about the session?”

He tapped the corner of his notebook. “Yeah.”

Nia smiled, and for a moment they both sat in the feeling of it. Not just excitement. Recognition. The rumor at the end of the last week had carried tension because it asked a hard question: were they ready to see themselves as students who could step into larger academic opportunities? The answer had not arrived all at once, but now that the information session was confirmed, the tension had shifted. It was no longer about whether the opportunity existed. It was about whether they would move toward it with courage.

“I think we should go in with questions,” Nia said.

Malik gave her a dry look. “I already started a list.”

“Of course you did.”

He turned the page toward her. Research interests. Time expectations. Faculty mentorship. Skill gaps. Project fit. Application process. Preparation strategies.

Nia looked it over and laughed softly. “You absolutely spiraled politely.”

“Productively,” he corrected.

“Fair.”

The rest of Friday unfolded in the quiet, capable rhythm that had defined their summer weeks. Nia continued guiding students through concepts at the board, never rushing to perform intelligence when she could instead build understanding. Malik closed out his deep-work block with the kind of satisfaction that came from clean setup and honest effort rather than frantic speed. Campus life moved around them with that unmistakable FAMU texture—students greeting each other by name, professors visible and approachable, the mathematics department feeling less like a maze of requirements and more like a real intellectual home.

Saturday pushed each of them back into their individual habits in ways that deepened the larger theme of the week.

Malik spent part of the day doing timed calculus drills again, but this time he watched not just his speed. He watched his decisions. Where did he hesitate? Where did he assume too much? Where did a messy start produce a messy middle? It felt less like test prep and more like self-study, which in its own way was a research mindset too. He was collecting evidence about his thinking.

Later, he crossed paths with Dr. Price, who had a gift for asking questions that made ordinary conversations feel like invitations into more serious thought.

“How are you using your time this summer?” Dr. Price asked.

Malik answered with the practical version first. “Reviewing, keeping pace, organizing internship materials.”

Dr. Price nodded. “And beneath the practical version?”

Malik smiled. “Trying to become the kind of student who can do more than finish assignments well.”

“That’s a strong goal,” Dr. Price said. “Just remember—before any model becomes useful, somebody has to state the assumptions clearly. The same is true for your future. Don’t build it from vague ambition. Build it from examined purpose.”

State assumptions before modeling.

The phrase landed with fresh force. Malik had heard it academically before. Now he heard it personally.

Across campus, Nia spent the same day rewriting notes into another broad concept map, but her real turning point came when a younger student lingered after asking for help.

“You make it look easy,” the student said.

Nia shook her head immediately. “No. I make it look organized.”

The student blinked.

“That’s different,” Nia continued. “A lot of this only feels smooth because I’ve had to learn how not to drown in too many ideas at once.”

The honesty mattered. So did the relief it brought to the other student’s face. Nia had grown into someone who could model excellence without pretending she had never struggled. That was part of what made her a believable leader inside the FAMU mathematics community. She did not showcase perfection; she showcased growth.

By Sunday, the homework-due rhythm and weekly planning reset sharpened the tempo. Summer courses never allowed students to stay casual for long. Malik attended office hours with targeted questions, arriving with a marked page and an actual agenda rather than a vague hope of rescue. Dr. Brooks reviewed one of his explanations and pointed to a line in the middle.

“You know the answer,” Dr. Brooks said. “But here—you skipped showing why the transition is valid.”

Malik leaned in.

“Show your reasoning clearly,” Dr. Brooks continued. “Not because I need proof that you did the work. Because clarity forces the mind to reveal where it is guessing.”

That was pure FAMU mathematics culture right there: rigor as care, not cruelty. Precision as formation, not punishment.

Meanwhile, Nia spent part of Sunday helping a peer with a difficult concept that had begun to knot itself tighter every time the student looked at it. Instead of solving the whole problem in front of them, Nia helped the student identify what kind of idea the problem belonged to, what assumptions it required, and what visual picture might anchor the reasoning. By the end of the session, the student was smiling with the startled expression of someone who had just watched confusion loosen its grip.

“I thought I was just bad at this topic,” the student admitted.

Nia capped the marker and shook her head. “Usually it’s not that. Usually you just need a better entry point.”

Monday brought the new calculus problem set, and with it came a subtle rise in tension. The research session still sat ahead of them like a bright marker on the horizon, but the daily work had not stopped for their anticipation. That, too, felt important. Opportunity did not replace discipline. It asked for more of it.

Malik updated his internship and career checklist that afternoon, adding new categories beneath the research heading he had started the week before. Faculty to approach. Questions about modeling. Statistical applications. Possible connections to actuarial work. He could feel his future becoming less abstract each time he translated hope into structure.

Nia, for her part, attended a student organization planning meeting and found herself thinking more carefully than ever about how leadership and scholarship connected. The best student life opportunities at FAMU were never just social decoration. At their best, they created belonging, confidence, and shared purpose—the same ingredients that allowed students to persist in demanding fields. She spoke during the meeting about making academic support visible and welcoming, about making research and advanced opportunities feel reachable rather than mysterious.

One of the other students nodded thoughtfully. “That’s what people need,” she said. “Not just information. Permission to imagine themselves in the room.”

The sentence stayed with Nia long after the meeting ended.

Tuesday gave them another familiar pattern: Malik back in a library deep-work block, Nia again at the whiteboard leading a study circle. But the emotional tone had changed. The week was no longer merely steady. It was gathering confidence.

At the study circle, one student asked Nia whether she thought undergraduate research was really meant for students who were “still figuring things out.”

Nia paused before answering.

“I think most people doing meaningful work are still figuring things out,” she said. “The point isn’t that you arrive finished. The point is that you learn how to ask better questions.”

Several students went quiet in the thoughtful way that meant the words had landed. Nia felt it too. She was not only answering them. She was answering part of herself.

Wednesday brought the quiz and concept-check window, and even though it was not a semester-defining exam, it carried enough pressure to test whether their new habits were solid or fragile. Malik practiced timed drills with a calmer face than he would have worn months ago. Nia rewrote her notes into another concept map, but this time she focused on relationships between ideas likely to appear in unfamiliar forms.

Dr. Bennett, seeing Malik after the assessment window, offered a brief but timely reminder. “When you review, don’t just count what was right and wrong. Ask what the results are telling you.”

Interpret numbers as evidence.

There it was again.

Nia had her own faculty echo that day, this time from a conversation circling back to how understanding deepens. The professors did not merely fill students with content. They kept shaping how students thought about thought itself. By now, Malik and Nia were carrying those voices into their own routines, their study partnerships, their leadership, even their private self-corrections.

And then Thursday arrived—the end of the week, and with it, the information session that had cast its glow backward across all the days before it.

The room was not packed in a dramatic way, but it was full in the meaningful way: students attentive, faculty present, questions ready, summer light angling through the windows as if the campus itself wanted to witness the moment. Malik and Nia sat together near the middle, notebooks open.

Faculty members spoke plainly and seriously about undergraduate research culture—about curiosity, preparation, mentorship, and the value of learning to contribute even before you felt like an expert. They described projects involving modeling, data, applied questions, and collaborative inquiry. They spoke about office hours not just as a place to survive coursework, but as a place where intellectual relationships could begin. They spoke about FAMU students as students capable of serious work.

That last part mattered most.

No one in the room was being flattered. They were being invited.

During the question portion, Nia asked about balancing research with existing commitments without losing academic footing. It was the kind of question only someone who had truly learned from overextension would ask. The answer she received was thoughtful and encouraging: start with fit, communicate early, choose depth over scattered involvement.

Malik asked about how a student could know whether their interests were developed enough to approach faculty about a possible project. One professor smiled and answered in a way that softened something in his chest.

“You do not need a finished research identity to begin a research conversation,” the professor said. “You need seriousness, curiosity, and the willingness to prepare.”

Malik wrote that down immediately.

When the session ended, students lingered instead of rushing out. That alone said a lot. Conversations opened in clusters. Questions continued. Names were exchanged. Possibilities became slightly more concrete.

Outside afterward, the evening air felt charged.

They walked slowly across campus, past buildings that now held a year’s worth of memories—hard weeks, growth weeks, ordinary weeks that turned out to matter more than expected. FAMU looked beautiful in the late light, but it also looked like what it had steadily become for them: a place that expected much from them and believed they could rise to meet it.

“Well?” Nia asked.

Malik exhaled and laughed once. “I think the suspense from last week officially paid off.”

She smiled. “Yeah. It did.”

“But now,” he added, glancing down at the notes in his hand, “I think we have a new problem.”

Nia raised an eyebrow. “A good problem?”

“A very good one.”

He turned the notebook so she could see the page he had just started.

**Faculty to Contact This Week**

Nia looked at the heading, then back at him, and her grin widened. “That’s not a problem. That’s momentum.”

They kept walking.

The week had resolved the tension it inherited from the one before. The rumor was true. The opportunity was real. The future they had been cautiously circling now had a clearer shape. But as often happened in good stories and good school years, resolution did not end the motion. It redirected it.

Because now the question was no longer whether research belonged in the world around them.

Now the question was which door they would knock on first.

And as the campus evening settled around them and the mathematics building stood behind them like both a memory and a promise, that next question carried its own bright charge forward into the week to come.
