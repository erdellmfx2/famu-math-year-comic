# Week Thirty-Three: The Week They Started Acting Ready

The question that closed the previous week did not disappear over the weekend.

If research, leadership, mentorship, and bigger opportunities were no longer distant ideas but real possibilities beginning to move toward Malik and Nia, then who would they need to become when those doors finally opened?

By the beginning of Week Thirty-Three, neither of them had a perfect answer.

But they had something better than a perfect answer.

They had the beginning of proof.

Not proof in the sense of certainty. Not proof that every plan was settled or every insecurity had vanished. But proof that readiness was not a mysterious trait that arrived all at once like a spotlight. Readiness, they were learning, was built in smaller choices—how you used an office hour, how you handled a study session, how you treated pressure, how clearly you reasoned when the week became crowded, how well you carried your responsibilities without dropping yourself.

And Week Thirty-Three gave them many chances to test that idea.

Campus was alive from the very first day. Men’s basketball against Southern, baseball against Southern University, softball against Mississippi Valley State—FAMU pulsed with the layered energy it carried so naturally in spring, when athletics, academics, weather, ambition, and friendship all seemed to move at once. Students crossed the grounds in clusters, checking scores on phones, trading updates about assignments, calling out to friends across walkways, and still somehow making their way to the library, to office hours, to study halls, to departmental spaces where the quieter work of the semester kept unfolding.

That was one of the things Malik had come to admire most about FAMU.

The university never felt one-dimensional.

You could feel pride in the air from the games, hear laughter from a student organization table, pass a professor in thoughtful conversation with a student, and then turn a corner and walk straight into the disciplined stillness of someone solving a difficult mathematical problem at a whiteboard. The campus did not ask students to choose between excellence and life. It asked them to learn how to carry both.

On Friday, March 13, Malik settled into a two-hour deep-work block at the library with more intention than anxiety. That difference mattered. Earlier in the year, he would have mistaken intensity for productivity. Now he understood that a calm mind could often do more rigorous work than a panicked one.

His notebook was already open when he paused over a line from Dr. Price that had followed him since class:

**State assumptions before modeling.**

It sounded simple. It was not simple.

Malik had begun to realize that many of his older mistakes came not from lacking ability, but from moving too quickly past the invisible frame of the problem. He wanted the solution before he had fully honored the structure. He wanted the result before he had properly named the conditions that made the result meaningful.

So that afternoon, before he solved, he wrote.

What is being assumed here?
What is fixed?
What is changing?
What would make this interpretation invalid?

The questions slowed him down in the best way.

Across campus, Nia stood at a whiteboard leading a study circle that felt more confident than it had even a few weeks earlier. The students around her no longer gathered with the slightly desperate energy of people hoping someone smarter would rescue them. They came ready to think. Ready to explain. Ready to test ideas in public.

That change had not happened by accident.

Nia had built a culture in the room, and the culture reflected what she herself had been learning. She asked students to speak their reasoning. She let pauses happen. She made confusion discussable instead of embarrassing. She pushed for conceptual understanding before symbolic speed. In a department like FAMU Mathematics, where rigor and mentorship could live side by side, that kind of student leadership mattered.

At one point, a first-year student frowned at the board and said, “I keep wanting to jump to the formula, but I can tell that’s not the right move.”

Nia smiled. “That’s growth. The fact that you can hear the shortcut calling your name and still resist it—that’s growth.”

The group laughed, but the student nodded seriously.

“Okay,” Nia continued, drawing a clean line beneath the setup. “Then let’s earn the formula instead of hiding behind it.”

That sentence stayed with the room.

Later that evening, when the sounds of game-day excitement floated through campus in waves, Malik and Nia crossed paths near the mathematics building. The light was beginning to soften, and students drifted past them carrying backpacks, snacks, team gear, and the layered expressions of a spring semester in motion.

“How was the library?” Nia asked.

Malik exhaled. “Less dramatic than it used to be.”

“That is very high praise from you.”

“I think Dr. Price is rewiring my brain.”

She laughed. “Because of assumptions?”

“Yeah. I keep seeing how many mistakes come from solving a problem I haven’t actually defined.”

Nia nodded. “That applies to more than math.”

He looked at her. “You mean life?”

“I mean leadership,” she said. “And responsibility. And saying yes to the right things.”

That was the first quiet resolution of the week: the tension from Week Thirty-Two did not vanish in a dramatic speech. It began to resolve in practice. The answer to who they needed to become was not abstract. They needed to become more deliberate people.

Saturday, March 14 carried another full sports slate—baseball against Southern University, softball against Mississippi Valley State—and yet both of them protected academic time with noticeable maturity.

Malik worked through calculus drills on a timer, but his relationship to the timer had changed. He was no longer treating the clock like an opponent. He treated it like a training condition. When he made an error, he stopped, marked it, and named the reason for it instead of just erasing it and pretending it had not happened.

Rushed setup.
Skipped unit check.
Assumed pattern too early.

Each note turned a mistake into information.

Meanwhile, Nia rewrote her notes into a sprawling concept map that stretched across more pages than she had intended. Arrows connected ideas from class to prior units; small margin comments translated formal procedure into memorable language; starred sections marked ideas she knew she could teach someone else if necessary.

Dr. Brooks’s guidance—**show your reasoning clearly**—echoed in her mind as she reorganized the material. She realized that clear reasoning was not just something performed for professors on quizzes and exams. It was an act of respect toward one’s own thinking. When she clarified a concept fully, she trusted herself more.

Sunday, March 15 brought homework due, a weekly planning reset, and one more baseball game against Southern University. It also delivered one of the week’s warmest scenes.

Malik attended office hours carrying targeted questions, not because he was in collapse, but because he had learned that strong students did not wait until confusion became a crisis. Dr. Delgado reviewed his work carefully and then tapped a section near the top of the page.

“Your finish is fine,” she said. “But the setup is where your confidence really lives.”

Malik leaned closer.

“You reached the correct result,” she continued, “but I want you to notice something. The moment your setup became clean, the rest of the problem almost solved itself.”

He looked at the page and saw it immediately.

Dr. Delgado gave him a small nod. “Clean setup before solving. Not because neatness is decorative. Because clear beginnings protect thinking.”

That sentence landed deeply. Malik had always respected precision, but now he was understanding its emotional power too. A clean start could steady a mind.

Across campus, Nia was helping a peer who had become tangled in a difficult concept and, more painfully, embarrassed by that struggle.

“I should know this by now,” the student muttered.

Nia shook her head gently. “That sentence is usually where learning gets stuck.”

The student looked up.

“You don’t need shame,” Nia said. “You need a clearer picture.”

She spent the next stretch of time breaking the problem into visual pieces, asking questions instead of overwhelming the student with explanation. By the end, the student was not magically transformed into a master of the topic, but something more important had happened: the fear had loosened.

That mattered.

In the FAMU Mathematics Department, mentorship was not always formal. Sometimes it looked like a professor redirecting a thought in office hours. Sometimes it looked like a study-circle leader protecting the dignity of someone who needed another pass through the material. Sometimes it looked like peers refusing to let each other fail in silence.

Monday, March 16 shifted the week toward future-facing momentum. A new calculus problem set was assigned. Malik updated his internship and career checklist. Nia attended a student organization planning meeting.

This could have been a quieter day. Instead, it became the hinge of the chapter.

While reviewing options and notes he had been collecting over previous weeks, Malik realized that his checklist no longer felt like fantasy inventory. Research opportunities, internships, faculty relationships, summer plans—these were not random items on a productivity page. They were connected to the exact habits he had been building all semester. Show your reasoning. State assumptions. Ask better questions. Build consistency. Seek help early. Interpret evidence well.

It struck him with unusual force that readiness for future opportunity was already being rehearsed in his present discipline.

Nia had a parallel realization during the student organization meeting. Plans moved quickly. Excitement was real. Several students wanted to build more programming, more outreach, more visibility. Earlier in the year, she might have felt the old pull to take on too much simply because she cared. But now she listened with intention. She contributed meaningfully. She volunteered where her gifts actually strengthened the group.

Walking out of the meeting, she felt something she had not always associated with restraint.

Pride.

Not the brittle pride of being seen. The grounded pride of knowing she had acted wisely.

Tuesday, March 17 brought another library deep-work block for Malik, another whiteboard study circle for Nia, and baseball against Mercer University. The campus energy was again unmistakable, yet both of them seemed steadier inside it.

The study circle that afternoon became one of Nia’s best yet. Students arrived with more pointed questions than before, and instead of dissolving into side chatter or passive waiting, the group moved with purpose. She structured the session into short rounds: understand the problem, articulate the concept, solve carefully, compare reasoning.

At one point Malik arrived after his own work and stood near the back long enough to notice something new.

The room trusted her.

Not just because she was smart. Because she helped people think better.

Afterward, when most of the group had dispersed, he said it plainly.

“You know that’s leadership, right?”

Nia capped the marker and raised an eyebrow. “What, writing too much on whiteboards?”

“No,” he said. “Building a room where people stop hiding what they don’t understand.”

Her joking expression softened.

“That means a lot,” she said quietly.

He hesitated, then added, “I think this is what last week was pointing toward. Not just future opportunities. Practice becoming the kind of people who can carry them.”

She looked back at the board, now full of equations, arrows, circles, and corrected lines. “Yeah,” she said. “I think so too.”

Wednesday, March 18 brought the quiz or concept-check window, and the whole week narrowed to a point. This was one of those days when effort needed to become execution.

Malik returned to timed calculus drills before the assessment, but he did not chase speed recklessly. He used the timer as preparation, not intimidation. Nia revised her concept map again, tightening it until she could move through the material with confidence and flexibility. Dr. Price’s reminder about assumptions hovered in Malik’s mind; Dr. Brooks’s insistence on clear reasoning stayed with Nia.

They both felt the pressure.

But pressure no longer automatically translated into panic.

That was the real achievement.

After the quiz window, they met outside with the tired half-laughter of students who had just spent too much mental energy in too short a period of time.

“Well?” Nia asked.

“I survived without arguing with the page,” Malik said.

“That’s enormous growth.”

“And you?”

She grinned. “I think my concept map won at least two points for me.”

He laughed, then grew thoughtful. “You know what I’m realizing?”

“What?”

“I used to think feeling ready would mean not feeling tension.”

Nia nodded slowly. “But now?”

“Now I think readiness might mean being able to work clearly while the tension is still there.”

That was the second major resolution of the chapter, and perhaps the most important one. The question from the previous week—who would they need to become?—had found a deeper answer. They did not need to become fearless. They needed to become steady.

Thursday, March 19 closed the week without the spectacle of a major sporting event, but the emotional momentum was stronger than ever. Malik returned to office hours for targeted questions. Nia helped another peer with a difficult concept. Dr. Brooks’s reminder to show reasoning clearly returned once more, this time feeling less like instruction and more like identity.

When they met near the end of the day, the campus had entered that familiar late-afternoon mood where sunlight stretched long across the walkways and the rush of the day softened just enough for reflection.

Malik held his notebook under one arm. Nia had a marker stain on the side of her hand.

“You still answering last week’s question?” she asked.

He smiled. “I think this week answered part of it for us.”

She folded her arms, listening.

“We need to become people who are deliberate under pressure,” he said. “People who can help others while keeping our own foundation strong. People who don’t just want opportunity—people who can carry it well.”

Nia let the words settle.

Then she nodded. “That feels true.”

For a moment they stood in silence, watching students pass by the mathematics building, hearing the ordinary sounds of a university that was doing what it did best: shaping futures through daily work, community, rigor, and care.

The week had resolved something meaningful. The excitement from Week Thirty-Two had not gone unanswered. It had found form. Malik and Nia were no longer merely imagining a larger future. They were practicing for it.

And yet the week ended with a new tension rising naturally behind that resolution.

Because once you begin acting ready, another question appears almost immediately.

Who is watching?

Not in a paranoid sense. In a consequential one.

Professors noticed consistency. Peers noticed leadership. Departments noticed reliability. Opportunities often began long before formal invitations, quietly taking shape in the minds of people who observed how students moved through ordinary weeks like this one.

As Malik and Nia started walking across campus, that realization lingered between them with equal parts excitement and weight.

If they were becoming more ready,
if the FAMU Mathematics Department was helping shape them into students who could think clearly, mentor generously, and lead with discipline,
then it was only a matter of time before someone entrusted them with something bigger.

And when that moment came, it would not feel like a random surprise.

It would feel like the next line in a proof they had already begun to write.
