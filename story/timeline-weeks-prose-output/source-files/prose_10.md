# Week Ten: The Week Pressure Learned Their Names

Friday began without fanfare, but both Malik Baptiste and Nia Reynolds could feel the campus tightening around the coming week.

Not in a dramatic way.
Not yet.

It was there in the quieter tone inside the library, in the way students spoke with half-finished confidence about “being ready,” in the sudden seriousness that came over even the most social corners of campus whenever somebody mentioned a quiz, a concept check, or the midterm-style exam window waiting just ahead.

FAMU still looked beautiful in the morning light. The breeze still moved through the trees. Students still crossed campus wearing Rattler green with coffee in hand, laughter rising in quick pockets between buildings. But underneath that ordinary movement was something else now: concentration. The kind that made a week feel important before it had even unfolded.

Malik felt it first as he sat at a table in his room with his internship and career checklist open beside a stack of calculus notes.

A month earlier, that pairing would have felt impossible. Career planning belonged to the future. Calculus urgency belonged to the present. He would have treated them as competing demands and resented both.

Now he understood something FAMU had been teaching him slowly, one demanding week at a time: serious goals did not wait politely for each other. You had to learn how to carry more than one kind of ambition at once.

He highlighted two items on the checklist, updated a deadline, then shut the page before it could steal too much mental space.

“Future Malik can be grateful,” he muttered. “Present Malik has derivatives to survive.”

Across campus, Nia was leaving a student organization planning meeting with her notebook pressed against her chest and three ideas still sparking at once in her mind. She loved that feeling. At FAMU, meetings did not just feel administrative. They often felt alive—full of students trying to build something worthwhile for each other. That mattered to her. It was one of the reasons she had been drawn to this place in the first place.

But as she stepped out into the hallway and glanced at the time, the familiar pressure returned.

Too many moving pieces.
Too many things she cared about.
Too many opportunities that seemed good enough to say yes to.

She stopped walking, leaned against the wall for a moment, and opened her notebook to a blank page.

At the top she wrote:

**What must matter most this week?**

Underneath, she listed three answers.
1. Prepare clearly.
2. Protect energy.
3. Show up for the people who are also trying.

It was not a perfect plan.
But it was a boundary.
And lately, boundaries were becoming one of the hidden forms of wisdom in her first-year life.

By Saturday, the pressure had become communal.

Malik’s two-hour library deep-work block began in silence and ended with his brain humming from the effort of staying disciplined. He was tempted, more than once, to race through problems just to feel productive. Instead, he forced himself to slow down, state each assumption, and write clean setups before solving. Dr. Price’s voice stayed with him even though the professor was nowhere nearby: *State assumptions before modeling.*

It sounded simple when professors said it.
In practice, it required honesty.

Malik noticed that the hardest problems were not always the ones with the most steps. Sometimes they were the ones that exposed how quickly he wanted to move past uncertainty without naming it.

Later that afternoon, he found Nia at a whiteboard study circle she had organized. Students stood in loose clusters, markers uncapped, notebooks open, everybody trying to turn private confusion into something workable.

Nia was in her element.

“Don’t just tell me the answer,” she said to a sophomore volunteer who had stepped in to help. “Tell us what made that the right first move.”

A few students laughed softly.

“No, seriously,” she said, smiling. “That’s where half the learning is. The first move tells the truth about what you think the problem is.”

Malik leaned against the wall for a moment and watched her work.

He admired this about her, though he did not always say it out loud. Nia could make academic effort feel shared instead of isolating. She did not flatten difficulty. She did not pretend everything was easy. But she had a gift for creating space where people could struggle without feeling small.

When the group thinned, she turned to him.

“You look like you’ve been negotiating with a limit problem for two hours.”

“Three, actually.”

“That bad?”

“That honest.”

She grinned. “That might be progress.”

He nodded toward the board. “You’re building a whole little math civilization out here.”

“Somebody has to.” She capped a marker. “Everybody’s tense. If we don’t make room to think together, people start panicking alone.”

That sentence stayed with him longer than he expected.

By Sunday, the weekly reset arrived. Homework was due. Notes had to be reorganized. Plans had to be made before the week made them instead.

Malik used a timer for calculus drills and hated how exposed it made him feel. The ticking clock transformed every hesitation into something visible. Each delay felt measurable.

But he also noticed something encouraging: he was not collapsing when the timer pressed him. He was learning how to remain himself under pressure.

Not perfectly.
But more often.

Nia spent part of the evening rewriting her notes into a concept map, turning scattered pages into structure. Arrows linked ideas across chapters. Definitions became relationships. Procedures became patterns.

Her roommate looked over from across the room and said, “That looks less like notes and more like detective work.”

Nia smiled without looking up. “That’s because math is detective work. People just hide that part under symbols.”

Monday brought a fresh problem set and the kind of seriousness that settles over campus before exam windows. The walkways were still full, the conversations still warm, but more students were carrying folders, review sheets, and the look of people rehearsing courage.

Malik went to office hours with targeted questions instead of vague worry. That alone felt like growth.

Dr. Delgado looked over his work, tapped the top of one page, and said, “Your setup is improving. Cleaner start, cleaner mind.”

Malik let out a breath he had not realized he was holding.

“So I’m not just getting slower?”

Dr. Delgado almost smiled. “You’re getting deliberate. Students confuse those two all the time.”

Across the hall later that day, Nia helped a classmate untangle a difficult concept by drawing it three different ways until it clicked. She did not rush the explanation. She kept returning to the image first, just as Dr. Okafor often encouraged: *Visualize the concept first.*

That was one of the quiet gifts of the department. The professors were not merely delivering content. They were shaping habits of mind. Again and again, FAMU’s mathematics culture showed itself not only through rigor, but through mentorship—the steady insistence that students could become deeper thinkers if given both challenge and guidance.

Tuesday finally arrived, and with it the first true edge of the week.

The midterm-style exam window was no longer approaching.
It was here.

Students moved through the morning with a heightened stillness. Voices dropped. Jokes came quicker and ended faster. Backpacks looked heavier, though nothing inside them had changed.

Malik updated one last career checklist item before class, almost as an act of defiance. He refused to let the exam make his whole identity collapse into one moment. Nia came from another planning meeting, but this time she kept it short and left early, choosing her priorities on purpose.

When they met outside the building, neither pretended to be relaxed.

“You ready?” Nia asked.

“No,” Malik said.

She blinked.

Then he added, “But I’m prepared. I think that’s the more important one.”

That made her laugh—a small, needed sound in a tense morning.

“Okay,” she said. “That was annoyingly wise.”

Inside, the exam did what real academic pressure always does: it revealed habits.

Malik felt the old urge to sprint when he recognized the first problem. This time he resisted. He wrote the setup first. He named the assumptions. He kept his breathing steady. One question later, when uncertainty hit, he did not let panic reinterpret it as failure. He moved, recalibrated, and kept going.

Nia, meanwhile, felt the danger from another direction. Her mind was quick enough to chase too many interpretations at once. She had to choose clarity over cleverness. More than once, she silently repeated to herself: *One problem at a time. One clean thought at a time.*

When they emerged, neither one was smiling immediately.

“How bad?” Malik asked.

Nia exhaled. “Not bad. Just intense. There were two questions that definitely wanted to start a fight.”

“That sounds right.”

They started walking, and slowly the tension cracked enough for reflection.

By Wednesday, the pressure doubled: a quiz or concept check window layered over the exam rhythm already in motion. It felt almost unfair.

Which was exactly why the week mattered.

Anyone could feel committed on a calm week.
This was the week that tested whether their new systems actually held.

Nia led another whiteboard session, but this one felt sharper than the Saturday circle. Less exploratory. More urgent. Students were tired, but they kept showing up for each other. Malik took a marker and worked through a problem in deliberate steps, hearing Dr. Bennett’s reminder in his head: *Interpret numbers as evidence.*

Not verdicts.
Evidence.

It changed the mood in the room.
A wrong answer became a clue.
A gap became something you could address.
Not something you had to hide.

By Thursday, exhaustion had settled into their shoulders, but so had something else.

Confidence.
Not the loud kind.
Not the kind that boasts before results arrive.

The earned kind.
The kind built from repeated choices made under strain.

Malik ran one final set of timed calculus drills and noticed, to his surprise, that the timer no longer sounded like an enemy. It sounded like a challenge he knew how to meet. Nia rewrote her final notes into a cleaner concept map and realized she could now see the week as a coherent story rather than a blur of threats.

Later that evening, they crossed a quieter part of campus together, the light softening around them, the buildings holding the last warmth of day.

FAMU did not feel easy.
It felt worth it.

That difference mattered.

“You know what’s strange?” Nia said.

“What?”

“A few weeks ago, a week like this would’ve made me feel like I was falling behind my own life.”

“And now?”

She looked toward the mathematics building before answering.

“Now it feels like I’m actually becoming the person this place keeps asking me to be.”

Malik was quiet for a moment.
Then he nodded.

“Yeah,” he said. “Me too.”

They stood there another second, neither hurrying to fill the silence.

In the distance, laughter drifted from another part of campus. Somewhere nearby, students were still studying. Somewhere else, somebody was probably already worrying about the next round of assignments, the next score, the next test of discipline.

And that was the truth waiting at the edge of the week: this was not the end of the pressure.
It was proof that they could survive it differently now.

Before they parted, Nia adjusted the strap on her bag and said, “Next week, when the results start coming back, we’re not spiraling.”

Malik raised an eyebrow. “That a request or a rule?”

“Both.”

He almost laughed. “Fine. But if one of those grades comes back ugly, I reserve the right to stare at a wall for five minutes.”

“Five,” she said. “Not fifty.”

“Deal.”

They walked in opposite directions then, carrying fatigue, discipline, and the unfinished tension of a week that had demanded a lot and still had not fully shown its hand.

Behind them, the campus kept breathing with its usual mixture of challenge and promise.
Ahead of them, the next week waited with answers they did not yet have.

And for the first time, that uncertainty felt less like a threat than the opening line of another chapter.
