# Week Three: The Syllabus Reality

The electricity that had gathered at the edge of the previous weekend did not dissipate. Instead, it sharpened into something distinctly real.

By the time Friday arrived, the final, lingering quiet of the summer was definitively over. The FAMU campus, which had felt like a picturesque waiting room just days before, was now fully awake. The Yard was buzzing with the kinetic energy of returning upperclassmen, lost first-years, the distant thumping bass of the marching band practicing in the humid afternoon air, and the unmistakable, overlapping chatter of thousands of people whose lives were all starting at once.

For Malik Baptiste and Nia Reynolds, the abstraction of college was officially over.

The syllabi had dropped.

## Friday and Saturday: The Architecture of the Semester

On Friday evening, Malik sat at his desk in his dorm, his laptop screen illuminating his face in the dim room. The digital class folders he had so meticulously created during week two were no longer empty monuments to preparation. They were now holding actual documents, actual schedules, and actual expectations.

He had spent the last two hours mapping out his actuarial pathway milestones against the concrete dates listed in the newly published syllabus for MAC 2311—Calculus I.

He stared at the screen, a slight furrow in his brow. The pace outlined in Dr. Brooks’s syllabus was aggressive. It wasn't just the sheer volume of chapters they were expected to cover before the first midterm; it was the depth. High school calculus had felt like a series of distinct, manageable puzzles. This syllabus read like a blueprint for an entirely new language, and they were expected to be fluent by October.

He carefully added the midterm dates, the weekly quiz dates, and the homework deadlines into his master calendar. Every time he typed a new date, the safety net of "prep week" dissolved a little more.

Dr. Price’s voice from an orientation seminar echoed in his mind: *State your assumptions before modeling.*

Malik realized his primary assumption had been that his high school work ethic would neatly map onto college expectations. Looking at the syllabus, he was already recalibrating. The model had to change. The efficiency he prized was going to be tested not by how fast he could work, but by how much complexity he could absorb without breaking his own systems.

A few buildings over, Nia was having a similar reckoning.

She had laid her color-coded note system out across her bed. The planner that she had so carefully negotiated with herself—the weekly balance plan holding study blocks, rest periods, and community engagement—now felt precarious.

She looked at the reading requirements for her mathematical sciences track. She looked at the problem sets. Then she looked at the block she had labeled *Rest*.

"You are going to have to fight for your life," she whispered to the yellow highlighter marking her downtime.

But even with the sudden, heavy weight of the expectations, Nia felt an undeniable surge of excitement. This was what she had wanted. The challenge wasn't a threat; it was an invitation. She was finally going to touch the real thing.

## Monday: The First Bell

Monday morning arrived with a humid, golden light that made the red bricks of Jackson Davis Hall seem to glow from within.

Malik and Nia met near the entrance fifteen minutes before their first Calculus I lecture. The energy around them was palpable. Students were checking room numbers on their phones, shifting heavy backpacks, and trying to project a confidence that most of them didn't yet possess.

"You look ready," Nia said, noting Malik's perfectly straight posture and the notebook already gripped in his hand.

"I look like I know where the room is," Malik corrected. "Ready is a strong word."

"I'll take it," Nia smiled, though her own hands were adjusting the straps of her bag a little more nervously than usual. "Let's go see what we signed up for."

Walking into the classroom, they were immediately struck by the atmosphere. The room was large, the whiteboards expansive, and the murmur of fifty other first-year students created a low hum of collective anxiety and anticipation.

At precisely the start time, Dr. Brooks walked in. He didn't ease them in with icebreakers. He walked straight to the board, picked up a marker, and wrote a complex limit problem before he even introduced himself.

He turned to the class, the marker still in his hand. "Welcome to Calculus I at Florida A&M University. I am Dr. Brooks. And before we talk about textbooks, or office hours, or my expectations, I want someone to tell me not just the answer to this problem, but the story of how you get there."

The room fell completely silent.

"Calculus," Dr. Brooks continued, his voice carrying easily to the back row, "is not a collection of tricks to find an answer. It is a rigorous system of reasoning. In this room, a correct number with poor reasoning is a wrong answer. *Show your reasoning clearly.* If you cannot explain the architecture of your thought process, you do not understand the math."

Malik wrote down *Show your reasoning clearly* at the exact top of his first page of notes. He felt a sudden, sharp thrill. This wasn't about surviving a class; it was about mastering a standard.

Nia was writing furiously, her color-coding system immediately challenged by the speed at which Dr. Brooks moved. He didn't just lecture; he orchestrated. He threw questions to the room, demanded participation, and framed every mathematical concept as a living, breathing tool rather than a static rule.

By the time the fifty-minute lecture ended, the class let out a collective breath they hadn't realized they were holding.

Nia looked at Malik, her eyes wide, a smudge of ink on her thumb. "That was... intense."

"That," Malik said, packing his bag with deliberate care, "was fast. We are not in high school anymore."

## Wednesday: The Friction of the First Assignment

By midweek, the adrenaline of the first day had worn off, replaced by the friction of actual work.

They met in the library on Wednesday afternoon to tackle the first problem set. The campus outside was vibrant, the organizations fair setting up on the quad, music drifting through the large windows. Nia had visited the fair earlier, gathering flyers for the Math Club and a peer tutoring organization, feeling the pull of the community she wanted to be a part of.

But right now, the numbers demanded evidence.

Malik was staring at a problem that seemed straightforward but refused to resolve elegantly. He had erased his setup three times. His frustration was a quiet, contained thing, manifesting as a tightening of his jaw.

"It's not working," Malik said quietly. "The setup is flawed."

Dr. Delgado’s voice, from a department welcome seminar the day before, echoed for him: *Clean setup before solving.*

"You're jumping to the computation," Nia observed, leaning over to look at his page. "You didn't visualize the concept first. Look at the geometry of the function before you try to derive it."

Malik looked at her, then back at the paper. He took a breath, turned to a fresh page, and sketched the graph. The moment he did, the constraint he had been missing became obvious. The tension in his jaw relaxed.

"You're right," he admitted grudgingly.

"I know," Nia said, tapping her own notebook. "Dr. Okafor said it yesterday at the welcome mixer. Visualize first. But knowing it and doing it under pressure are two different things."

They worked in companionable silence for another hour. It was hard work. The problems required a level of synthesis they weren't used to. They couldn't just pattern-match; they had to build logical bridges from scratch.

But as they packed up, Malik looked at his completed, heavily annotated assignment. He felt a profound sense of satisfaction. It wasn't perfect, but it was earned.

## Friday: The First Real Test Looms

By Friday, the week had exacted a physical toll. The transition from the relaxed rhythms of summer to the relentless cadence of the FAMU math department had left them both exhausted.

But it was a good exhaustion. The kind that comes from being stretched, not broken.

They sat on a bench outside Jackson Davis Hall in the late afternoon. The week was technically over. They had survived the syllabus shock. They had navigated the first lectures. They had completed the first grueling problem sets.

"We made it," Nia said, leaning her head back against the brick wall and closing her eyes. "One week down. Only... fourteen more to go in the semester."

"Don't do the math on that," Malik said, though he was smiling slightly. "Just take the win for today."

"I am. I'm going to sleep for twelve hours, and then I'm going to actually look at those club flyers."

Malik pulled out his planner to check off the week's final tasks. As he did, he paused.

"What is it?" Nia asked, opening one eye to look at him.

Malik traced a line on the page with his pen. "Dr. Brooks's announcement at the end of class today. Did you catch it?"

Nia sat up, the exhaustion suddenly clearing from her face. "The quiz."

"First quiz. First thing Monday morning," Malik confirmed, his voice devoid of its usual calm certainty. "Covering everything from this week. And he specifically said it would test our reasoning, not just our computation."

The warm Friday evening suddenly felt a little cooler. The accomplishment of surviving the first week was instantly eclipsed by the looming reality of the first assessment.

Preparation was over. The lectures were over. The practice problems were done.

Monday morning would bring the first real test of their systems, their understanding, and their nerve. The grace period had expired.

Nia looked at Malik, the competitive spark returning to her eyes despite her fatigue. "Well," she said, her voice steadying. "We wanted to know what the real thing felt like."

Malik closed his planner with a decisive snap.

"The real thing," he said, looking toward the math building, "starts Monday."
