# Week Forty-One: The Summer That Started with a Question

The invitation waiting at the end of Week Forty did not fade when the next week began.

If one year at FAMU could change Malik and Nia this much, what might come next?

Week Forty-One answered that question the way real life often does—not with one dramatic reveal, but through a shift in rhythm. The pressure of spring had loosened. Summer A had not fully settled in yet. Athletics still pulsed through campus life, with FAMU baseball pushing through an important stretch against Alabama A&M and then returning home to face Texas Southern. The campus atmosphere carried both motion and pause at once. Students who had just come through the long tunnel of finals were breathing differently now, but that breath did not mean the story had gone still.

It meant a new chapter was beginning.

Friday, May 8, opened in that in-between mood.

The spring semester was over, but summer had not yet taken on its full academic shape. There was space in the day, a little more softness around the edges of campus, but not emptiness. FAMU still felt alive. Conversations drifted through walkways and common areas—baseball updates from Alabama A&M, laughter from students who finally looked less burdened, and the familiar undercurrent of people asking each other some version of the same question:

“So what’s next for you?”

Malik had been asking himself that all morning.

He sat outside with his laptop open, a clean copy of his resume on screen and a notebook beside it full of revisions, crossed-out phrases, and bullet points that tried to capture a year larger than any single document could hold. He was polishing language again, but this time the task felt less mechanical than it had the week before. He was no longer just trying to make the page look professional. He was trying to make it truthful.

He had learned enough by now to know that truth mattered in the small things. Clean verbs. Clear evidence. No inflated claims. No vague filler pretending to be accomplishment.

He stared at one section for a while, then sighed.

“How do you summarize growth without making it sound like a motivational poster?” he muttered.

“Very carefully,” Nia said as she approached.

He looked up and laughed under his breath. “You always arrive exactly when a sentence needs judging.”

“I prefer the term editorial intervention.” She dropped into the seat across from him and glanced at the screen. “Let me see.”

Nia had a journal tucked under one arm, already marked with tabs and sticky notes. While Malik had spent the morning reshaping career language, she had been doing a different kind of sorting—looking back over her year not to archive every event, but to name what had actually changed in her.

She read a few lines from Malik’s resume draft, then shook her head.

“This is fine,” she said.

“That sounded painful.”

“It should sound painful. ‘Fine’ is not where your best work lives.”

Malik leaned back. “All right, Professor Reynolds. What’s wrong with it?”

“You’re still describing what you did, but not how you did it.” She pointed to the screen. “This year wasn’t just assignments and meetings. It was discipline under pressure. It was improvement after setbacks. It was learning how to think more clearly. Put some of that in here.”

He considered that. “Without sounding dramatic?”

“Without sounding fake,” she corrected. “That’s different.”

That was one of the reasons their conversations worked better now than they had in the fall. They no longer heard challenge as criticism. They heard it as respect.

Across campus, news from the baseball series at Alabama A&M gave the day a current of energy. Even students who were not diehard sports fans paid attention during stretches like this. FAMU pride had a way of becoming collective. Someone always had a score update. Someone always knew who had made a big play. Someone always said “Rattlers” with enough feeling to remind everybody that belonging at FAMU was not abstract. It showed up in classrooms, residence halls, rehearsals, student meetings, and game-day conversations alike.

Nia spent part of that first day journaling under a shaded tree near a quieter edge of campus. She wrote not only about growth, but about gratitude. She wrote about how the FAMU Mathematics Department had challenged her without flattening her personality. She wrote about professors whose standards were high enough to feel serious and whose mentorship was personal enough to feel human. She wrote about the difference between being busy and being useful, a lesson she had paid for in stress earlier in the year.

At the bottom of one page she wrote a sentence, underlined it once, and sat with it for a long moment.

_I do not have to prove my value by being everywhere at once._

It was not a flashy sentence.

But it was hard-won.

Saturday, May 9, moved more gently.

Baseball at Alabama A&M remained part of the day’s background pulse, but Malik’s focus shifted toward rest and reset. That choice, simple as it seemed, still carried meaning. Earlier in the academic year, he would have struggled to rest without secretly accusing himself of laziness. Now he treated recovery with more intelligence. He cleaned his desk. Organized old notes into neat stacks. Took a longer walk than usual. Ate slowly instead of hunched over work. Let silence exist without filling it immediately.

At one point he caught himself reaching for a practice sheet out of reflex and paused.

Dr. Delgado’s voice came back to him from the semester: clean setup before solving.

He smiled at the thought.

Apparently that principle applied to more than calculus.

Before throwing energy at the next challenge, set the space right. Set the mind right. Set the assumptions right.

Meanwhile, Nia previewed upcoming topics with a lighter touch than she would have used months ago. She sat with a notebook, sketched out a few ideas, and read ahead just enough to feel the edges of what summer might demand. She was not trying to dominate the material before class began. She was introducing herself to it.

When Malik found her later, she turned the notebook around so he could see a page full of arrows, circles, and labeled concepts.

“Concept map?” he asked.

“Very unofficial one.”

“It looks official. Also mildly threatening.”

She grinned. “That’s how you know it’s mathematics.”

Sunday, May 10, deepened the sense that this week was a bridge.

There was family time for Malik, light review folded into the day, and a creative reset for Nia that included reading something non-technical simply because she wanted to. Those choices mattered. They gave the week texture. They made clear that FAMU student life, especially for students learning to thrive rather than merely survive, included more than assignment calendars and assessment windows.

Malik’s family conversations grounded him. Nia’s reading widened her emotional breathing room. And around them, campus continued to hum with its own layered life—baseball headlines, students coming and going, conversations about summer plans, and that subtle sense that everyone was orienting themselves again.

Then Monday, May 11, arrived, and Summer A officially began.

The week’s first real turn happened there.

Even before classes got fully underway, the atmosphere shifted. Not into panic—nothing like the opening of fall semester—but into renewed intention. Summer courses carried a reputation. Faster pace. Less wasted time. Smaller margin for drifting.

Malik felt that immediately when a calculus problem set was assigned.

He stared at the assignment notice for a second, then let out one short breath through his nose.

“Here we go,” he said.

There was no dread in it. Only recognition.

This was the next stretch of the climb.

He spent part of the day updating an internship and career checklist, making sure practical goals stayed attached to academic effort. That was another sign of growth: he could now see coursework and future planning as connected instead of competing priorities. Across campus, Nia stepped into a student organization planning meeting, bringing the stronger boundaries she had learned this year with her.

She still cared deeply. She still contributed thoughtfully. But she no longer said yes automatically.

At one point someone asked if she could take on an extra organizing role.

Old Nia might have agreed before thinking.

This time she smiled and said, “I can help with strategy and one deliverable, but I don’t want to overpromise.”

It was a small moment.

It was also a victory.

Later that afternoon, Dr. Bennett’s familiar guidance surfaced through a class exchange: interpret numbers as evidence. The words landed with fresh relevance at the start of summer. In a compressed term, every quiz, draft, and problem set could feel amplified if students were not careful. But evidence could guide; it did not have to condemn.

Tuesday, May 12, brought some of the week’s clearest examples of the pair’s maturing roles.

Malik carved out a two-hour deep-work block in the library and protected it with unusual calm. No checking messages every few minutes. No pretending multitasking was efficient. He set up his materials, wrote down what he intended to finish, and worked the way he had learned to work when he was at his best—fully present.

Nearby, Nia led a whiteboard study circle that drew exactly the kind of students FAMU’s undergraduate research and mentorship culture so often helps shape: curious, earnest, sharp, and unafraid to learn together. She did not perform expertise for the room. She facilitated thinking.

“All right,” she said, marker in hand, “before anybody solves anything, what assumptions are we making?”

A student near the edge answered cautiously. “That the model fits the conditions we think it does?”

Nia pointed at him. “Good. Keep going.”

Another student jumped in. “And that we’re not skipping over definitions just because the notation looks familiar.”

“That too,” Nia said. “Exactly.”

Dr. Price would have approved. State assumptions before modeling. It was becoming one of those principles that reached beyond a single course and into a way of carrying yourself intellectually.

Malik passed by the room after his library block and paused at the doorway, watching for a moment. Nia was in her element—not because she needed the spotlight, but because she had become skilled at drawing other people into the work.

That was leadership. Not noise. Not performance. Not control.

Wednesday, May 13, tightened the week’s tension.

A quiz or concept-check window always changed the atmosphere, even in summer. The campus energy grew a little sharper, a little more focused. Malik ran timed calculus drills and found himself wrestling with a familiar old temptation: to speed up whenever uncertainty appeared.

This time he stopped.

Dr. Brooks’s advice rose in his mind with perfect clarity: show your reasoning clearly.

So he slowed down instead.

The result was not dramatic in the moment. No movie-scene breakthrough. No instant transformation.

Just cleaner work.

Better choices.

Fewer avoidable mistakes.

Sometimes that was what progress looked like.

Nia spent the day rewriting notes into a concept map, turning scattered information into structure. When they compared their work later, Malik laughed.

“You turned the whole topic into a city map.”

“And you turned yours into a stopwatch competition,” she replied.

“Balanced ecosystem.”

“Exactly.”

That night, the week’s unresolved thread began to sharpen. Summer A had started faster than either of them could ignore. The rhythm was real now. And with it came a familiar question in a new form:

Had they truly grown enough to meet a faster season without slipping back into old habits?

Thursday, May 14, gave the week its emotional payoff and its next-pointing tension all at once.

FAMU baseball was back home against Texas Southern University, and that brought a special brightness to campus. Home-game energy always felt different—more immediate, more communal, more visibly woven into student life. The same campus that housed deep work in libraries and careful questions in office hours also made room for cheers, pride, color, and collective spirit. That fullness mattered. It reminded Malik and Nia that excellence at FAMU was never meant to be isolated from community.

Malik attended office hours with targeted questions instead of vague anxiety. That alone would have marked growth. He no longer approached faculty support like a confession of weakness. He approached it like a serious student making disciplined use of a serious opportunity.

Dr. Delgado listened to one of his questions, looked over his setup, and nodded.

“You’re thinking more clearly,” she said. “Cleaner start. Better structure.”

Malik felt a quiet satisfaction at that—not because he needed praise, but because he recognized the truth of it.

Across the day, Nia helped a peer work through a difficult concept. She did not just give the answer. She walked them through the shape of the confusion, where the jump had happened, and how to rebuild understanding one piece at a time. It was the kind of scene the FAMU Mathematics Department seemed built to encourage: students helping students, rigor held inside generosity, confidence growing through community.

By evening, with baseball excitement alive around them and summer coursework already proving its pace, Malik and Nia found themselves walking across campus again, carrying books and thoughts and that familiar sense that one week had become more meaningful than it looked on paper.

“So,” Nia said, glancing over at him, “what comes next?”

Malik smiled. “You really like asking impossible questions.”

“I like asking accurate ones.”

He looked ahead before answering.

“More work,” he said. “More chances to do it better. More proof that this year changed us for real.”

Nia nodded slowly. “Yeah. That sounds right.”

The week ended there—not with closure, but with momentum.

Week Forty-One had resolved the glow of anticipation from the previous chapter by showing that the next stage was not some distant future. It had already begun. In quiet revisions, better boundaries, whiteboard leadership, office-hour confidence, and cleaner thinking, Malik and Nia had stepped into the summer version of themselves.

And yet the new excitement was unmistakable.

Summer A was moving quickly. The pace would test them. The old habits they had outgrown would still try to reappear. New responsibilities, new opportunities, and a faster academic rhythm were already gathering just beyond the edge of this week.

But this time the tension felt earned.

Not the fear of whether they belonged.

The challenge of how far they could go now that they knew they did.
