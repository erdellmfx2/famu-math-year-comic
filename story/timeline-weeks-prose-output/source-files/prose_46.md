# Week 46 Prose — June 12 to June 18, 2026

By the time Friday morning settled over Florida A&M University, the excitement from the undergraduate research information session had not faded at all.

It had sharpened.

That was the first thing Malik realized when he sat down outside the mathematics building with a notebook balanced on one knee and a cup of coffee cooling faster than he was drinking it. The question that had closed the previous week—*which door would they knock on first?*—had not lingered as a vague, dramatic thought. It had followed him into the practical details of summer life, into his planning, into the way he looked at office hours, assignments, and even the small choices that filled an ordinary day.

Because now the future no longer felt like a distant category called *later.* It felt close enough to schedule.

Across campus, the summer atmosphere moved with a steady, bright calm. There was none of the overcrowded pressure of the main fall rush, but the university was far from sleepy. Students still crossed the walkways with purpose. Faculty doors still opened and closed. Study groups still formed around tables, whiteboards, and hallway corners. FAMU in the summer had a different music to it—less loud, maybe, but more deliberate. The mathematics department seemed to breathe in that rhythm especially well. It felt like a place where serious work could happen without performance, where growth could be visible in quiet ways.

Malik wrote a heading at the top of the page:

**Next Steps: Research, Career, and Coursework**

Then, beneath it, he made himself stop.

A few months ago, he would have rushed into listing tasks just to soothe the anxiety of wanting progress. But this year had taught him that clean structure began with honest thinking, not frantic movement. So he sat still for a moment and asked himself what Dr. Brooks, Dr. Delgado, Dr. Bennett, Dr. Price, and even Dr. Okafor had all been teaching him in different language for months.

Show your reasoning clearly.
Clean setup before solving.
Interpret numbers as evidence.
State assumptions before modeling.
Visualize the concept first.

Those principles were not just for class anymore. They were becoming a way of living.

So he wrote the actual first question:

**What am I really trying to build?**

The answer came more easily than he expected. Not a perfect identity. Not a polished future he could present without uncertainty. He was trying to build readiness. The kind that came from disciplined habits, clear communication, and the courage to approach opportunities before feeling fully qualified.

That thought stayed with him later that afternoon when he headed to office hours.

The summer emphasis on office hours and study hall had started as a syllabus feature. By now, it felt more like an invitation into the department’s culture. Students who used those spaces well learned more than content. They learned how mathematicians actually talked through confusion, how professors responded to partial understanding, and how serious thinking sounded when it was still under construction.

Malik arrived with targeted questions, just as he had promised himself he would. Not a pile of panic. A page marked with places where his reasoning became thin.

Dr. Brooks looked over one of his solutions, then tapped a line near the center.

“You got where you needed to go,” he said, “but you made one leap too quickly.”

Malik leaned forward. “Here?”

“Yes. The conclusion is fine. But if someone reads your work, they should be able to see how you knew this transition was justified.”

Malik nodded slowly. “So even when I’m right, I need to make the reasoning visible.”

Dr. Brooks smiled. “Especially then. Hidden reasoning is where fragile understanding likes to hide.”

It was such a Dr. Brooks sentence—gentle, exact, impossible to forget.

Malik rewrote the section right there, adding the missing explanation. The mathematics looked almost the same at the end, but the logic felt stronger, more honest. He thought again about research, about future conversations with faculty, about how much of becoming useful in academic spaces might simply begin with being willing to show your incomplete thinking clearly.

At nearly the same time, Nia was across campus stepping into a student organization planning meeting with a legal pad, two pens, and the focused expression she wore when she had decided she was not going to let a meeting drift.

Summer student life at FAMU had its own particular energy. Even when attendance was lighter and calendars were thinner, there were still students willing to imagine what campus life could become if someone cared enough to shape it. Nia loved that. She loved the idea that community did not just happen on its own. People built it. Protected it. Expanded it.

The meeting began with the usual scatter of updates, half-finished ideas, and well-meaning enthusiasm. In earlier semesters, Nia might have said yes to every suggestion, every side role, every extra obligation that made her feel useful. But she had learned too much to do that now. She had paid for that lesson already.

So when discussion turned toward upcoming events and academic outreach, she spoke with a steadier kind of leadership.

“If we want students to show up,” she said, “we need to make the opportunity feel real. Not abstract. Not like something only a few people are born knowing how to do.”

A few heads nodded.

“I’m thinking especially about math students,” she continued. “Research, study circles, mentoring, career planning—those shouldn’t feel like secret doors. We should make them visible. Welcoming. Reachable.”

One of the upperclassmen smiled. “That sounds like somebody who’s been thinking about this for more than one meeting.”

Nia laughed. “That’s because I have.”

It was true. Ever since the information session, something in her had become more focused. She was not just thinking about what she personally might do next. She was thinking about what it meant for the FAMU mathematics community to keep lifting students into wider visions of themselves. She had benefited from that culture. She wanted to help strengthen it.

Saturday brought a pair of scenes that looked quiet from the outside and important from the inside.

Malik claimed two hours in the library for a deep-work block and treated it like a promise. The library in summer had a clean, patient feel—light across the tables, air-conditioning humming softly, the occasional rustle of backpack zippers and notebook pages. Malik worked through problem sets deliberately, pausing to mark not only errors but patterns. Where did time pressure make him untidy? Where did confidence make him skip setup? Where did he understand enough to continue, but not enough to explain?

He was no longer measuring himself only by completion. He was studying his own habits as if they were data.

Across another part of campus, Nia led a whiteboard study circle that began with the usual uncertainty of students pretending they were less confused than they were. She had grown good at recognizing that posture. Instead of asking, “Does everyone get it?”—a question that often invited silence—she uncapped a marker and wrote a different prompt at the top of the board:

**What part of this idea still feels slippery?**

That changed everything.

One student admitted that they could perform the procedure but could not explain why it worked. Another said they understood the graph but lost the algebra. A third confessed that timed quizzes made every concept feel worse than it actually was.

“Good,” Nia said, not because they were struggling, but because they were finally being specific. “Now we can do something with that.”

The study circle turned lively after that. Students started speaking to each other instead of just waiting for rescue. Nia guided, redirected, and occasionally paused everyone to draw a concept map so the whole group could see the relationships between ideas. She was not merely helping people solve that day’s problem set. She was teaching them how to notice the shape of their own thinking.

That afternoon, when she ran into Malik near the student union, he could tell immediately that the session had gone well.

“You’ve got the marker-smudge look again,” he said.

She glanced at her hand and laughed. “Occupational hazard.”

“How’d the circle go?”

“Better once everybody stopped pretending confusion was a personal failure.”

Malik nodded. “That should honestly be printed on the wall.”

Nia adjusted the strap of her bag. “How was the library?”

He considered the question. “Productive. Also humbling. I’m realizing how often my first instinct is still to rush clean-looking work instead of fully reasoned work.”

“That’s growth, though,” she said. “Noticing it sooner.”

They walked a little farther together under the warm June sky, the kind that made campus trees look vivid and forgiving at the same time.

On Sunday, the homework-due rhythm arrived right on schedule, along with the weekly planning reset that summer classes demanded. The week had not been chaotic, but summer coursework had a way of punishing drift anyway. That was part of its discipline. If you let one day slide too far, the next day arrived already asking for more.

Malik spent part of the afternoon doing timed calculus drills, but with a new layer of attention. Instead of merely racing the clock, he practiced beginning well. Dr. Delgado’s advice echoed through his mind: clean setup before solving. So he made himself write the structure first—the givens, the form, the likely path—before moving his pencil into full motion.

The strange thing was that it did not make him slower in the way he feared. It made him steadier.

Nia, meanwhile, spread her notes out and rewrote the week’s main concepts into a fresh map, using arrows, labels, and small sketches to connect ideas that otherwise might have stayed isolated. She could feel Dr. Okafor’s influence in that habit now. Visualize the concept first. If the picture made sense, the symbols stopped feeling like a wall and started feeling like language.

By Monday, a new calculus problem set had arrived, and with it the familiar challenge of balancing ordinary academic work with larger aspirations. That tension was healthy. Neither Malik nor Nia wanted to become students who loved the idea of research, leadership, or future success more than the daily habits required to support any of it.

Malik updated his internship and career checklist again, this time with more specific notes about faculty interests, skill areas he wanted to strengthen, and possible ways actuarial thinking might connect with broader mathematical inquiry. The page was beginning to look less like a dream journal and more like a roadmap.

Nia spent the evening helping a peer work through a difficult concept rather than simply handing over a finished explanation. She asked questions, drew a visual model, then asked the peer to explain it back in their own words.

When they finally did, their whole face changed.

“Oh,” they said. “I thought I was missing everything. I was really missing the picture.”

Nia smiled. “That happens more than people think.”

It was one of the quiet joys of the week: watching understanding return to people in real time.

Tuesday gave them another round of student-org planning and future-facing thought. Malik returned to his checklist. Nia returned to community-building. FAMU kept surrounding both of them with the same message in different forms—that student success was not supposed to happen in isolation. Mentorship mattered. Peer learning mattered. Showing up mattered. The mathematics department, in particular, kept proving that rigor and support were not opposites. They were partners.

Wednesday opened the quiz and concept-check window, and even though it was not the biggest assessment of the term, it carried enough weight to test their habits honestly. Malik went into it calmer than he would have months earlier. Nia entered with her mind organized around relationships between ideas rather than memorized fragments.

Afterward, they met outside, both trying not to overread every moment.

“How do you feel?” Nia asked.

“Better than I usually do right after a quiz,” Malik admitted. “Which probably means I either grew or I’m about to be surprised.”

She laughed. “I think it means you grew.”

“And you?”

“I think I actually understood what I was looking at,” she said. “Not perfectly. But enough to think while solving.”

“That’s huge.”

“It is.”

The week might have ended there as simply another good summer week—steady, disciplined, quietly affirming. But Thursday gave it the spark that turned steadiness into momentum.

Late that afternoon, after another round of calculus drills for Malik and another concept-map rewrite for Nia, they met outside the mathematics building again. The air was warm, the walkways glowing in the long light of early evening, and the department behind them carried that familiar sense of possibility it had been building all summer.

Malik had his notebook out.

Nia noticed immediately. “That look again,” she said. “What did you do?”

He turned the notebook toward her.

At the top of the page was a short list of faculty names. Beneath each name were one or two careful notes about possible interests, relevant courses, and questions worth asking.

Nia read it, then looked up with a slow smile. “You actually made the list.”

“I did.”

“Have you emailed anybody yet?”

He hesitated. “Not yet.”

“Why not?”

Malik exhaled. “Because making a list feels responsible. Sending the email feels real.”

Nia held that answer for a moment, then nodded. “Fair.”

He looked at her. “You?”

She opened her bag, pulled out her own notebook, and showed him a page headed:

**Questions for a Faculty Conversation**

This time he laughed first. “So we’re both pretending preparation isn’t the same thing as bravery.”

“Exactly.”

They stood there under the soft evening light, both a little amused and both completely aware that the week had brought them to a real threshold. They had done the coursework. Used office hours. Led study circles. Helped peers. Built better habits. Paid attention to professor guidance. Imagined wider futures. The excitement from last week had not vanished into vague inspiration. It had condensed into something sharper and more demanding.

Action.

Nia closed her notebook. “Okay,” she said. “New rule.”

Malik raised an eyebrow. “I’m listening.”

“Before this weekend is over, we each contact one faculty member.”

He stared at her for half a second, then smiled the way people do when they recognize a challenge they actually need. “One message. One real door.”

“One real door,” she repeated.

Behind them, the mathematics building stood warm in the last of the light, less like a backdrop than like a witness. Around them, FAMU felt beautifully alive in its summer form—students growing quietly, mentors shaping futures, community making ambition feel possible.

Malik looked back at the names on the page.

This time, the uncertainty was still there, but it no longer felt like a warning.

It felt like the exact edge of becoming.

And as they started walking, already talking through how to write a first email that was serious without being stiff, hopeful without being naïve, the week closed not with a dramatic answer but with a charged, unresolved beginning.

The next step was small enough to fit in a single message.

Which, somehow, made it feel even bigger.
