# Week Thirteen: The Week Readiness Had to Become Real

The resolution came early, which was probably the only reason the rest of the week could hold as much as it did.

After ending the previous week with that sharpened awareness—that feeling that the semester was about to stop asking whether Malik and Nia were trying and start revealing whether their new habits could actually withstand pressure—they arrived at Wednesday’s concept check carrying more than notebooks and pencils. They carried proof of the versions of themselves they had been building all month.

Not perfect versions.
Not fearless versions.
But sturdier ones.

Malik felt it as soon as he sat down.

A few weeks earlier, the beginning of a quiz would have filled his mind with static: timing, comparison, dread, the need to look composed even while internally unraveling. This time, when the paper landed in front of him, he heard Dr. Price’s instruction before panic could get the first word.

*State assumptions before modeling.*

Even in calculus, even in a short concept check, the advice helped. Slow down. Name what the problem is asking. See the shape of it before trying to outrun it.

He did not start writing immediately. He read every question once. Then again. He marked what was familiar, what needed care, and where he was most likely to lose points by rushing. It was not dramatic from the outside. Nobody looking across the room would have seen a breakthrough. But from inside Malik’s mind, it felt like a quiet revolution.

Across campus, Nia was doing something equally important. She was resisting the urge to perform confidence.

Nia had always known how to sound ready, even on the days when her thoughts were still arranging themselves. This week she decided that real strength meant meeting the material honestly. She underlined the language in each question, built tiny concept anchors in the margins, and treated uncertainty as information instead of embarrassment.

When the check ended, neither of them floated out of the room glowing with certainty. That was the point. They did not need instant emotional fireworks to know something had gone differently.

They met later on a brick path outside the mathematics building, moving with that half-casual, half-searching energy students carried after an assessment.

“Well?” Nia asked.

Malik tipped his head from side to side. “Not easy. But not chaos.”

She smiled. “That sounds almost healthy.”

“I know. I’m concerned.”

Nia laughed, then exhaled. “Same. I had to stop myself twice from writing what sounded right instead of what I could actually justify.”

Malik looked at her more closely. “And you stopped?”

“I stopped.”

He nodded once, satisfied. “That’s growth.”

“Wow,” she said. “You say one emotionally intelligent thing and suddenly you think you can mentor people.”

“Just you,” he said.

That exchange carried them into the rest of the week with a steadier footing than the previous one had allowed. The tension at the end of Week Twelve had not vanished; it had been answered, at least in part, by action. The challenge was no longer abstract. They had met it. Now they had to keep going.

Friday opened quietly, with the kind of ordinary campus rhythm that rarely looks cinematic until you realize how much a life is being built inside it.

Students crossed the yard with coffee and folders tucked under their arms. Someone was reciting definitions aloud while walking. A bike clicked past. The mathematics department hallway smelled faintly like dry-erase ink and paper and the kind of effort that accumulates in rooms where people return to difficult ideas until those ideas finally soften.

Malik claimed a table in the library for a two-hour deep-work block and treated it with a seriousness that would have amused the version of him from August. He set a timer. He stacked only the materials he needed. He wrote the date at the top of the page and, beneath it, a sentence that had started becoming one of his private rules:

**Clean setup. Calm mind. Honest work.**

The phrase borrowed a little from Dr. Delgado, a little from his own hard-earned experience.

He worked through problem after problem, and when he got stuck, he did not lunge for shortcuts. He traced the structure. He named what the problem required. He checked whether he had carried assumptions he had never actually earned. By the end of the block he was mentally tired, but it was the satisfying kind of tired—the kind that comes from building something solid instead of from thrashing.

Meanwhile, Nia was doing what she did best: turning group study into a living academic culture.

That afternoon she led a whiteboard study circle in one of the shared spaces near the department, and the room filled in layers. A couple of students arrived early. Three more drifted in after another class. One student came with the specific look of someone hoping not to be called on. Another brought three colored markers and the emotional intensity of someone prepared to battle derivatives personally.

Nia took one look at the growing cluster and clapped once.

“Okay,” she said. “Today we do not fake understanding. Today we build it.”

“That sounds threatening,” one student muttered.

“It is supportive threat,” Nia replied.

Laughter moved through the room, loosening shoulders almost instantly.

That was part of what made her effective. She did not flatten rigor into friendliness, and she did not make seriousness feel cold. She could hold people to a high standard while making them feel welcome enough to try.

Malik arrived halfway through and stayed along the side wall at first, watching her guide the session. She began with a conceptual sketch before letting anyone touch an equation. She asked students to explain why a method fit, not just whether it produced a number. When someone stumbled publicly, she did not rush to rescue them. She made space for the thought to unfold.

“Start with what you do know,” she told one student, handing over the marker. “Not what you think you should know.”

The student swallowed, nodded, and began.

Malik felt something shift while he watched. He had always respected Nia’s intelligence. But seeing her in this role—anchored, generous, sharp, and fully part of the learning life of the department—made him realize how much FAMU was giving both of them beyond coursework. This was not just a place that assigned mathematics. It cultivated mathematicians. Not only through lectures and exams, but through office hours, peer teaching, whiteboard circles, hallway conversations, and the unspoken expectation that students could grow into leadership while they were still learning.

That culture showed itself even more clearly on Saturday, when game-day energy swept through campus.

Football at Southern meant many students were following updates from afar, and the mood across campus had that bright, communal electricity that always made the day feel larger than itself. School colors stood out more vividly. Voices carried farther. Conversations moved easily between scores, classes, deadlines, and weekend plans.

Malik and Nia found each other outside the student center after each had already protected the morning for academics.

“You did the timer drills, didn’t you?” Nia asked.

“I did,” Malik said.

“Voluntarily?”

“Why do you always say it like I’ve escaped from somewhere?”

“Because you look suspiciously responsible on a Saturday.”

He laughed. “And you rewrote your notes into another giant concept map?”

She lifted her chin. “A beautiful one.”

They spent part of the afternoon inside the pulse of campus life, letting themselves enjoy it without pretending the semester had paused. That balance mattered. FAMU’s student life did not pull against academic identity in this story; it gave it context. Pride, joy, belonging, and discipline were not enemies. They were threads in the same year.

At one point, while standing near a group of students discussing the game, Malik ended up explaining a calculus setup to a classmate who had confessed he kept getting lost once a timer was involved.

Malik surprised himself by not answering with technique first.

“What’s the first thing you do when the clock starts?” he asked.

“Panic a little,” the student admitted.

“Yeah,” Malik said. “Don’t start there. Start by identifying what kind of problem it is. Buy yourself ten calm seconds. That’s cheaper than three minutes of confused algebra.”

Nia, who was listening nearby, glanced at him with obvious delight. “Look at you,” she said when the student walked away. “You’re becoming one of those people who says useful things casually.”

Malik made a face. “I hate that for me.”

She grinned. “I love it for the department.”

Sunday narrowed the energy again. Homework was due. Weekly planning returned. Men’s basketball at Florida State gave the day another pulse of school spirit, but underneath that pulse sat the practical reality of reset.

Malik went to office hours with targeted questions instead of diffuse worry, and the change was noticeable even before he spoke. Dr. Delgado looked over his notes, saw the way each problem was marked with a specific point of confusion, and gave a small nod that Malik understood immediately as professional respect.

“Better,” Dr. Delgado said.

Malik almost smiled. “I thought you’d say that.”

“You are making the work visible now,” she replied. “That matters. Clean setup before solving. Always.”

The conversation that followed was short, precise, and valuable. No dramatics. No rescue. Just instruction meeting preparation halfway.

Later that afternoon, Nia helped a peer unravel a difficult concept, but she was more intentional than she had been earlier in the semester. She did not take ownership of someone else’s learning just because she could. She asked questions. She paced the explanation. She left room for the other student to think. It was a subtle act of maturity—one that reflected the lesson she herself had been learning about sustainability, boundaries, and leadership that multiplies rather than exhausts.

By Monday, a new calculus problem set had been assigned, and the week turned again toward the future.

Malik used part of the day to update his internship and career checklist, which no longer felt like an act of pretending to be a future professional. It felt like a continuation of the same discipline he was practicing in class: notice what matters, make the next decision, do not wait for some imaginary perfect version of yourself to begin.

Nia headed into a student organization planning meeting and came out energized but thoughtful. She had ideas. She had invitations. She had opportunities that genuinely mattered. She also had a growing awareness that every yes shaped the quality of the other yeses around it.

When they compared notes later, sitting outside as evening lowered itself gently over campus, their conversation had the tone of people who were no longer just surviving the semester one alarm at a time.

“We’re getting better,” Nia said quietly.

Malik looked toward the mathematics building before answering. “Yeah. But better doesn’t mean settled.”

“No,” she said. “It means responsible.”

That word stayed between them.

Tuesday brought another deep-work block for Malik and another whiteboard study circle for Nia, but by then the week had started accumulating a different kind of pressure. Not crisis. Not even immediate panic. Something more interesting.

Momentum.

The systems were working.
The professors were noticing.
Their peers were starting to rely on them.
The department was beginning to reflect back versions of themselves that looked more capable than the ones they had arrived with.

And with that came a new, sharper question:

What would happen when opportunity arrived before they felt fully ready for it?

The answer began to stir on Thursday.

Nia left a conversation with a faculty mentor carrying the possibility of a larger student leadership role than she had expected to be considered for this early. Malik, meanwhile, heard from an upperclassman about an upcoming career-development event that could open doors if he was willing to put himself forward now, not later.

Neither opportunity had fully materialized yet.
That almost made them more powerful.

Because by the end of the week, as campus settled into the soft orange light of late afternoon and students crossed the yard carrying books, jerseys, questions, and futures they were still naming, Malik and Nia both felt it:

The semester was no longer only testing whether they could recover from difficulty.
It was beginning to ask whether they were ready to be seen.

They stood for a moment outside the mathematics building, not speaking right away.

Nia broke the silence first. “I think the next part might be bigger than quizzes.”

Malik nodded slowly. “Yeah. I think so too.”

She looked at him, excitement and nerves alive in equal measure. “That should probably terrify me more than it does.”

“It might tomorrow,” he said.

She laughed softly. “True.”

Then she grew serious again, eyes bright with that particular kind of hope that only appears when fear and ambition finally decide to share the same room.

“But I also think,” she said, “we’ve been building toward this without realizing it.”

Malik looked back at the department doors, at the place that had challenged them, corrected them, steadied them, and quietly insisted they were capable of more.

“Then I guess,” he said, “next week we find out what that more looks like.”

That was how the week closed: not with disaster, not with relief alone, but with the thrilling unease of students who had answered one kind of pressure and could now feel another one approaching.

This time the unknown was not only about whether they might fail.

It was about whether they might truly be ready to rise.
