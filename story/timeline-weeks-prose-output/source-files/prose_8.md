# Week Eight: The Week of Steady Building

Friday morning began with the kind of relief that did not arrive loudly.

It came in a small breath.
A quick glance.
A phone screen lit beneath fluorescent hallway lights.
A number that did not solve everything, but settled something.

Malik Baptiste stood just outside Jackson Davis Hall with his backpack over one shoulder and finally checked the grade he had refused to open the evening before. Students moved around him in uneven currents, some walking fast to class, some lingering in little circles of conversation, some pretending not to care while clearly caring a great deal. The campus was awake in that particular FAMU way—warm sunlight already spreading across brick and grass, voices rising from the walkway, banners lifting in a soft breeze—but Malik heard almost none of it for the first second after the grade loaded.

Then he exhaled.

Not perfect.
Not disappointing.
Strong.
Strong enough to confirm that the long nights, the targeted questions, the mistake logs in the margins of his notebook, and the deliberate slowing down had all amounted to something real.

He stared one moment longer, not out of vanity, but out of disbelief that steadiness could feel this satisfying.

“Good?” a voice asked.

He looked up to find Nia Reynolds approaching with a coffee cup in one hand and an expression already halfway to a smirk.

Malik locked his phone and nodded once. “Good enough to justify my personality.”

Nia laughed. “That sounds exactly like you.” She lifted her own phone. “Mine too. Not dramatic. Just… reassuring.”

They fell into step together toward class, and for a few moments neither of them said much. That silence was not awkward. It was earned. Last week’s tension had not vanished, but it had changed its nature. The question was no longer whether they could survive college mathematics at FAMU. The evidence, imperfect but persuasive, said yes.

Now the question was what to build on top of survival.

By the time they reached the building entrance, Nia tapped the folded research symposium flyer peeking out from Malik’s notebook.

“So,” she said, lowering her voice just enough to make it feel serious, “are we actually going to do something with that, or are we just carrying it around like a symbol of future greatness?”

Malik glanced at it. “I think this week we stop being hypothetical.”

Nia’s grin sharpened. “Now that is the most exciting thing you’ve said before 9 a.m.”

Inside, the day unfolded without spectacle, which turned out to be exactly what the week needed.

There was no football-game spike, no all-campus disruption, no crisis pressing down from every direction. Instead there was the subtler challenge of ordinary discipline: the kind of week that reveals whether growth is real because nobody is forcing it into visibility.

Later that morning, Malik sat in a side room near office hours with three pages of targeted calculus questions. Not panicked questions. Not “help me because everything is broken” questions. Focused ones. He had marked each problem with colored tabs: where his timing slipped, where his assumptions got muddy, where he could arrive at a correct answer while still feeling conceptually late.

Dr. Price reviewed one of Malik’s pages, then leaned back in his chair.

“You’ve improved your setup,” he said.

Malik tried not to sound too eager. “I’ve been working on that.”

“I can tell. But here”—Dr. Price tapped the middle of a solution—“you are still treating the model like the answer is already obvious. State assumptions before modeling. If you don’t name the world your math is describing, you can make elegant mistakes very quickly.”

Malik nodded and wrote the sentence down nearly word for word.

Dr. Price stood and moved to the board. “Anyone can manipulate symbols. Strong mathematicians and analysts learn to say what must be true before the symbols begin to move. That habit will help you in calculus, statistics, actuarial work—everywhere.”

The conversation lasted less than twenty minutes, but Malik came away from it with the pleasant discomfort of someone discovering that improvement had layers. Getting better did not mean being finished. It meant being capable of seeing finer weaknesses.

Outside, under a shaded walkway lined with students balancing notebooks, snacks, and half-finished conversations, Nia was doing what had slowly become one of her roles in the department without anybody formally assigning it to her.

A sophomore named Alana had caught her after class with a question that had started as one difficult concept and widened into a more familiar frustration.

“I swear I understand it when the professor says it,” Alana admitted, pressing her pen against her notebook. “Then I get back to my room and it all turns into fragments.”

Nia nodded with immediate recognition. “That means the idea hasn’t connected into a picture yet.”

“A picture?”

“Not literally. Well—sometimes literally.” Nia flipped to a blank page. “You can’t always hold disconnected steps in your head. You need a shape. A map. Some reason one part belongs next to another.”

She began sketching arrows, labels, and short phrases rather than equations alone. Her notes always looked a little alive, as if the ideas were in motion rather than pinned down. Students who first saw them sometimes thought they were too messy. Students who learned from them understood quickly that the mess had structure.

Alana watched, then leaned in. “Oh. Wait. So this part isn’t just another step. It’s the reason the next method works.”

“Exactly.”

“That would’ve been nice to know yesterday.”

Nia smiled. “Yesterday was the rough draft. Today is the better version.”

That, too, felt like FAMU.

The campus did not only reward polished brilliance. In the mathematics department especially, there was a culture of visible growth, of office doors opening, of upperclassmen advising first-years, of professors expecting more because they believed students could rise to it. Undergraduate research posters from previous years hung in hallways like quiet promises. Announcements about seminars, tutoring, and student organizations layered the bulletin boards. The message was constant, even when unspoken: you do not have to arrive finished to belong here.

Saturday carried that spirit into a slower, steadier campus rhythm.

Malik spent part of the morning on the lawn near the student center with his internship and career checklist open again, but this time it looked less like a private survival document and more like a blueprint. He updated a section on long-term goals, adding notes about actuarial pathways, programming skills to strengthen, and something new: possible research interests that connected mathematics to real-world modeling.

When Nia found him there, she dropped into the seat across from him and squinted at the tablet.

“Please tell me that isn’t another five-year plan.”

“It’s not,” Malik said.

She narrowed her eyes. “Is it a three-year plan pretending to be flexible?”

He gave her a look. “It’s an updated checklist.”

“That answer was somehow even more suspicious.”

But she was teasing lightly, and he knew it. She had spent her own morning in a student organization planning meeting, talking through freshman engagement ideas, peer support, and how to make more students feel invited into academic life rather than intimidated by it.

“They want more programming that connects first-years to opportunities earlier,” she said. “Not just social events. Real pathways. Research talks, peer panels, study support, faculty introductions.”

Malik looked up. “That sounds smart.”

“It does. And honestly, it feels necessary. Too many students think undergraduate research is something you hear about after everyone else already knows the rules.”

There it was again—that same thread from last week, no longer abstract.

Malik turned the tablet toward her and showed a new section he had typed: **Questions about research**.

Nia’s expression changed instantly. “You actually made a section for it.”

“I said we should stop being hypothetical.”

She leaned back, pleased in a way she did not bother hiding. “Okay. I’m impressed.”

“Document that. It may not happen again.”

They spent the next half hour not committing to a research plan exactly, but giving their curiosity structure. What kind of topics interested them? Which professors might be approachable? How much did first-years need to know before asking serious questions? What counted as a good question versus a vague ambition? Their discussion had the texture of early possibility—still clumsy in places, but no longer imaginary.

Sunday brought the weekly reset.

Homework was due. Dorm hallways filled with laundry baskets, review sheets, and people trying to reclaim a few hours before Monday arrived. In the library, Malik protected a two-hour deep-work block with near-religious seriousness. He chose a quiet table, silenced his phone, set a timer, and worked calculus problems not for speed alone, but for rhythm. Each time he felt himself rushing, he stopped and restated the assumptions. Each time he felt panic begin to push him toward shortcuts, he reset the setup first.

Across campus, Nia led her whiteboard study circle again, and the group had grown.

Not dramatically.
But noticeably.

Keisha was back. David too. Alana arrived with two friends. Somebody brought extra markers. Somebody else brought snacks. What could have become a chaotic group instead held together because Nia had begun shaping it with intention.

“Same rule as last week,” she said, writing in bold strokes across the board: **Explain before solve.**

A few students groaned in playful protest.

“I know,” Nia said. “Terrible burden. Understanding your own logic.”

Laughter loosened the room, and then the work started.

She moved from problem to problem with a style that never felt showy. She asked questions before offering solutions. She turned silence into participation. When someone gave a partial answer, she treated it like a foundation instead of a failure. And by the end of the session, the room carried that same unmistakable energy as before: people were not merely getting through assignments. They were learning how to think together.

When Malik arrived near the end and watched from the doorway for a moment, he saw what others were starting to see. Nia was not just a strong student. She was becoming a mentor.

Afterward, as they walked out into the late afternoon light, he said it plainly.

“You know they trust you.”

Nia glanced at him. “That sounds heavy.”

“It is.”

She was quiet for a moment after that. “I’m trying to deserve it.”

“You do.”

Monday and Tuesday deepened the week’s central lesson: growth is often quiet before it becomes obvious.

A new calculus problem set arrived. Malik practiced timed drills again, but now the timer no longer felt like an enemy. It felt like a diagnostic tool. Nia rewrote another dense page of notes into a concept map that connected theorem, intuition, and application in a way that made the chapter feel less like a wall and more like a path. Dr. Bennett reminded a class that numbers were evidence, not decoration. Dr. Brooks repeated his steady refrain about showing reasoning clearly. In office hours, Dr. Price once again returned to assumptions, to setup, to discipline before cleverness.

The repetition mattered.

These were not random lines from different professors. They were beginning to sound like one conversation told in different voices.

Wednesday’s concept check window tightened the atmosphere slightly, but not in the way earlier quizzes had. The fear was still there, because college mathematics at this level did not stop being serious simply because students became more capable. But there was less chaos inside the fear now. More method. More recovery skill.

Nia helped another peer after class and noticed that she no longer had to force confidence into her voice. Malik walked into office hours already knowing how to ask sharper questions. Small things. Invisible things, if you were not paying attention. But those were often the things that changed a semester.

By Thursday, the week seemed at first to be ending with nothing dramatic at all.

And maybe that was the drama.

Late that afternoon, Malik and Nia sat on a bench outside the mathematics building while the campus eased toward evening. The light had softened. Students crossed the lawn in groups, trading jokes, carrying instrument cases, backpacks, and half-finished drinks. Somewhere in the distance, music lifted and fell. FAMU felt expansive in that hour—not only a place of classes, but of pathways, performances, ambitions, and futures unfolding in parallel.

Nia had the research flyer again, now more worn around the fold lines.

“We need an actual next step,” she said.

Malik nodded. “Agreed.”

“No dramatic speeches. No ‘someday we should.’ A real next step.”

He thought for a moment. “We each talk to one professor next week. Not asking for a full research position. Just asking informed questions.”

Nia tilted her head. “About undergraduate research? About how students get started?”

“About how first-years can prepare to contribute. About what kind of habits matter. About what we don’t know yet.”

A smile spread across her face slowly, the kind that meant a possibility had shifted into a plan.

“That’s good,” she said. “That’s actually good.”

Malik looked at her. “You sound surprised.”

“I am surprised. I expected your first suggestion to involve a spreadsheet.”

“It can still involve a spreadsheet.”

She laughed and shook her head. Then her expression softened.

“You know what I like about this?” she asked.

“What?”

“It doesn’t pretend we’re already ready. It just refuses to let that stop us.”

The words settled between them with more force than either expected.

That was the whole week, really. Not triumph. Not a collapse. Not a cinematic breakthrough. Something steadier, and maybe harder to earn: a week of proof that routines were becoming character, that the mathematics department’s expectations were shaping them rather than scaring them off, that FAMU’s culture of mentorship and student opportunity was not some distant thing happening to other people.

The sun lowered further, catching the edges of campus buildings in gold.

Students passed by wearing FAMU colors, laughing, planning, hurrying, dreaming. Posters for student organizations and academic events fluttered on nearby boards. Somewhere inside the building behind them, a professor was still talking through a problem with a student who had stayed late to ask. The whole place felt alive with effort and possibility.

Nia folded the flyer carefully and handed it back to Malik.

“Next week,” she said, “we ask.”

Malik took the flyer and slipped it into his notebook with unusual care.

“Next week,” he agreed.

It was a small promise.
But it carried real voltage.

Because this week had taught them that not every important turning point arrives in crisis. Some arrive in ordinary days, in repeated habits, in clearer questions, in the courage to move from private ambition to spoken intention.

They stood and started down the walkway together as evening gathered over FAMU.

Behind them, the mathematics building glowed with the last light.
Ahead of them, the next week waited with something sharper than anxiety and more daring than relief.

A question was about to leave the safety of their notebooks and enter the world.

And once they asked it, the year might begin opening in an entirely new way.
