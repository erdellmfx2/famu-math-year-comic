# Week Forty: The Shape of What Comes Next

The question waiting at the end of the previous week did not stay unanswered for long.

After a year of becoming, who were Malik and Nia now ready to be?

The beginning of Week Forty answered in the quietest possible way: not through some grand speech, not through a sudden declaration, and not through a perfect cinematic moment where everything became clear at once. The answer emerged through how they moved.

They moved like students who had been tested and had not broken.

They moved like first-years who no longer looked at every challenge as a verdict on whether they belonged.

They moved like young mathematicians who were beginning to understand that growth was not only measured by scores, but by how they recovered, how they supported others, how they reflected, and how they prepared for what came next.

That was the atmosphere surrounding Thursday, May 1.

Final exams were still shaping campus life, and the pressure had not magically disappeared just because the calendar had turned. FAMU still carried that end-of-semester intensity—students walking with purpose, backpacks heavy with notebooks and review sheets, conversations flipping rapidly between exam questions, summer plans, and quick updates about the baseball series at Grambling State University. The air felt stretched between strain and relief. Nothing was fully finished yet, but the finish line was visible.

Malik felt that in his chest before he said it out loud.

He had spread out a final review packet in one of his usual study spaces, the pages aligned more neatly than they would have been in the fall. That difference mattered. Earlier in the year, his preparation had often contained hidden panic. Now even his urgency had structure.

He was working through a set of problems with the sharp focus of someone who had learned not to confuse motion with progress. He paused after each one long enough to ask whether the logic held. When a step felt shaky, he did not pretend it was fine and race ahead. He circled it, backed up, and cleaned it up.

Across from him, Nia was leading a group review session with the steady confidence that had become one of her clearest signatures. She was no longer trying to save every overwhelmed student by carrying all of their confusion for them. Instead, she was guiding the room with a kind of disciplined generosity.

“Let’s slow the problem down,” she told a cluster of students crowded around a table. “What is the question actually asking for?”

One student sighed. “For me to suffer, apparently.”

That got a tired laugh from the group.

Nia smiled too, then shook her head. “No. It’s asking for evidence that you can think clearly under pressure. That’s different.”

The mood shifted immediately. That was one of her gifts now. She could take tension that felt personal and reframe it into a challenge that felt workable.

Malik glanced up from his review packet and watched her for a moment. He had seen her do this all year in different ways—in study groups, hallway conversations, library check-ins, late-night recaps, and those moments when somebody’s confidence was about to collapse and she found the exact sentence that kept it standing. But there was something fuller about it now. Less frantic. More grounded.

It struck him that this was part of the answer to last week’s question.

Nia was ready to be the kind of leader who didn’t disappear inside everyone else’s needs.

And he—quietly, steadily—was ready to be the kind of person who didn’t need control in order to feel capable.

Later that day, Dr. Bennett crossed paths with Malik and offered the kind of advice that sounded simple and landed deep.

“Remember,” she said, gesturing lightly toward the packet in his hand, “interpret numbers as evidence. Not as identity.”

Malik nodded, but she held his gaze long enough to make sure he really absorbed it.

“A score tells you something,” she continued. “It never tells you everything.”

That line stayed with him long after the conversation ended.

It fit the whole year.

FAMU had been teaching him mathematics, yes, but it had also been teaching him how to read outcomes without letting them define his worth. In the fall, he might have heard that sentence as comfort. Now he heard it as discipline.

By the time Thursday evening settled over campus, the emotional center of the day had shifted from pressure to steadiness. There was still stress in the air, still unfinished work to do, but beneath it was a growing sense that Malik and Nia had already answered the biggest part of the week’s opening question.

They were ready to become students who could carry pressure without letting it decide who they were.

Friday, May 2, felt different the moment it began.

The exam pressure was easing. Space had opened up inside the day.

Baseball was still at Grambling State University, and campus conversations kept drifting toward scores, travel, and Rattler pride, but academically the mood was no longer all-out strain. It was that strange and beautiful threshold that appears after hard effort, when exhaustion and possibility exist in the same breath.

Malik used that space for career reflection and resume polishing.

At another point in the year, that might have felt premature or performative. Now it felt earned.

He sat with a laptop open, an older draft of his resume on one side and notes from the year on the other. He looked at lines that felt too flat for what he had actually lived.

How was he supposed to summarize a year that had taught him how to recover from mistakes, ask better questions, think more carefully, and trust community without sounding generic?

Nia found him partway through this struggle and leaned over his shoulder.

“You look like you’re trying to solve a proof written in corporate language,” she said.

Malik laughed. “That is exactly what this feels like.”

She scanned the page. “You’re listing tasks. But some of your real growth this year wasn’t task-based.”

He looked at her. “So what do I write?”

“Write with evidence,” she said, sounding enough like their professors to make them both smile. “Show the habits. Show the responsibility. Show the leadership in how you approached the work.”

He leaned back in his chair, thinking.

That conversation turned his reflection outward. He began rewriting not just to sound impressive, but to sound accurate. He had become more disciplined, yes, but also more adaptable. More willing to seek feedback. More honest about what needed work. Those were not small changes.

Nearby, Nia had her own quieter ritual underway.

She was journaling about the year—about what had stretched her, where she had overcommitted, what she had learned about leadership, and what still needed work. Her reflection was honest in the best way: not harsh, not flattering, just clear.

She wrote about the difference between being needed and being effective.

She wrote about how the FAMU Mathematics Department had shaped her not only through coursework, but through examples of mentorship that treated students as developing scholars rather than just names on a roster.

She wrote about how undergraduate life at FAMU had given her more than obligations and events. It had given her rooms full of people trying, failing, adjusting, and trying again. That culture mattered. It made excellence feel communal instead of lonely.

When she closed the journal, she did not feel finished.

She felt more certain.

Saturday, May 3, carried even more of that release.

Baseball remained part of the background rhythm, but the center of the day was rest and reset for Malik and a light preview of future material for Nia. It would have been easy to play those things as opposites—one person relaxing, one person already getting ahead—but that was not the truth. Both were expressions of maturity.

Malik was finally learning that rest could be strategic instead of guilty.

He took the day more slowly. He slept a little longer, cleaned up his space, stretched, ate without multitasking, and let his mind settle. At one point he caught himself almost reaching for work simply because stopping still felt unfamiliar.

Dr. Brooks’s voice returned to him then, one of those recurring faculty echoes that had shaped the year: show your reasoning clearly.

Oddly enough, that advice applied here too.

If he claimed he wanted long-term excellence, then his habits had to reflect long-term thinking. Burnout was not proof of dedication. Sustainable effort was.

Meanwhile, Nia skimmed ahead lightly through upcoming topics, not in a frantic way, but with curiosity. She made a few notes, drew a few concept sketches, and then stopped before the work turned heavy.

When they crossed paths later, she lifted a page of loose notes and grinned.

“I looked ahead just enough to remind myself the future is real.”

Malik shook his head. “That sounds like something only you would say casually.”

“And you took a real break without apologizing for it,” she shot back. “Look at us evolving.”

Sunday, May 4, deepened the emotional reset.

Malik spent part of the day with family and part of it doing light review. Nia gave herself a creative reset and time for reading. The pace of the week had slowed, but not into emptiness. Instead, it had opened space for a fuller picture of student life—one where academic seriousness and personal grounding could coexist.

That mattered to the story of FAMU too.

This was not a campus where students only appeared in moments of strain. It was a place where intellectual growth sat beside family pride, athletic energy, friendship, reflection, and the broader shaping of identity. The mathematics department was part of that ecosystem, not apart from it. Its rigor worked best because it existed inside a culture that kept calling students upward while also surrounding them with opportunities to belong.

Monday, May 5, with the SWAC Softball Tournament adding fresh energy to campus conversation, brought Malik and Nia back to reflection with a slightly more future-facing tone.

Malik returned to his resume and career notes. Nia returned to journaling. But now the conversations between them sounded less like recovery and more like planning.

“What do you think changed most this year?” Nia asked.

Malik answered after a pause. “I trust process more now.”

She nodded. “That’s real.”

He looked at her. “What about you?”

Nia thought for a moment. “I think I finally understand that saying yes to everything is not the same as saying yes to purpose.”

He let that sit. “That sounds like a chapter title.”

She laughed. “It sounds like a lesson I should’ve learned sooner.”

“Maybe,” he said. “But you learned it while still helping a lot of people.”

“And you learned that needing perfect control is a trap,” she replied.

“True.”

There was affection in the honesty. They had enough history now to name each other’s growth without making it sentimental.

Tuesday, May 6 and Wednesday, May 7 carried the final shape of the week.

Rest and reset returned for Malik. Light preview and reading returned for Nia. Family time and gentle review remained part of the rhythm. Dr. Bennett’s reminder about evidence and Dr. Price’s repeated insistence on stating assumptions before modeling seemed to hover over everything, not just academically but personally.

What assumptions had they brought into this year?

That belonging had to be proven quickly.

That mistakes were threats.

That pressure always meant danger.

That leadership meant carrying too much.

That discipline meant never resting.

By the end of Week Forty, those assumptions no longer held.

In their place stood something sturdier.

Belonging could be built.

Mistakes could instruct.

Pressure could refine.

Leadership could be shared.

Discipline could include recovery.

That was the real resolution at the start of the week, and it unfolded fully by the end.

After a year of becoming, Malik and Nia were ready to be more than students who had survived their first year.

They were ready to be examples.

Not perfect ones. Not finished ones. But real ones.

As the week closed, the excitement was no longer about whether they would make it through finals. They had. The new tension—the one that lifted the story forward naturally—was larger and more hopeful.

If this was what one year at FAMU could do to them, what might year two make possible?

That question did not hang over them like fear.

It glowed ahead of them like an invitation.
