# Week Twenty-Five: The Week the Energy Rose

If Week Twenty-Four had shown Malik and Nia that discipline could survive an ordinary spring week, then Week Twenty-Five asked a sharper question:

What happened when that discipline had to hold steady inside a week that felt bigger than ordinary?

Because by the time Friday, January 16 arrived, the semester had begun to gather emotional layers.

Classes were no longer new enough to excuse sloppy adjustment. Professors were already signaling that spring would reward clarity, consistency, and initiative. The FAMU campus rhythm was alive in that particular way only a strong semester can produce—students moving with purpose, conversations overlapping between academics and student life, basketball energy echoing through the social atmosphere, and beneath it all the steady pressure of becoming the kind of student who could manage opportunity without losing focus.

That was the real challenge now.

At FAMU, the opportunities were everywhere. So was the responsibility.

On Friday morning, Malik settled into a two-hour deep-work block in the library.

He had chosen the same kind of space he had come to trust: a table with enough room to spread out his notebook, textbook, and scratch paper without feeling crowded, close enough to the window for natural light, far enough from the main traffic flow to protect concentration. Around him, the library carried that quiet academic hum that never really counted as silence—pages turning, keyboards tapping, backpacks brushing against chair legs, somebody whispering an apology after dropping a pen.

Malik liked that sound now. It reminded him that discipline was not loneliness. It was community expressed differently.

He opened his notebook, wrote the date neatly at the top of the page, and paused before beginning the first problem. He could still hear Dr. Bennett’s voice from earlier in the week and from the patterns that had started shaping his thinking more deeply this semester: **interpret numbers as evidence**.

Evidence of what?

Not just whether an answer was right.

Evidence of habits. Evidence of reasoning. Evidence of what he understood and what he only wanted to believe he understood.

So instead of rushing, he built each solution carefully. He wrote down known quantities. He named the relationship between them. He marked where an assumption entered the logic. When he caught himself trying to jump ahead, he stopped and reset. The work was slower than his pride always preferred, but cleaner than his pride alone could ever make it.

Later that afternoon, across campus, Nia stood at a whiteboard with three other students and a marker already in hand.

Her study circle had started becoming something more recognizable now—less an improvised gathering and more a real academic ritual. People came expecting to think, to ask, to contribute, and to be challenged kindly. That mattered. In the FAMU Mathematics Department, the culture of learning was not just about surviving rigorous coursework; it was about building a community where students could rise together.

Nia had begun to embody that culture without trying to perform it.

“Okay,” she said, drawing a box around the central concept they had been circling. “Before we solve anything, tell me what the problem is demanding from us. Not the answer. The demand.”

One student frowned at the board. “It wants the process.”

“More specific.”

“It wants us to identify what matters first?”

Nia smiled. “Better.”

Another student pointed at one of the earlier lines. “So if we don’t organize the information right, we’re technically doing math, but we’re not really doing *this* math.”

“That,” Nia said, tapping the board with the marker, “is exactly the kind of sentence people need to hear out loud.”

The group laughed softly, but the insight stayed in the room.

By evening, campus energy had begun bending toward the weekend game at Arkansas–Pine Bluff. Even away from the court, that excitement changed the atmosphere. FAMU students carried school spirit with a kind of ease that made it feel less like interruption and more like part of academic life itself. Group chats lit up. Predictions flew around. Someone down the hall in the residence space was already talking like the result had been decided.

Malik ran into Nia outside later that night as students crossed campus in clusters.

“You still in professor mode?” he asked.

Nia narrowed her eyes playfully. “That depends. Are you still in spreadsheet monk mode?”

“I’m evolving.”

“Into what?”

He adjusted the strap on his bag. “A slightly more flexible spreadsheet monk.”

She laughed. “That’s real character growth.”

The joke was light, but it rested on something true. They had both changed enough to notice it in one another.

Saturday carried a different kind of intensity.

The basketball trip to Arkansas–Pine Bluff gave the day a background pulse, even for students not traveling. On campus, there was a social brightness to conversations, an extra layer of anticipation that turned ordinary exchanges into mini-events. But Malik and Nia had both learned enough by now to enjoy that atmosphere without letting it steal the structure of the day.

Malik spent part of the morning doing timed calculus drills.

He set the timer, stared at the first problem, and immediately felt the old temptation—to beat the clock emotionally instead of mathematically. That impulse had been one of his quiet enemies for months. Under time pressure, his mind sometimes mistook urgency for intelligence. Dr. Price’s guidance from this week steadied him: **state assumptions before modeling**.

So he slowed down, even with the timer running.

“What am I assuming?” he muttered to himself.

He wrote the assumption first.

Then the structure.

Then the move.

The timer kept counting, but it no longer controlled the emotional temperature of the page.

Meanwhile, Nia rewrote her notes into a sprawling concept map that looked, to anyone else, halfway between careful scholarship and artistic instinct. Arrows connected one topic to another. Related ideas curved back into earlier definitions. One margin held a cluster of questions she still wanted to ask. Another section connected what they were doing now to what might matter later in more advanced courses.

When one of her friends leaned over the table and stared at the page, she shook her head in admiration.

“How do you even think like this?”

Nia shrugged. “I need to see the relationships. If everything stays separate, it doesn’t stick.”

The friend pointed to one branch of the map. “This actually makes it less scary.”

That comment lingered with Nia after her friend walked away.

Less scary.

That was part of what good teaching, good mentoring, and good peer culture could do. The FAMU Mathematics Department was rigorous, yes, but rigor did not have to feel cold. It could also feel invitational when students and faculty helped one another see structure instead of just difficulty.

Sunday, January 18, brought homework due dates and the weekly planning reset.

This was the kind of day that could either restore control or expose how fragile it really was.

Malik went to office hours carrying targeted questions and a sharper confidence than he would have had a semester earlier. He did not arrive hoping to be rescued. He arrived ready to refine.

He laid out his notebook and pointed to one section. “I can get to this step,” he said, “but I’m not fully convinced I’m communicating the logic clearly enough for the solution to be trustworthy.”

Dr. Brooks looked down at the page, then back at him.

“That’s a stronger question than ‘Is this right?’” he said.

Malik felt that land.

Dr. Brooks tapped one of the written lines. “Your reasoning is there. But if you want your mathematics to hold under pressure, don’t hide the bridge between steps. Show it. Especially when the bridge feels obvious to you.”

**Show your reasoning clearly.**

Malik left with only a few markings on the page, but the conversation had done more than correct notation. It had sharpened his standard.

Elsewhere, Nia spent part of the day helping a peer with a difficult concept that had turned into an emotional block as much as an academic one. They sat together with open notebooks, and at first the other student kept apologizing.

“I know I should get this by now.”

Nia shook her head immediately. “Don’t start there.”

The student looked embarrassed. “I just feel behind.”

“You might be behind on one concept,” Nia said gently. “That is not the same thing as being behind as a person.”

The student fell quiet.

Then Nia began again, slower this time, drawing the structure, naming the idea, and letting the confusion breathe instead of trying to crush it. By the time the student finally said, “Wait—okay, I see why that part changes,” the shift felt bigger than the specific problem.

That was one of the quiet strengths of student life at FAMU when it worked well: learning was personal, but it did not have to be solitary.

Monday, January 19, carried the emotional significance of Martin Luther King Jr. Day.

That changed the tone of the week.

Even with the calculus problem set assigned and the men’s basketball team facing Mississippi Valley State, the day held a broader layer of reflection. On campus, conversations moved a little differently. Community history felt closer to the surface. Service, dignity, excellence, and purpose were not abstract themes at an HBCU like FAMU; they lived inside the culture, inside the expectations, inside the examples students saw around them.

For Malik, that day became unexpectedly reflective.

He sat with his internship and career checklist, but instead of treating it like a sterile task, he began asking himself harder questions. What kind of work did he want to do? What kind of professional did he want to become? Was he pursuing achievement only as proof, or as preparation to contribute meaningfully?

Numbers, credentials, and plans all mattered. But what were they for?

Across campus, Nia joined a student organization planning meeting that felt more purposeful than routine. People were discussing events, outreach, and ways to support students beyond the classroom. She listened carefully, then contributed with unusual calm.

“We should make sure the event doesn’t just look good,” she said. “It should actually help students feel like they belong once they get there.”

The table grew quiet for half a second.

Then someone nodded. “That’s the right question.”

Nia felt a flicker of surprise at her own certainty.

Not because the idea was dramatic. Because it came from somewhere earned.

She was no longer just reacting to the year. She was beginning to help shape it.

Tuesday returned to ordinary structure, but the week was no longer emotionally ordinary.

Malik went back to the library for another deep-work block, and Nia returned to leading a whiteboard study circle. Dr. Okafor’s guidance—**visualize the concept first**—seemed to hover over both of them all day. Malik started sketching relationships before calculations. Nia asked students what picture they were carrying before they chose a method.

The work felt more connected now.

More mature.

As if spring were slowly teaching them that excellence in mathematics was not only about endurance, but about seeing clearly enough to act with intention.

Wednesday brought the quiz or concept-check window.

This was the day the week had been quietly building toward.

By morning, students all around campus carried that familiar mix of concentration and compressed nerves. Hallway conversations got shorter. Backpacks seemed heavier. The energy in the Mathematics Department felt sharper, more focused, almost metallic.

Malik practiced timed drills one last time, but this time the anxiety did not flare the way it once might have. He could still feel pressure, but now pressure had structure. He knew what to do with it.

Across the room, Nia revised one more concept map, then closed her notebook and rested both hands on top of it.

“You good?” Malik asked.

She let out a slow breath. “I think so.”

He nodded. “That sounded like a person trying not to fight the week.”

She gave him a small smile. “That’s because fighting the week never helps.”

The concept check itself did not become some miraculous, cinematic victory. It became something better.

It became evidence.

Evidence that their systems were working.

Evidence that office hours, study circles, clean setup, visual thinking, timed practice, and clearer reasoning were not just nice ideas—they were becoming reliable supports under real academic conditions.

By Thursday, January 22, the week should have felt finished.

Instead, it felt like it had opened a door.

Malik returned to office hours with another set of targeted questions, and Nia helped a peer again, almost without thinking about it. Around them, the FAMU campus kept moving with its familiar blend of academic seriousness, cultural pride, mentorship, and student possibility. The Mathematics Department did not feel like a place they were merely passing through anymore. It was becoming part of who they were.

Late that afternoon, they walked outside beneath a sky beginning to soften toward evening.

There was no dramatic crowd around them, no giant event announcing itself in the moment. Just students crossing paths, conversations drifting, and the quiet after a demanding week that had still somehow left both of them standing a little taller.

Nia looked ahead and said, “Do you ever feel like the semester is starting to notice who we are?”

Malik frowned, thinking. “That sounds strange.”

“It is strange.”

“But I kind of know what you mean.”

She glanced at him. “Like the work is getting harder, but it’s also asking more specific things from us now.”

He nodded slowly. “Yeah. Not just whether we can keep up. What kind of people we’re going to be while we do.”

That was the true ending of Week Twenty-Five.

Not a finished problem set. Not a game result. Not even the concept check.

The real ending was the sensation—exciting and a little dangerous—that spring was beginning to raise its expectations because Malik and Nia had raised themselves.

Their routines were no longer experiments.

Their growth was no longer hypothetical.

Their influence on other students was becoming visible.

And that meant the next challenge would almost certainly ask more from them than simple survival.

By the close of the week, the tension was no longer about whether they could find a rhythm at FAMU.

They had.

Now the unresolved question—the one that would make the opening of the following week feel meaningful—was whether they were ready for what happened when rhythm turned into momentum, and momentum started drawing them toward larger opportunities, sharper expectations, and moments that would test not just how well they studied, but how fully they were prepared to lead.

That was the thrill humming underneath everything now.

And for the first time, that thrill felt close enough to touch.
